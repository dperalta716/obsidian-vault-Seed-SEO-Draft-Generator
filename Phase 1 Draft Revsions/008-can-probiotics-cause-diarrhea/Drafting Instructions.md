### **What About Food Sources?**

**You might have heard that fermented foods like yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, and kombucha are great sources of probiotics. And while these foods can certainly be nutritious additions to your diet, there's an important scientific distinction worth understanding.**

**According to the International Scientific Association for Probiotics and Prebiotics (ISAPP), a true probiotic must be a "live microorganism that, when administered in adequate amounts, confers a health benefit on the host."** [NEW CITATION NEEDED] **In other words, it needs three things: live organisms, a defined dose, and demonstrated health benefits from research.**

**Most fermented foods don't quite meet this bar. What they contain are called "Live Dietary Microbes" (LDM)â€”beneficial microorganisms that lack the specific characterization, defined dosing, and clinical evidence required to be classified as probiotics.** **That's not to say fermented foods aren't valuable! They're delicious, often nutrient-rich, and may support your gut microbiome in their own way. But they're playing a different role than a clinically studied probiotic supplement.**

**So, if you're specifically looking to address diarrhea concerns or reap the benefits demonstrated in probiotic research, you'll want to stick with supplements that clearly identify their strains and dosages. (And if you want to dive deeper into this topic, check out Seed's article on** [**fermented foods vs. probiotics**](https://seed.com/cultured/fermented-foods-vs-probiotics/)**.)**

**The bottom line: Enjoy your fermented foods, but don't expect them to deliver the same targeted benefits as a well-formulated probiotic supplement.**
