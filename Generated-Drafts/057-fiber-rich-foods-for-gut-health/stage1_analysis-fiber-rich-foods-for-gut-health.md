# Stage 1: Competitive Analysis - "fiber rich foods for gut health"

## 1. User Search Intent

### Primary Intent
**Informational / List-seeking**: Users want a practical, scannable list of specific high-fiber foods they can eat to improve their gut health. They are looking for actionable dietary guidance, not abstract nutrition science.

### Secondary Intents
- **Understanding the mechanism**: Why does fiber help the gut? What does it actually do for gut bacteria?
- **Practical how-to**: How to incorporate more fiber into daily meals without digestive discomfort (bloating, gas).
- **Quantitative guidance**: How much fiber do I need daily? What are the gram counts per serving?
- **Type differentiation**: What is the difference between soluble and insoluble fiber, and which matters more for gut health?

### Core Questions Users Are Trying to Answer
1. What specific foods should I eat to get more fiber for my gut?
2. How much fiber do I need each day?
3. Why is fiber good for gut health specifically (not just digestion generally)?
4. How do I add fiber without causing bloating or gas?
5. What is the difference between soluble and insoluble fiber?

## 2. Competitive Baseline Requirements

### Content Expectations (What Every Top Article Includes)
- **Categorized food lists**: Fruits, vegetables, legumes, whole grains, nuts/seeds -- all 5 ranking articles organize by food category
- **Gram counts per serving**: Specific fiber content with serving sizes (table or inline)
- **Soluble vs. insoluble fiber explanation**: All articles distinguish fiber types
- **Daily recommended intake**: 25-38g range depending on gender/age (referenced by 4/5 articles)
- **Practical tips for increasing fiber**: Gradual introduction, hydration, food swaps
- **Benefits beyond digestion**: Heart health, blood sugar control, weight management

### Standard Advice Identified (Opportunities to Differentiate)
1. **"Eat 25-38g of fiber per day"** -- Generic daily targets without explaining that fiber diversity matters as much as quantity
2. **"Add fiber slowly to avoid bloating"** -- Standard caution without explaining WHY your gut reacts (microbiome adaptation) or how prebiotics specifically feed beneficial bacteria
3. **"Choose whole grains over refined grains"** -- Simple swap advice without discussing how specific fibers (beta-glucan, resistant starch, inulin) have distinct effects on different bacterial populations

### Content Gaps Across Competitors
- **Microbiome specificity**: No article explains which bacterial species benefit from which fiber types
- **Short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production**: Only Hopkins briefly mentions SCFAs; none explain the mechanism deeply
- **Prebiotic vs. general fiber distinction**: Only UHC separates prebiotics from general fiber; others conflate them
- **Fiber diversity concept**: No article discusses the importance of eating diverse fiber types (not just high volume)
- **Gut barrier integrity**: No article connects fiber to intestinal barrier function and permeability
- **Supplement context**: None discuss when whole-food fiber is insufficient and what role supplemental fiber plays

## 4. People Also Ask Questions

### Selected for FAQ Section (4 Questions)

**1. "What is the best fiber for the gut?"**
- **Rationale**: Directly aligned with keyword intent. Allows Seed to differentiate between fiber types and introduce the concept of prebiotic specificity -- not all fiber is equal for gut health. Opens the door to discuss how specific fibers feed specific beneficial bacteria.

**2. "How to get 100% fiber daily?"**
- **Rationale**: Practical and actionable. Users want meal-planning guidance. This lets Seed provide concrete food combinations while subtly introducing the concept of fiber diversity over sheer volume.

**3. "What are the signs of low fiber?"**
- **Rationale**: Symptom-awareness question that connects to gut health directly. Allows the article to discuss constipation, bloating, poor microbiome diversity, and even immune function -- reinforcing the importance of adequate fiber for gut health specifically.

**4. "What drink is high in fiber?"**
- **Rationale**: Unexpected angle that adds practical value. Allows discussion of smoothies, coffee as a fiber source, and vegetable juices -- broadening the article beyond standard food lists while remaining accessible.

### Not Selected (with reasons)

**5. "Should I take fiber with zepbound?"**
- **Reason**: Too narrow and medication-specific. Not relevant to Seed's audience or product positioning. Would require medical disclaimers that detract from the article's flow.

**6. "What foods are high in fiber for the gut?"**
- **Reason**: Redundant with the article's core topic. The entire article answers this question, so including it as a standalone FAQ adds no value.

**7. "What are the 7 signs of an unhealthy gut?"**
- **Reason**: Too broad. Shifts focus away from fiber specifically toward general gut health diagnostics. Better suited for a separate article.

**8. "How to get 100% fiber daily?" (duplicate)**
- **Reason**: Duplicate of question 2 above.

## 6. Synthesis for Drafting

### Seed's Differentiation Opportunities

1. **Fiber diversity over fiber quantity**: Every competitor focuses on hitting a daily gram target. Seed can lead with the idea that eating diverse types of fiber (beta-glucan, inulin, pectin, resistant starch, cellulose) matters more than raw grams because different fibers feed different bacterial populations.

2. **Prebiotic specificity**: Most competitors treat all fiber as equal for gut health. Seed can explain that certain fibers are specifically prebiotic (feeding beneficial bacteria and producing SCFAs) while others primarily add bulk. This connects directly to Seed's microbiome expertise.

3. **SCFA production as the real mechanism**: Only Hopkins briefly mentions SCFAs. Seed can explain the full mechanism: fiber reaches the colon undigested, specific bacteria ferment it into butyrate, propionate, and acetate, which fuel colonocytes, reduce inflammation, and strengthen the gut barrier.

4. **Gut barrier integrity**: No competitor discusses intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") and how fiber-derived SCFAs help maintain tight junctions between intestinal cells. This is a unique scientific angle.

5. **Synergy with probiotics**: No competitor (except UHC superficially) discusses how fiber and probiotics work together. Seed can position fiber as the essential fuel that makes probiotic supplementation more effective.

6. **The "more is not always better" angle**: Competitors default to "eat more fiber." Seed can introduce nuance -- too much fiber too fast can disrupt the microbiome, and the source matters more than the quantity. This contrasts with standard "just eat 38g" advice.

### Recommended Article Angle
Lead with the concept that **not all fiber is created equal for gut health** -- the type, diversity, and source of fiber determines how effectively it feeds your gut microbiome and produces protective compounds like SCFAs. This positions Seed as the smarter, more nuanced voice in a sea of generic food lists.

### Structural Recommendations
- Open with the insight that most people focus on fiber quantity when they should focus on fiber quality and diversity
- Include a curated food list (not exhaustive -- 10-15 foods max) organized by the type of fiber/prebiotic benefit they provide
- Explain the SCFA mechanism in accessible language
- Address the "start slow" advice with a microbiome-informed explanation of WHY
- Close with the synergy between dietary fiber and probiotic support
