# Stage 1 Pre-Draft Analysis: Best Milk for Gut Health

## 1. User Search Intent

The primary intent is **practical, comparative, and enumerative**: "Which type of milk should I drink (or avoid) if I care about my gut — across dairy and non-dairy options?" Searchers want a scannable, ranked run-through of milk types with reasons.

Two sub-intents sit under the keyword:
- **Tolerability seekers** — people with lactose intolerance, IBS, or general digestive sensitivity who want a milk that won't trigger gas, bloating, or cramping. This dominates the competitive SERP.
- **Microbiome optimizers** — a smaller, growing group asking which milk actively *supports* beneficial gut bacteria, not just which one avoids harm.

**Core questions users are really asking:**
1. Which milk is gentlest on a sensitive gut?
2. Does any milk actually *help* my gut bacteria, or just avoid upsetting them?
3. Is dairy bad for my gut, or is that overblown? (A1/A2, lactose)
4. Are plant milks (oat, almond, soy, coconut, etc.) better or worse for the gut?
5. What about the additives and emulsifiers in plant milks?

## 2. Article Type & Coverage Inventory

### Article Type: ENUMERATED LISTICLE

The keyword "best milk for gut health" demands an enumeration of milk *types*. Every competitor that engages the topic (nourish, zoe, verywell) structures it as a numbered list of milks, each with its own heading. **Structure requirement (binding for Phase 2):** each milk gets its own H3 whose heading is just the milk's name ("Kefir", "A2 Milk", "Oat Milk"). H3s are grouped into H2s by a meaningful evidence/category axis — fermented dairy (strongest evidence) → regular dairy & the lactose/A1-A2 question → plant-based milks (the nuance competitors skip) → the additives/emulsifier hidden variable. The only descriptive (non-name) H3 is the fringe catch-all.

## 5. Article-by-Article Analysis

### nourish.com — "The Best Milk for Gut Health: 5 Dietitian-Approved Options"
~1,450 words, Apr 2024, dietitian-authored. Avoidance-first framing (gut health = "tolerable for sensitive guts"). Items: lactose-free, goat, almond, coconut, hemp. Strong citation density (NIDDK, PubMed, USDA, PMC5756203). **Gap:** zero microbiome/fermented-dairy mechanism. No kefir, no A2, no soy, no oat.

### zoe.com — "How healthy are different types of milk?"
~1,750 words. Profiles 9 milk types with USDA nutrition. Signature hooks: emulsifier-inflammation (Microbiome 2021) and "dairy may raise helpful bacteria but not diversity" (Gut Microbes 2020). Items: cow, lactose-free, goat, soy, oat, almond, hemp, coconut, rice. **Gap:** gut section ~150w; no fermented/probiotic mechanism depth; no kefir H3, no A2.

### mcpress.mayoclinic.org — "Dairy milk, soy milk, almond milk — Which is healthiest?"
~560 words, Q&A, no primary citations. Pure macro comparison (fat, protein, calcium, sugar). **Does not address gut health.** Owns "ultrafiltered milk" (higher protein/calcium, reduced lactose).

### uclahealth.org — "Plant-based milks make great substitute for cow's milk"
~560 words, "Ask the Doctors" Q&A, zero citations. Plant-forward bias (partly environmental). Names yogurt + kefir as gut-beneficial (live cultures break down lactose, support microbiome diversity) but undeveloped. Lists macadamia, cashew, oats, coconut, soy, almond.

### verywellhealth.com — "Top 9 Milks for a Happy Gut"
~1,200 words, IBS/FODMAP-focused, MD-reviewed. Items: lactose-free, soy, almond, hemp, coconut, rice, oat, cashew, kefir. **Only kefir** gets an "improves" (not just "avoids") claim (Peluzio 2021, Front Nutr). Anti-dairy editorializing (fracture, acne, cancer). Monash FODMAP recurring authority.

## 6. Synthesis for Drafting

**The winning angle (aligned with SciCare POV):** Every competitor answers "which milk won't upset my gut?" Seed answers a richer question: **"which milk actually works *with* your microbiome — and why the honest answer depends on you."** Lead with fermented dairy (strongest evidence: kefir, yogurt drinks). Then the real A1/A2 + lactose nuance on regular cow's milk and the under-covered A2 angle. Then the counterintuitive plant-milk truths competitors miss (oat milk loses its beta-glucan; soy's equol story; almond's distinct-but-thin evidence) with the mandatory human-evidence caveat. Then the hidden variable: additives and emulsifiers.
