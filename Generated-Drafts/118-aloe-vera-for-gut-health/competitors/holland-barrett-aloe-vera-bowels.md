# Does aloe vera help with constipation?

03/02/2026 — 6 min read

Written by Eloise Wright
Reviewed by Madalena Vieira Costa

Struggling with constipation or digestive discomfort? Here's what you need to know about aloe vera and gut health, the evidence around IBS and how to take it safely

## Summary

### 1. What is aloe vera?

Aloe vera is a water-rich plant used to soothe digestion. It contains enzymes and plant compounds that may help support gut comfort.

### 2. Can aloe vera help with constipation and IBS?

Purified or decolourised aloe vera helps reduce side effects. Products should be taken in small amounts and not used long term.

### 3. How do you take aloe vera safely?

Purified or decolourised aloe vera helps reduce the risk of side effects. Products should be taken in small amounts and not used long term.

You may have heard the digestive system described as a window into your overall health – and for good reason. When digestion is working well, your body can properly absorb nutrients, support immunity and keep your energy levels steady. When it's not, discomfort like bloating, constipation or irregular bowel movements can quickly take centre stage.1-3

That's where aloe vera comes in. Best known for soothing sunburn, this spiky plant also has a long history of internal use for digestive comfort – particularly when it comes to constipation, gut irritation and ongoing digestive complaints. H&B nutritionist Madalena Vieira Costa explains how aloe vera can support gut health, which forms are best for everyday use and how different parts of the leaf affect digestion.

## What is aloe vera?

Aloe vera is a succulent plant with thick, fleshy leaves – and what's inside those leaves is where the digestive benefits really come into play. "As a plant, aloe vera is quite unique," Madalena explains. "Each leaf is made up of three distinct layers, and each one has a different role and impact on the digestive system."

At the centre of the leaf is the clear inner gel, which makes up around 99% water. The remaining small amount contains active plant compounds such as polysaccharides, vitamins, minerals, amino acids, lipids and plant sterols.11,12 "This inner gel is the part most commonly used in gut-friendly supplements and drinks," Madalena says. "It's generally well tolerated and associated with a soothing effect on the digestive tract."

Just beneath the leaf skin sits the middle layer, also known as aloe latex. This bitter yellow sap contains anthraquinones, compounds believed to have a strong stimulating effect on the bowel.9,13 "This is the layer that gives aloe vera its reputation as a 'natural laxative'," Madalena explains. "As it's so potent, it's not suitable for everyday or long-term use and is usually removed during processing." The outer layer (rind) mainly acts as a protective barrier for the inner parts of the plant. On its own, it doesn't offer specific digestive benefits, but when whole-leaf aloe products are used, small amounts of latex can sometimes remain in the final product.11

## What are the health benefits of aloe vera for gut health and digestion?

When it comes to general gut health, aloe vera is often used as a gentle, supportive aid, particularly in the form of purified inner leaf gel. "The digestive benefits of aloe vera's inner gel are mainly linked to hydration and its natural plant compounds," Madalena says.

Firstly, aloe vera gel's water content helps support hydration within the digestive tract.5 Adequate hydration is important for maintaining the integrity of the gut lining and allowing food and waste to move through the intestines more comfortably.11 Secondly, aloe vera gel contains polysaccharides, which are thought to help calm inflammation in the gut, support a healthier balance of gut bacteria and help protect the intestinal lining.11,14 "Together, these effects may help reduce gut sensitivity and digestive discomfort, which is why inner leaf aloe vera is often chosen for ongoing gut support rather than quick-fix relief," Madalena adds.

## Aloe vera for constipation

Let's talk about constipation – because this is where aloe vera tends to get most of the attention and where the differences between leaf layers really matter. "The inner aloe vera gel can help support bowel movements in a much gentler way," Madalena explains. "As it's mostly water and contains soothing plant compounds, it may help soften stools and support natural gut movement rather than forcing the bowel to act." This makes purified inner gel a more suitable option for people looking for everyday digestive support.11

However, aloe vera's stronger laxative reputation comes from the aloe latex. The natural plant compounds in this layer are suggested to stimulate the muscles of the bowel and increase water secretion into the stool, which can trigger a bowel movement more quickly.9,13

"In some situations, that stimulating effect may be appropriate, such as short-term, occasional constipation where basics like fibre, fluids and movement haven't helped," says Madalena. However, because of its potency, aloe latex isn't designed for regular use. Frequent consumption may cause cramping, diarrhoea or irritation of the gut lining, which is why it's not recommended for long-term constipation management.9,13 "For ongoing gut health, products made from purified inner leaf gel are generally the more appropriate option."

## Aloe vera for IBS

IBS is associated with a more sensitive digestive system, meaning the gut may react more strongly to certain foods or supplements. "Aloe vera can be a bit hit-or-miss for people with IBS," Madalena explains. "Some people find it soothing, while others don't tolerate it at all." Research into aloe vera and IBS has produced mixed results. Some studies suggest aloe vera may improve symptoms such as bloating, gas, constipation and diarrhoea, while others have found no significant difference compared to a placebo.6,9

Madalena notes that aloe vera may be more suitable for IBS-C (IBS with constipation) than IBS-D (with diarrhoea), as it can help keep digestion moving. "But starting with small amounts and seeking professional advice is always important," she adds.

## What are other benefits of aloe vera?

Beyond digestion, aloe vera has been studied for a range of additional benefits. Research suggests it may:

- support skin hydration and repair when used topically
- provide antioxidants that help protect cells from oxidative stress7
- contribute to oral health when used in mouth rinses
- support immune health due to its nutrient and natural sugar content8

"These benefits are promising," Madalena says. "But aloe vera should always be used appropriately and in line with product guidance."

## How to take aloe vera

If you choose aloe vera juice or supplements, Madalena stresses the importance of choosing purified or decolourised inner leaf products. "This processing step removes phenolic compounds and anthraquinones from the latex layer, which helps reduce the risk of cramping and diarrhoea," she says.

Always follow label instructions and avoid exceeding recommended doses unless advised by a healthcare professional. If you're more sensitive, starting with a lower dose can help assess tolerance. "Aloe vera juice can be taken on its own or mixed with juice, smoothies or milk," Madalena adds. "That's really just down to taste preference."

## The final say

Aloe vera has earned its reputation as much more than just a skincare staple. When used correctly, purified inner leaf aloe vera gel may offer gentle support for digestive comfort, hydration and occasional constipation. "As with any supplement, it's about choosing the right form and using it in the right way," Madalena says. If digestive symptoms are persistent, severe or affecting daily life, speaking to a GP or healthcare professional is always the best next step.
