# Is Buttermilk Good for Gut Health? What the Science Actually Supports—and What It Doesn't

### Overview

- Buttermilk is a fermented dairy drink that often shows up on "natural probiotic" lists, but the direct evidence that it benefits your gut is thinner than most articles let on.
- The most interesting science centers on its milk fat globule membrane (MFGM)—a coating of bioactive compounds studied mostly in animals and lab models, not in people.
- Most buttermilk gut research comes from mice and test tubes. The one human trial that measured gut bacteria after buttermilk intake found no change.
- Buttermilk may still earn a spot in your fridge for its protein, calcium, and easier-on-lactose profile—just keep your gut expectations grounded.
- Fermented foods and clinically studied probiotics work in different ways, and understanding that difference helps you choose what fits your routine.

You've probably seen buttermilk filed under "natural probiotic," right beside kefir and yogurt. The tangy reputation makes the question feel almost answered before you ask it.

So, is buttermilk good for gut health? The honest answer: it's biologically plausible, but the direct human proof is limited. Buttermilk is a fermented drink with live cultures and some genuinely interesting compounds, yet most of the gut research uses animals or lab models rather than people.

Here's where it gets more nuanced than the listicles suggest. "Buttermilk" actually means two different things, and the version studied in labs isn't always the tangy drink in your glass. Let's look at what the research really shows—and where it goes quiet.

## What "Buttermilk" Actually Is, and Why It Matters for Your Gut

Before we talk gut effects, we need to clear up a mix-up that trips up nearly every article on this topic. The word "buttermilk" covers two quite different things.

### Traditional Cultured Buttermilk vs. the By-Product

Traditional buttermilk—the tangy drink, and the Indian *chaas*—is milk fermented by lactic acid bacteria. Those bacteria are what give it the sour taste and the live cultures people associate with gut health.

The other "buttermilk" is an industrial by-product. It's the liquid left over when cream is churned into butter, and it's often not fermented at all. What makes it interesting is that it's rich in the milk fat globule membrane, or MFGM ([Barukčić 2019](https://doi.org/10.17113/ftb.57.04.19.6460)).

This distinction matters because much of the lab research on "buttermilk and the gut" actually uses the MFGM-rich by-product, not the fermented drink. So when you read that buttermilk does something for the gut, it's worth asking which buttermilk the study meant.

### Is Buttermilk Actually a Probiotic?

Here's a distinction worth holding onto. A *probiotic* has a specific scientific definition: live microorganisms that, in adequate amounts, deliver a health benefit ([Hill 2014](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrgastro.2014.66)).

Fermented foods like buttermilk may contain live microbes, but most don't meet that full definition, because the specific strains and doses haven't been characterized and tied to a measured benefit. Fermented foods are defined simply as foods made through desired microbial growth ([Marco 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41575-020-00390-5)).

That doesn't make buttermilk any less nourishing or enjoyable. It just means "fermented" and "probiotic" aren't synonyms, and your expectations should follow the evidence rather than the label. Buttermilk belongs to the broad family of fermented milk drinks, a category linked at a general level to favorable shifts in gut bacteria ([Stachelska 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17010009)).

## Is Buttermilk Good for Gut Health? What the Microbiome Research Shows

This is the heart of the question, so let's give it the room it deserves. The research here is genuinely promising in places and genuinely thin in others, and an honest read holds both.

### The Animal and Lab Evidence

Most of the encouraging findings come from mice. In one study, whey- and buttermilk-based formulas helped restore beneficial bacterial families and short-chain fatty acid production in mice whose gut bacteria had been disrupted by antibiotics ([Bellés 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300248)).

A related study in mice found these same formulas helped normalize gut motility and immune-receptor balance in the intestine after antibiotic treatment ([Bellés 2024](https://doi.org/10.1089/jmf.2024.0155)). Lab digestion models add another piece: buttermilk's MFGM lipids appear to survive digestion and reach intestinal cells ([Martínez-Sánchez 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16162798)).

It's worth being clear-eyed here. These are findings from mice and from cells in a dish, not from people drinking buttermilk. Animal and lab results point to plausible mechanisms, but they don't yet tell us what happens in a human gut, so this remains an early area of research.

### MFGM and Milk Sphingomyelin

Here's the part most buttermilk articles skip entirely, and it's the most scientifically interesting. The MFGM carries compounds called phospholipids and sphingomyelin—bioactive molecules that may interact with the gut lining and the microbes already living there ([Norris 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2019.108224)).

Unlike a probiotic, which adds new live bacteria, these are non-living milk compounds. They appear to act through the existing system rather than introducing newcomers. In cell and rodent studies, milk sphingomyelin has been linked to protection against disrupted lipid metabolism, gut dysbiosis, and inflammation ([Norris 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2019.108224)).

The catch is the same one we keep returning to: this evidence comes largely from isolated compounds in controlled settings, not from buttermilk itself in human diets. The mechanism is real and studied. The leap to "drink buttermilk for these effects" is not yet supported in people.

### What the One Human Trial Found

If you want the most direct human data, there isn't much, and what exists is humbling. In a controlled study, healthy adults drank buttermilk during physical work in a hot chamber while researchers measured several outcomes, including gut bacteria.

The drink helped with hydration and heat strain. But it produced no measurable change in gut microbiota ([Lundgren-Kownacki 2017](https://doi.org/10.2486/indhealth.2017-0030)).

One study can't close a question on its own, and this trial wasn't designed primarily around gut health. Still, it's a useful reality check: the one time researchers looked directly at buttermilk and human gut bacteria, the needle didn't move. More human research is needed before anyone can promise a gut benefit.

## Digestion, Lactose, and Your Bowels

Set aside the microbiome question for a moment, because buttermilk has a couple of more grounded perks for everyday digestion. These are where the evidence feels steadier.

### Easier on Lactose Than Milk

If regular milk leaves you uncomfortable, buttermilk may sit better. The fermentation process uses bacteria that break down some of the lactose, which can make it gentler than standard milk for people sensitive to it.

This tracks with what's known about fermented milk drinks as a category, which have been linked to eased lactose intolerance ([Stachelska 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17010009)). It's still dairy, though, so a true dairy allergy is a different story.

### Buttermilk and Regularity

The connection between buttermilk and smoother regularity is more suggestive than settled. In the mouse studies, buttermilk-based formulas helped normalize gut motility after a disruption ([Bellés 2024](https://doi.org/10.1089/jmf.2024.0155)).

Whether that translates to people drinking buttermilk for regularity hasn't been directly shown, so this is best filed under "plausible, not proven." As a fluid that's also a source of protein and calcium, buttermilk can be a pleasant part of a varied diet regardless.

## Beyond the Gut: Cholesterol, Blood Pressure, and the Rest

Buttermilk's reputation stretches well past the gut, and a couple of these claims have some footing. The MFGM compounds we discussed have been linked to reduced cholesterol absorption in human evidence, again tied to sphingomyelin rather than buttermilk as a whole ([Norris 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2019.108224)).

There's also some interest in buttermilk and blood pressure, though that rests on limited, smaller studies and shouldn't be oversold. Beyond those, buttermilk's other reputed perks—bones and calcium, energy from riboflavin, skin, immune support, weight, and easing acidity—sit mostly outside gut health and rest on thinner or more general evidence. They're fine reasons to enjoy it, just not gut-health claims.

One quick comparison, since it comes up a lot: people often ask whether buttermilk or kefir is better for the gut. Kefir typically carries a broader, more diverse set of live cultures, but both are fermented milk drinks in the same general family, and neither is a substitute for evidence-backed expectations.

## A Different Tool: How Clinically Studied Probiotics Compare

It helps to step back and see fermented foods and probiotics as different tools rather than competitors. A fermented food like buttermilk brings live cultures and bioactive compounds as part of a whole food. A probiotic, by contrast, is built around specific, characterized strains studied at a defined dose for a measured benefit ([Hill 2014](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrgastro.2014.66)). Neither is "better"—they simply work through different routes.

That difference raises a fair question about any live microbes you swallow: do they survive the trip through your stomach? It's a real hurdle, since digestion is harsh on bacteria.

## The Key Insight

So, is buttermilk good for gut health? It's a nourishing fermented drink with live cultures and genuinely interesting bioactive compounds, and that makes it biologically plausible as a gut-friendly choice. But plausible isn't the same as proven.

The honest picture is this: most of the encouraging evidence comes from mice and lab models, the MFGM science is studied in isolated compounds rather than buttermilk itself, and the one human trial that measured gut bacteria found no change. That's not a reason to skip buttermilk—it's a reason to enjoy it for what it reliably offers, like protein, calcium, and a lactose-gentler option, while keeping your gut expectations grounded.

The best approach to your gut rarely hinges on a single food. Variety, fiber, fermented options you enjoy, and evidence-backed choices all work together. Drink the buttermilk because you like it—and let the science, not the hype, set your hopes.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### Does buttermilk increase gut bacteria?

The honest answer is that we don't have strong human evidence that it does. In mice, buttermilk-based formulas helped restore beneficial bacteria after antibiotic disruption ([Bellés 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300248)), which is encouraging at the mechanism level. But animal findings don't automatically apply to people.

The one human trial that actually measured gut bacteria after buttermilk intake found no change ([Lundgren-Kownacki 2017](https://doi.org/10.2486/indhealth.2017-0030)). So while buttermilk contains live cultures and is biologically plausible, the direct evidence that it meaningfully increases gut bacteria in humans isn't there yet. It's an open question that needs more research.

### Is buttermilk good for your bowels?

For everyday digestion, buttermilk can be a gentle choice—especially if regular milk bothers you. The fermentation process breaks down some of the lactose, which may make it easier to tolerate, in line with what's known about fermented milk drinks generally ([Stachelska 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17010009)).

When it comes to regularity, the evidence is suggestive rather than settled. Buttermilk-based formulas helped normalize gut motility in mice ([Bellés 2024](https://doi.org/10.1089/jmf.2024.0155)), but that hasn't been directly shown in people drinking buttermilk. As a hydrating, protein-containing fluid, it can be a comfortable part of a varied diet.

### When is the best time to drink buttermilk for gut health?

There's no research-backed "best window" for drinking buttermilk, so timing comes down to personal comfort. Many people enjoy it with or after meals, where it can feel pleasant and aid hydration.

It's worth setting expectations honestly here. Since the direct human evidence for a buttermilk gut benefit is limited, no particular time of day will unlock an effect the research hasn't established. If you like buttermilk, drink it when it suits you and your digestion. Pair it with a varied, fiber-rich diet, which does more for your gut than the timing of any single drink.

### What happens if you drink buttermilk every day?

For most people, a daily serving of buttermilk is a fine addition to a balanced diet, offering protein, calcium, and a hydrating, lactose-gentler dairy option. It's still dairy, though, so anyone with a dairy allergy should avoid it, and those watching sodium should note that some buttermilk can be relatively high in it.

As for your gut, daily buttermilk is unlikely to transform your microbiome based on current evidence, but it's a reasonable, nourishing habit if you enjoy it. If you have specific digestive concerns or sensitivities, it's a good idea to check in with your healthcare provider about what fits your needs.
