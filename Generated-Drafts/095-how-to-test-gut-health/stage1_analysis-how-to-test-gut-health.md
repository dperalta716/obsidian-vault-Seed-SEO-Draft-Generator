# Stage 1 Pre-Draft Analysis: How to Test Gut Health

## 1. User Search Intent

**Primary Intent:** Informational — users want to understand what methods exist for evaluating their gut health, whether at-home microbiome tests are worth it, and what clinical tests doctors use.

**Core Questions Users Are Trying to Answer:**
1. Can I test my gut health at home, and are those tests reliable?
2. What do doctors use to test gut health?
3. What does a gut microbiome test actually tell me?
4. What are signs of an unhealthy gut that might prompt testing?
5. Is there a "normal" gut microbiome I should be aiming for?

**Search Context:** The keyword sits at the intersection of curiosity-driven health consumers and people experiencing digestive symptoms. Many searchers have likely seen DTC gut test ads (Viome, ZOE, etc.) and want independent guidance on whether testing is worthwhile.

## 2. Competitive Baseline Requirements

**Must-Cover Topics (present in 3+ articles):**
- What a gut microbiome test is and how it works (stool sample → sequencing) — 5/5 articles
- Limitations of at-home/DTC gut microbiome tests — 4/5 articles
- No established definition of a "healthy" microbiome — 4/5 articles
- What dysbiosis is and signs of an unhealthy gut — 4/5 articles
- Role of diet in shaping the gut microbiome — 4/5 articles
- Clinical tests vs. consumer tests — 3/5 articles
- General guidance that experts don't recommend routine microbiome testing — 3/5 articles

**Standard Advice (to contrast against):**
1. "Just take a gut microbiome test to see what's going on" — many articles present DTC tests as a reasonable starting point
2. "Look for diversity in your results" — articles emphasize diversity scores without acknowledging how little we can do with that info
3. "Use your test results to adjust your diet" — implies a clear, personalized roadmap exists from test results

**Content Gaps / Seed Differentiation Opportunities:**
- No competitor article addresses the inconsistency between DTC testing providers (the Jackson 2026 study)
- No competitor deeply addresses the stool-vs-colon sampling limitation
- No competitor references the international consensus statement against routine testing
- Seed's angle: Focus on what you CAN do proactively (diet, clinically validated probiotics) rather than waiting for a test to tell you what's wrong

## 6. Synthesis for Drafting

### Seed's Unique Angle (SciCare POV-Driven)
The SciCare POV brief is clear: routine microbiome testing is not recommended, and DTC tests lack reliability. This is Seed's differentiation opportunity — rather than selling false certainty through testing, Seed focuses on what IS clinically validated: taking action with studied formulations that address root causes of digestive distress.

### Narrative Throughline
"Testing your gut sounds like a smart idea — but the science says the available tests can't yet give you a meaningful picture. What CAN you do? Focus on what's clinically validated: a diverse diet and evidence-based probiotics."

### Key Contrasts with Standard Advice
1. **Standard:** "Take a gut microbiome test to understand your gut" → **Seed:** Testing isn't reliable enough yet — results vary wildly between providers and don't capture the full picture
2. **Standard:** "Your diversity score tells you about your health" → **Seed:** There's no consensus on what a "healthy" microbiome looks like
3. **Standard:** "Use test results to personalize your diet" → **Seed:** The science isn't there yet for actionable personalization from a single stool sample

### DS-01 Positioning Notes
- This is a topic where DS-01 fits naturally as "what you can do instead of testing" — focus on validated outcomes rather than measuring unknowns
- Use "clinically validated in the largest clinical trial for a probiotic on bloating + gas in healthy adults°µ"
- Relevant claims: improves regularity, reduces bloating + gas, transforms the gut microbiome
- Do NOT position DS-01 as a diagnostic alternative or as making testing unnecessary — position as "action you can take while the testing science catches up"

### Dirk Gevers Quote Direction
Focus on: why single-snapshot testing is limited from a microbiome science perspective, and why functional outcomes (how you feel) matter more than compositional snapshots.
