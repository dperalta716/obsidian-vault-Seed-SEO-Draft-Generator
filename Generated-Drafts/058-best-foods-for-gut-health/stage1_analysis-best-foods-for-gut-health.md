## 4. User Intent Analysis

### Primary Intent
**Informational**: Users want a clear, actionable list of specific foods they should eat to improve their gut health. They are looking for practical dietary guidance, not deep scientific understanding.

### Secondary Intents
- **Problem-solving**: Many users arrive with symptoms (bloating, irregular digestion, discomfort) and want food-based solutions
- **Validation**: Users may already eat some gut-friendly foods and want confirmation they are on the right track
- **Shopping/meal planning**: Users want to know what to buy at the grocery store

### Core Questions Users Are Trying to Answer
1. What specific foods should I eat to improve my gut health?
2. How do these foods actually help my gut (what is the mechanism)?
3. Are fermented foods really better than other approaches?
4. How much of these foods do I need to eat, and how often?
5. What foods should I avoid for gut health?

| Dietary diversity | 3/5 | "Eat a wide variety of plant foods" |
| Hydration | 2/5 | "Drink plenty of water to support fiber and digestion" |

### Standard Advice to Challenge (Seed Opportunity)
1. **"Just eat more yogurt and fermented foods"** - Oversimplified; doesn't address strain specificity, survival through stomach acid, or dosing. Seed can differentiate with precision probiotic science.
2. **"Take any probiotic supplement"** - Stony Brook explicitly warns against this. Seed's strain-specific, clinically studied approach is a natural counter-narrative.
3. **"Fiber is fiber"** - Most articles treat fiber generically. Seed can distinguish between fiber types and their specific microbiome effects, connecting to prebiotic science.

### Content Gaps in Competitor Articles
- **Microbiome testing or personalization**: No article addresses individual variation in gut microbiome composition
- **Gut-skin axis**: Barely mentioned despite strong user interest
- **Specific bacterial strains and what they do**: Articles mention "good bacteria" generically without naming specific strains or their functions
- **Synbiotic approach (prebiotics + probiotics together)**: Only EatingWell touches on the synergy, but lightly
- **Bioavailability of nutrients from food vs. supplementation**: Not addressed

## Summary

**Competitive landscape**: Dominated by healthcare institutions (Hartford, Hopkins, Stony Brook, NHS) and one strong health publisher (EatingWell). Most articles are list-format, citation-light, and focus on the same 8-10 foods (yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, whole grains, leafy greens, beans, fruits). Only EatingWell provides meaningful academic sourcing.

**Seed's differentiation opportunity**: The market is saturated with generic "eat fermented foods and fiber" advice. Seed can stand apart by (1) explaining WHY specific foods work at the microbiome level with strain-specific science, (2) introducing the synbiotic principle (prebiotic foods + precision probiotics working together), (3) challenging the "any probiotic will do" narrative with evidence-based strain selection, and (4) connecting gut health foods to DM-02's comprehensive nutritional approach and microbiome support.

**Recommended article angle**: Lead with the familiar foods readers expect, but layer in Seed's unique microbiome science perspective - specifically how food diversity feeds different bacterial populations, why strain specificity matters more than generic "probiotics," and how a precision approach to gut health (food + targeted supplementation) outperforms the standard "just eat yogurt" advice.
