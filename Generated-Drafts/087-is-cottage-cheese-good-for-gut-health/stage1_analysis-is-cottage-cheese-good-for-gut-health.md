## 6. Synthesis for Drafting

### SciCare POV (Article Backbone)

**Core Takeaway:** Cottage cheese is nutritionally valuable but its gut health benefits are largely theoretical or indirect. The cooking step can eliminate starter cultures, human research on cottage cheese + gut health is nearly nonexistent, and most health claims are extrapolated from individual components (casein, whey, calcium) — not from studies on cottage cheese as a whole food.

### Standard Advice vs. Seed's Unique Angles

| Standard Advice (Competitors) | SciCare/Seed Perspective | Article Angle |
|---|---|---|
| "Cottage cheese is a probiotic food" | Cottage cheese is cooked after curd formation, which can eliminate cultures. Not typically probiotic-rich unless specifically enriched post-heat. | Honest nuance: yes cottage cheese can be good for gut health, but the probiotic angle is overstated |
| "Just look for live and active cultures on the label" | Even with live cultures, CC hasn't been studied for gut health outcomes in humans. Evidence is extrapolated from components or analogy with yogurt/kefir. | The label isn't the whole story — what matters is whether the microbes survive digestion and reach the colon |
| "Cottage cheese supports digestion through probiotics" | There's a scientific distinction between fermented foods and probiotics. Most CC doesn't meet probiotic criteria (defined strain, demonstrated benefit, adequate dose). | Fermented ≠ probiotic — explain the difference simply |

### Dirk Gevers, Ph.D. Quote Angles

- **On the fermented vs. probiotic distinction:** The idea that any food containing live microbes equals a "probiotic" oversimplifies the science. A true probiotic requires characterized strains at specific doses with demonstrated health benefits.
- **On evidence gaps:** Cottage cheese has clear nutritional value, but its gut health benefits are largely extrapolated from research on other fermented dairy products — not from direct studies.
