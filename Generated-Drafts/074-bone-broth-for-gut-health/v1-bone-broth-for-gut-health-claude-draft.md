# Bone Broth for Gut Health: What It Can (and Can't) Do for Your Digestive System

### Overview

- Bone broth provides amino acids—glutamine, glycine, and proline—that help maintain the physical barrier lining your intestines.
- Research supports these nutrients for gut barrier function, but human clinical evidence on bone broth as a whole food remains limited.
- Gut health depends on both structural integrity (where bone broth helps) and microbial diversity (where it doesn't).
- A comprehensive approach pairs nutrient-rich foods like bone broth with targeted support for the gut microbiome.

---

If you've spent any time searching for ways to heal your gut, bone broth has probably come up more than once. And if you've been sipping it hoping it'll fix years of digestive complaints—or wondering if it's just expensive stock—you're asking the right questions.

Here's the short answer: bone broth does contain nutrients that support your gut lining. That part is real, and there's science to back it up. But the full story is more interesting than "drink broth, heal gut."

Your digestive system is more complex than any single food can address. The gut barrier—that thin but mighty lining separating your intestines from the rest of your body—relies on both physical structure and a thriving community of microbes working together. Bone broth supports one piece of that puzzle, and understanding which piece helps you make smarter choices about the rest.

So let's look at what bone broth actually brings to the table—and where you might want to fill in the gaps.

## What Makes Bone Broth Useful for Your Gut

When you simmer animal bones for hours, you're extracting a concentrated mix of amino acids, collagen, gelatin, and minerals. It's not the bones themselves that matter—it's what dissolves out of them during that long cook.

The most relevant nutrients for gut health are three amino acids: glutamine, glycine, and proline. These aren't random buzzwords. Each one plays a documented role in maintaining the intestinal barrier—the single-cell-thick lining that decides what gets absorbed into your bloodstream and what stays out ([Matar 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10620-025-08997-x)).

Think of your gut lining like a brick wall. The cells are the bricks, and the "tight junctions" between them are the mortar. When that mortar weakens, things can slip through that shouldn't—a concept researchers call increased intestinal permeability ([Khoshbin 2020](https://doi.org/10.1152/ajpgi.00245.2020)).

Bone broth's amino acids help maintain that mortar. But they don't build the wall from scratch, and they don't manage the neighborhood.

## The Amino Acids That Do the Heavy Lifting

### Glutamine: Your Gut Lining's Primary Fuel

Your intestinal cells are hungry. They divide and renew faster than almost any other cells in your body, and their preferred energy source is glutamine—not glucose ([Kim 2017](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40104-017-0183-5)).

When glutamine levels run low, those tight junctions can weaken. Research shows that adequate glutamine supports the proteins that hold intestinal cells together, helping maintain normal permeability ([Wang 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00726-014-1773-4)). One clinical trial found that glutamine supplementation helped restore intestinal barrier function in people with gut permeability issues following infection ([Achamrah 2017](https://doi.org/10.1097/MCO.0000000000000339)).

Bone broth is one dietary source of glutamine, though the exact amount varies depending on the bones used, cook time, and preparation method.

### Glycine and Proline: Structural Support and Calm

Glycine—the most abundant amino acid in collagen—does double duty. It provides raw material for the gut's protective mucus layer, and it has anti-inflammatory properties that may help calm irritated intestinal tissue ([Razak 2017](https://doi.org/10.1155/2017/1716701)).

In animal studies, glycine has been shown to reduce markers of intestinal inflammation and support the gut's natural repair processes ([Stoffels 2011](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2982.2010.01603.x)). Proline, another amino acid abundant in bone broth, contributes to mucin production—the gel-like coating that protects your gut lining from direct contact with digestive acids and bacteria.

Together, these amino acids offer what you might call structural maintenance. They help keep the physical architecture of your gut in working order.

### What About Collagen and Gelatin?

Collagen from bone broth breaks down into gelatin during cooking, and then further into peptides during digestion. Early research suggests these collagen-derived peptides may support intestinal barrier function in cell studies ([Zhang 2018](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu11010052), [Kanda 2023](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16010070)).

But here's an honest caveat: most evidence for collagen's gut benefits comes from lab and animal models. We don't yet have large human trials showing that drinking bone broth delivers enough collagen peptides to meaningfully affect gut permeability in healthy adults. The amino acid benefits are better supported.

It's also worth noting that the collagen content in bone broth varies widely. A long-simmered beef bone broth will yield more collagen than a quick chicken stock. And once those collagen peptides are further broken down during your own digestion, the amount that reaches your intestinal lining in a usable form remains an open question in the research.

## What Bone Broth Can't Do (And What Your Gut Still Needs)

Here's where the "bone broth heals everything" narrative breaks down.

Your gut barrier doesn't function on structure alone. It depends on trillions of microorganisms—your gut microbiome—that actively reinforce barrier function, produce protective metabolites, and regulate immune responses. Bone broth provides zero live microorganisms. It can't introduce new beneficial bacteria, can't transform your microbiome's composition, and can't deliver targeted microbial functions where they're needed most.

"The gut barrier relies on both physical scaffolding—amino acids that support tight junctions—and an active community of microbes that reinforce those structures from within," explains **Dirk Gevers, Ph.D.** "Supporting one without the other addresses only half of the equation."

## How to Get the Most From Bone Broth

If you want bone broth to be a useful part of your gut health routine (not a cure-all), a few things matter:

**Quality and preparation.** Longer cook times (12-24 hours for beef bones, 8-12 for chicken) extract more collagen and amino acids. Using a variety of bones—marrow bones, knuckles, feet—increases the nutrient diversity.

**How much to drink.** Research hasn't established a specific therapeutic dose for bone broth. Most practitioners suggest 1 cup (about 8 ounces) daily as a reasonable starting point. MD Anderson recommends staying around that amount due to potential heavy metal accumulation in longer-simmered broths ([Monro 2013](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mehy.2012.12.026)).

**Source matters for safety.** Bones can accumulate environmental contaminants, including lead. Choosing bones from pasture-raised, organic sources and varying your bone types can help minimize exposure, though no preparation method eliminates risk entirely. If you're buying pre-made broth, look for brands that test for heavy metals and keep sodium under 500mg per serving.

**Watch the sodium.** Commercial bone broths can be surprisingly high in sodium—some contain over 800mg per cup. If you're drinking it daily, either make your own (where you control the salt) or choose low-sodium options.

**Pair it with microbial support.** "A truly comprehensive digestive health strategy involves not just providing your gut with structural nutrients, but also ensuring the right microorganisms are present and active in your system," says **Dirk Gevers, Ph.D.**

## The Key Insight

Bone broth isn't a gut health myth—but it isn't a cure-all, either. Its amino acids genuinely support the physical structures that keep your gut barrier intact. Glutamine fuels intestinal cells. Glycine and proline help maintain the protective mucus layer. These are real, researched mechanisms.

But your gut is a living ecosystem, not just a wall that needs patching. It requires active microbial communities working alongside those structural components to function at its best. The most effective approach to digestive wellness acknowledges both needs—nourishing your gut with quality nutrition while also supporting the microbiome that does much of the daily maintenance work.

Sometimes the best answer isn't choosing between approaches. It's understanding what each one actually does—and building from there.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does bone broth actually heal the gut?

Bone broth contains amino acids like glutamine and glycine that support intestinal barrier function—the physical lining of your gut. Research confirms these nutrients play a role in maintaining tight junctions between intestinal cells ([Wang 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00726-014-1773-4)). That said, "healing" is too strong a word for what the current evidence supports. Human clinical trials on bone broth as a whole food for gut repair are limited. It's more accurate to say bone broth provides supportive nutrients rather than a cure. For active gut conditions, work with a healthcare provider rather than relying on any single food.

### How much bone broth should you drink daily for gut health?

There's no clinically established dose. Most integrative practitioners suggest about 1 cup (8 ounces) per day as a reasonable amount. Some people drink 2-3 cups daily without issues, but researchers have flagged potential heavy metal accumulation with very large or long-term consumption ([Monro 2013](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mehy.2012.12.026)). Starting with one cup and paying attention to how your body responds is a sensible approach.

### Is bone broth safe to drink every day?

For most healthy adults, yes. The main concerns are sodium content (especially in store-bought versions) and potential heavy metal exposure from bones. Making your own broth allows you to control sodium levels. Choosing quality bones from pasture-raised animals and varying your sources helps minimize contamination risk. If you have kidney disease or are monitoring lead exposure, check with your doctor before making bone broth a daily habit.

### Can bone broth replace probiotics for gut health?

No—they do fundamentally different things. Bone broth provides structural nutrients (amino acids, minerals) that support your gut lining. Probiotics introduce live microorganisms that interact with your immune system, produce beneficial compounds, and help maintain microbial balance in your gut. Bone broth contains no live bacteria and cannot influence microbiome composition. Think of them as addressing different layers of gut health: bone broth supports the physical barrier, while probiotics like [DS-01® Daily Synbiotic](https://seed.com/daily-synbiotic) support the biological ecosystem that reinforces it°.
