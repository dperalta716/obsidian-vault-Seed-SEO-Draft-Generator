# Stage 1 Pre-Draft Analysis (v2 — Coverage): Gut Health Drinks

## 1. User Search Intent

The primary intent is a **commercial/informational hybrid**: "Which drinks actually support my gut, and which are hype?" Searchers want a scannable, ranked list of specific beverages plus enough honest science to choose one with confidence.

Core questions:
- What are the best drinks for gut health, specifically (named drinks, not vague advice)?
- Do these drinks actually work, or is it marketing? (Strong skeptical sub-intent — the strongest competitors lead with caveats.)
- Are prebiotic/probiotic sodas (Poppi, Olipop, Culture Pop) legit, or just lower-sugar soda?
- How much sugar is hiding in these drinks, and which are pasteurized (live vs. dead cultures)?
- Do the live microbes even survive to reach my gut?

## 5. Article-by-Article Analysis

### GoodRx — "The 5 Best Drinks for Your Gut Health"
~1,450w, physician-written/edited, high E-E-A-T. ~16 primary citations (~1 per 90w). Skeptical consumer-protection framing (sugar, pasteurization, unverified-claim caveats up front). Each drink own H2 with mechanism + caution. Covers kombucha, ginger tea, kefir, ACV, green tea. Balanced ACV treatment (lists GERD/teeth/kidney risks). **This is the depth bar for the flagship drinks.** Whitespace: no evidence-tiering, no microbiome-mechanism depth, no survival angle.

### Mayo Clinic Press — "Probiotic drinks: what really works"
~1,100w, expert-authority (Dr. Karnatovskaia), near-zero academic citation (3 consumer links). Soda-skeptic angle: separates naturally fermented (kefir, kombucha — endorsed) from added-probiotic sodas (doubted). Raises stomach-acid survival doubt but never resolves it. Food-first conclusion ("gut health sodas don't count as probiotic-rich foods"). Whitespace: mechanistic citation + the unresolved survival/delivery problem.

### EatingWell — "15 Tasty Drinks for Better Gut Health"
~800w, pure recipe listicle (smoothies, mocktails, wellness shots, chia tonics), ZERO scientific citations. Inclusion rule: prebiotic/probiotic ingredients, no added sugar/alcohol. Kefir smoothie, kombucha mocktail, ginger/turmeric tonics, chia-fiber tonics. Whitespace: no mechanism, no dosing, no live-culture caveats.

### Wildwonder — "12 of the Best Probiotic Drinks for Gut Health"
~2,300w, RDN-bylined brand blog (sells its own drink, placed #1). 12 NCBI citations front-loaded in benefits section; the roundup itself largely uncited. **Broadest item list:** Wildwonder, kombucha, kefir, Yakult, probiotic yogurt drinks, kvass, coconut kefir, probiotic water, probiotic juices, buttermilk, rejuvelac, jun tea. This is the source of the long-tail fringe items. Whitespace: no honest "what beverages can vs. can't deliver" framing; treats all drinks as interchangeable; names strains.

### Browns Valley Market — "Top 6 Foods and Drinks for Gut Health"
~750w, grocery-retailer content marketing. Thin. 3 DOI citations. Brand name-dropping (GT's Synergy, Poppi). Drinks: kombucha, prebiotic sodas (Poppi), kefir; rest are fermented foods. Whitespace: no mechanism depth, no nuance.

## 6. Synthesis for Drafting

**Primary intent to satisfy:** rank the real gut-health drinks by evidence strength, honestly, with enough mechanism to feel trustworthy — and cover EVERY drink a reader will have seen on competing lists so nothing feels missing.

**Seed's differentiating angles (from SciCare POV + Claims Library):**
1. **Evidence-tiering honesty** — the SciCare throughline: dairy kefir has the strongest human evidence; kombucha and green tea show promise but lack robust human trials; prebiotic drinks are biologically plausible but under-researched in beverage form. NO competitor tiers drinks by evidence quality — primary whitespace.
2. **Individual variability of green tea** — most catechins reach the large intestine where YOUR microbiome decides the benefit (Liu 2024). Fresh, science-forward.
3. **Fermented food ≠ probiotic + "do the microbes survive?"** — Mayo raises survival and drops it. We answer it via the "Engineered to Survive" pillar in a clearly SEPARATED DS-01 paragraph (ViaCap®, SHIME-tested), framed as a different tool, not superior to drinks. POV: fermented foods are nutritious but rarely meet the probiotic definition.
4. **Honest caveat on the evidence base** — many trials are small/high-bias (Kairey 2023). Built into prose.

**Evidence-base composition note:** Tier 0 is human + review-heavy. Green tea, prebiotic-drink, ACV, and ginger claims rest partly on mechanistic/review/narrow-population data where beverage-format human trials are lacking — an **auto-caveat is MANDATORY** in those sections, varied wording each time.

**DS-01 positioning:** Topic is a food/beverage category, so DS-01 CAN appear — but only with objective "different tool, different mechanism" framing in its OWN paragraph, after probiotics are discussed as a category. Use bloating/gas + regularity claims (Allegretti, "largest clinical trial for a probiotic on bloating + gas in healthy adults°µ") and the survivability angle (ViaCap*°). No implied superiority over any drink. No synergy claims. Single separated mention (bone broth test).
