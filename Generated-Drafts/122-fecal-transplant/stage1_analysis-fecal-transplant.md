# Stage 1 Pre-Draft Analysis: Fecal Transplant

## 1. User Search Intent

**Primary intent: Informational.** Someone searching "fecal transplant" wants a plain-language explanation of what the procedure is, what it treats, how it's done, whether it works, whether it's safe, and where the science is headed. The query is broad and educational — not transactional, not local-service, not product-shopping. The audience is a mix of: patients (or caregivers) facing recurrent *C. difficile* infection, the microbiome-curious general reader, and people who've heard "poop transplant" in the news and want the real story.

**Core questions users are trying to answer:**
- What actually *is* a fecal transplant (FMT), in normal words?
- What conditions does it treat — and is it only *C. diff*, or more?
- How is it done (colonoscopy, capsule, enema)? Am I awake?
- Does it work? How well?
- Is it safe? What are the risks?
- Where do donors come from, and how are they screened?
- What's the future — and is there anything I can do for my own gut short of a transplant?

**Compliance note (binds the whole draft):** This is a **disease-treatment / medical-procedure topic**. Per the POV "Navigating Probiotic and Disease Discussions" and the skill's Topic Appropriateness Gate, FMT is a clinical therapy for *C. difficile*, IBD, and IBS — conditions NOT in the DS-01 Claims Library. **DS-01 must NOT be positioned as relevant to FMT, as an alternative to FMT, or as a treatment for any of these conditions.** General microbiome science may be discussed, but DS-01 product claims are kept out of the disease context entirely. The default here is to OMIT DS-01, or at most include a single, clearly-separated, hedged general-microbiome line that makes no therapeutic or alternative-to-FMT implication.

**3b. Wikipedia — "Fecal microbiota transplant"** (very long, heavily cited). **4. UChicago Biological Sciences — "Fecal transplant a potential health risk"** (~1,100w, news, cites DeLeon et al. *Cell* 2025). Directly supports the brief's "we don't fully understand which microbes drive success/failure" takeaway.

**5. ASM — "FMT: Past, Present and Future"** (~2,000w, expert-sourced, richly cited). Best on: history (4th-century "yellow soup," 1958 enemas, 1980s CDI use, 2013 NEJM landmark trial 94% vs 31%, 2013 FDA enforcement discretion), today's use (reserved for recurrent CDI after ≥2 antibiotic failures, ~70–90% cure), commercial products (Rebyota 70.6% vs 57.5%; Vowst 12.4% vs 39.8%; prices $9k/$17.5k), mechanism mystery (sterile filtrate also works), unknown-pathogen risk, and the "defined consortia" future. Single richest narrative source.

## 6. Synthesis for Drafting

**Narrative throughline (from POV brief Key Takeaway):** FMT is a genuine, clinically established success story for recurrent *C. difficile* — and a cautionary tale everywhere else. The honest story is "established for *C. diff*, promising-but-unproven for IBD/IBS, and methodologically thin beyond that," anchored by the deeper scientific point that we still don't fully understand *which* microbes drive success, that protocols aren't standardized, and that a single transplant can have persistent off-target effects (DeLeon/*Cell* 2025). This is Seed's POV-aligned differentiation: respect for the microbiome's complexity and intolerance for overreach.

**Seed angle (NOT a product pitch — DS-01 omitted from clinical context):** Where competitors either over-hype FMT's future or just list conditions, our article leans into the brief's nuance — the gut microbiome is a regional, context-dependent ecosystem, and "more microbes, anywhere" is not the same as "the right microbes, in the right place." A Dirk Gevers, Ph.D. quote can reinforce microbiome-complexity / precision-science WITHOUT naming any product and WITHOUT positioning DS-01 as an FMT alternative.

**DS-01 handling:** Per Topic Appropriateness Gate — **OMIT DS-01 from this article.** FMT treats diseases outside the Claims Library; positioning DS-01 anywhere near it risks an implied therapeutic/alternative claim. At most, ONE clearly-separated general line may note that everyday probiotics and a clinical procedure like FMT are entirely different things (to *prevent* reader confusion, not to sell) — and even that must avoid any "DS-01 instead" implication. Leaning toward full omission. No DS-01 disclaimers needed if omitted.
