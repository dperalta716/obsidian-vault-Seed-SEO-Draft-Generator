# Fecal Transplant: What It Is, What It Treats, and How Well It Actually Works

### Overview

- A fecal transplant (formally, fecal microbiota transplantation, or FMT) moves gut microbes from a healthy, carefully screened donor into a patient—usually to treat a stubborn infection called recurrent *Clostridioides difficile*.
- For recurrent *C. diff*, it works well, with cure rates often in the 70–90% range. For nearly everything else, it's still being studied.
- It can be delivered by colonoscopy, enema, or a swallowable capsule, and two products now carry FDA approval.
- Donors are screened intensively, but FMT still carries real risks—including transferring something harmful, and some effects scientists are only beginning to understand.
- The honest takeaway: FMT is a genuine success story for one condition and an open research question for the rest.

You've probably seen the phrase "poop transplant" in a headline and done a double take. It sounds like a punchline. It's actually one of the more surprising procedures in modern medicine.

So here's the short answer. A fecal transplant is a real, doctor-supervised treatment that moves gut microbes from a healthy donor into a patient who needs them—mainly to treat a gut infection that keeps coming back after antibiotics fail.

But there's a more interesting story underneath the headline. FMT is a clear win for one specific condition and a wide-open question almost everywhere else. Understanding that gap is the most useful thing you can take away.

## What Is a Fecal Transplant?

At its core, the idea is simple, even if the source material is unglamorous. You're moving a working microbial community from someone healthy into someone whose own community has been knocked down.

### What FMT Actually Is

Fecal microbiota transplantation transfers stool—processed in a lab—from a screened donor into a recipient's digestive tract. The stool itself isn't the point. What matters is the thousands of living microbes inside it.

Think of the gut as a crowded, self-regulating community of microorganisms. When that community gets thinned out, troublemakers can move in. FMT is an attempt to repopulate the neighborhood with good tenants. You'll also see it called a stool transplant, bacteriotherapy, or intestinal microbiota transplant—all the same thing.

### How It Works in the Gut

A healthy gut is home to trillions of microbes, and most of them are quietly helpful. One of their jobs is keeping harmful microbes from taking over—a kind of crowd control that scientists call colonization resistance ([Yadegar 2024](https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22)).

When that protective community gets disrupted—often by a course of antibiotics—the crowd control breaks down. FMT works by reintroducing a diverse mix of microbes that can re-establish that resistance, which is why restoring microbial diversity is thought to be central to its success ([Yadegar 2024](https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22)). Here's the honest part, though: researchers still don't fully agree on exactly *which* microbes do the heavy lifting.

## What a Fecal Transplant Treats

This is where the story splits in two. There's the one condition FMT is genuinely proven for—and a long list of conditions where it's still being investigated.

### Recurrent C. difficile Infection

*Clostridioides difficile*, usually shortened to *C. diff*, is a bacterium that can take over the gut after antibiotics wipe out the good microbes keeping it in check. It causes fever, cramping, and severe diarrhea, and in older or chronically ill people it can become dangerous.

The frustrating thing about *C. diff* is how it recurs. Antibiotics can knock it back, but they don't always clear it, and the infection can return again and again. FMT breaks that cycle by rebuilding the microbial community that naturally holds *C. diff* at bay. It's typically reserved for people whose infection keeps returning after standard antibiotic treatment.

### How Well It Works for C. diff

This is the part that turned skeptics into believers. A landmark 2013 trial compared donor-stool infusion against the antibiotic vancomycin for recurrent *C. diff*—and the difference was striking, with cure rates of 94% versus 31% ([van Nood 2013](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1205037)). The result was so lopsided that the trial was stopped early on ethical grounds.

Real-world numbers are a little more modest. Across delivery methods, cure rates generally land in the 70–90% range, and the most rigorous analyses tend toward the lower end of that. Still, for a condition that used to trap people in a miserable loop, that's a genuine breakthrough.

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Beyond *C. diff*, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)—which includes ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease—is the most actively studied area for FMT. And the evidence here is promising but uneven.

A meta-analysis pooling 14 randomized trials found that FMT improved both clinical and endoscopic remission in ulcerative colitis compared with placebo, with oral delivery and multi-donor protocols linked to better outcomes ([Gefen 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10151-025-03113-7)). That sounds encouraging—and it is. But a broader umbrella review of 16 meta-analyses found that while remission benefits showed up consistently, the methodological quality of most underlying studies was low ([Malik 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10620-025-08946-8)). In plain terms: the signal is real, but the research isn't yet strong enough to call it settled.

### Irritable Bowel Syndrome

FMT has also been studied for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), with some encouraging results. A network meta-analysis comparing FMT against probiotics, prebiotics, and synbiotics found that FMT and probiotics had the most favorable effects, with no serious adverse events reported in either group ([Wu 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16132114)).

That's worth sitting with. It suggests FMT may help some people with IBS—but also that simpler, lower-risk options performed comparably. For a condition as varied as IBS, that nuance matters.

### The Conditions Still Under Study

You'll see FMT floated as a possible answer for an eye-catching list of conditions: obesity, type 2 diabetes, autism, depression, multiple sclerosis, and liver disease, among others ([Montrose 2024](https://doi.org/10.1097/MOG.0000000000001027)). The logic is seductive—if the gut microbiome touches so much of our health, why not reset it?

Here's the reality check. Outside of *C. diff*, and to a lesser degree IBD and IBS, the evidence for these uses is preliminary and inconsistent ([Montrose 2024](https://doi.org/10.1097/MOG.0000000000001027)). Major medical centers are clear that there's no established proof FMT is safe and effective for conditions beyond *C. diff*. Interesting hypothesis, not yet a treatment.

## How a Fecal Transplant Is Done

If you've pictured this as one fixed procedure, it's actually a family of methods. The right one depends on the patient and the product.

### Colonoscopy, Capsule, and Enema

FMT can be delivered a few different ways. Historically, colonoscopy has been the go-to—a thin, camera-tipped tube guides the prepared microbial solution into the colon, exactly where it's needed.

There's also the enema, which delivers the microbes into the rectum and lower colon without bowel prep or sedation. And then there's the option that surprises people most: a swallowable capsule containing freeze-dried microbes, designed to stay sealed until it reaches the colon. Less commonly, a nasogastric tube routes the solution through the nose into the upper gut, though that carries more risk. If your transplant is delivered by colonoscopy, you'll typically be sedated; the enema and capsule routes generally don't require it.

### Where Donors Come From

Donors are the quiet backbone of the whole process. A donor might be someone the patient knows, or an anonymous volunteer whose sample lives in a screened stool bank.

Screening is rigorous, and for good reason. Potential donors undergo blood and stool testing for a long list of infections—hepatitis, HIV, intestinal parasites, *C. diff* itself, and more ([Yadegar 2024](https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22)). They're also generally ruled out for things like recent antibiotic use, gastrointestinal disorders, and autoimmune conditions. Once accepted, the donation is blended with sterile saline, filtered, and either used fresh or frozen for later.

### The FDA-Approved Products

For years, there were no commercial FMT products—physicians worked from stool banks. That changed recently. Two products have now earned FDA approval specifically for preventing recurrent *C. diff*: one delivered by enema, and one as an oral capsule.

The capsule product is especially notable because it doesn't contain a full, undefined stool mixture. Instead, it's purified down to a specific fraction of bacterial spores. In its phase 3 trial, it lowered the recurrence rate to 12.4% versus 39.8% with placebo ([Feuerstadt 2022](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa2106516)). Approval matters here for a practical reason, too—it opens the door to consistent manufacturing and the possibility of insurance coverage.

## Is a Fecal Transplant Safe?

Short answer: for recurrent *C. diff*, FMT is generally considered safe and well tolerated. But "generally safe" isn't the same as "risk-free," and the honest picture has a few layers.

### Common Side Effects

Most side effects are mild, temporary, and tied to the delivery method rather than the microbes themselves. People commonly report bloating, gas, cramping, or mild nausea in the day or two afterward.

These typically pass on their own. They're the kind of short-lived discomfort you'd expect from a gut that's adjusting, not a sign that something has gone wrong.

### The Serious Risks

The bigger concern is the small but real possibility of transferring something harmful along with the helpful microbes. A pathogen that was dormant or well-controlled in the donor could cause trouble in a vulnerable recipient ([Yadegar 2024](https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22)). This is precisely why donor screening is so intensive—and why standardized protocols still don't exist across the board.

There's a subtler version of this risk, too: unknown threats. Screening can only catch pathogens we already know to look for. Newly emerging ones can slip through simply because no one is testing for them yet.

### What We Still Don't Know

Here's where the science gets genuinely humbling. A 2025 study found that when colon-derived microbes from a standard FMT colonize parts of the gut they don't normally inhabit—like the small intestine—they can persist there for months and even alter the local tissue, with downstream effects on metabolism and behavior in mice ([DeLeon 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2025.05.014)).

The researchers' point wasn't that FMT is dangerous. It's that putting the right microbes in the *wrong place* may have lasting, off-target consequences we don't fully understand yet. It's a reminder that the gut isn't one uniform space—it's a set of distinct regional ecosystems, each suited to its own residents.

## The Future of Fecal Transplants

If there's a direction the field is moving, it's away from "transfer everything and hope" and toward precision. The black-box nature of whole-stool FMT—where the exact microbial mix is undefined—is exactly what researchers want to move beyond.

The vision is rationally designed products built from specific, well-characterized microbial communities rather than raw donor stool. The approved capsule made of purified spores is an early step in that direction. Such defined products would be easier to manufacture, screen, and study—and safer by design.

"The gut microbiome isn't one uniform environment—it's a set of distinct regional ecosystems, each suited to its own residents," explains **Dirk Gevers, Ph.D.** "The more we learn, the clearer it becomes that precision matters far more than simply adding more microbes."

That's the throughline for where FMT is headed. For *C. diff*, the more-microbes approach already works. For more complex conditions, the path forward almost certainly runs through specificity—understanding which microbes matter, and where they belong.

## The Key Insight

A fecal transplant is one of those rare ideas that sounds absurd and turns out to be quietly profound. For recurrent *C. diff*, it has rewritten what's possible, freeing people from a cycle that antibiotics alone couldn't break.

But the broader lesson is about humility. The same procedure that works so well for one infection is still unproven for the conditions people most hope it might solve. And the newest research suggests the gut is more regionally specific—and less forgiving of a one-size-fits-all reset—than we assumed.

So the honest framing is also the most hopeful one. FMT shows us how powerful a healthy microbial community can be. The next chapter is learning to harness that power with precision, not just enthusiasm.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### Why would a person need a fecal transplant?

The most common reason by far is a recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* (*C. diff*) infection that keeps coming back after antibiotics. *C. diff* tends to flourish when antibiotics wipe out the gut microbes that normally keep it in check, and in some people it returns again and again.

A fecal transplant works by restoring that protective community of microbes, helping crowd *C. diff* back out. It's usually offered to people whose infection has returned after standard treatment, or whose disease is severe. While researchers are studying FMT for other conditions, recurrent *C. diff* remains the one well-established reason a doctor would recommend it.

### Are you awake during a fecal transplant?

It depends on how the transplant is delivered. If it's done by colonoscopy—historically the most common method—you'll usually receive sedation, much like you would for a routine colonoscopy, so you won't be fully alert during the procedure.

The other delivery methods are gentler. An enema, which is one of the FDA-approved routes, generally doesn't require sedation or bowel prep, so you'd be awake. The same goes for the oral capsule, which you simply swallow at home over a few days. So whether you're awake really comes down to the route your healthcare provider chooses for your situation.

### How much does a fecal transplant cost?

Cost varies widely depending on the method and whether a commercial product is used. The two FDA-approved products carry notable list prices—roughly $9,000 for the enema-based product and around $17,500 for the oral capsule course, before any insurance coverage.

One reason FDA approval matters is that it opens the door to insurance reimbursement, which can change what a patient actually pays out of pocket. If FMT is a possibility for you, it's worth asking your provider and insurer directly, since costs differ a lot by setting and region.

### Are fecal transplants still being done?

Yes. Far from fading out, FMT has become the standard of care for recurrent *C. diff* that hasn't responded to antibiotics, and access has actually expanded recently. The arrival of two FDA-approved products—one enema, one oral capsule—has made the treatment more standardized and available than it used to be.

At the same time, researchers continue to study FMT for conditions like inflammatory bowel disease and IBS, where results are promising but not yet conclusive. So FMT is very much an active, evolving treatment: firmly established for one use, and under serious investigation for several others.
