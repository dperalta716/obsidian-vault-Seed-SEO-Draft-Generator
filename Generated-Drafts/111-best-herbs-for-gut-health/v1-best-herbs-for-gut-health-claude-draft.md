# Best Herbs for Gut Health: Ranking the Evidence From Peppermint to Turmeric

### Overview

- Peppermint oil has the strongest and most consistent clinical evidence for digestive symptom relief among all herbs studied for gut health.
- Ginger, turmeric, chamomile, and licorice root each support digestion through different mechanisms--from calming gut muscle spasms to reducing intestinal inflammation.
- Most clinical studies on herbs for gut health have been conducted in people with functional digestive conditions like IBS, so results may not apply equally to everyone.
- Herbs primarily address digestive symptoms, while probiotics work through a different pathway--directly interacting with the gut's microbial community.

You've probably noticed that every "gut health" list on the internet includes turmeric, ginger, and peppermint. And for good reason--people have been reaching for these herbs to soothe digestive complaints for thousands of years. But here's what most of those lists don't tell you: the evidence behind each herb varies dramatically.

Some have been tested in large, well-designed clinical trials. Others are still relying mostly on animal studies and traditional use. And the distinction matters, especially when you're deciding what's actually worth adding to your routine.

So rather than giving you another alphabetical rundown of "gut-friendly herbs," we ranked the ones with the most research behind them--and dug into how each one actually works in your digestive system.

## The Herbs With the Strongest Evidence

A Cochrane review--one of the most rigorous types of scientific analysis--assessed 41 trials across 27 different herbal medicines for digestive symptoms in adults with functional dyspepsia ([Baez 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013323.pub2)). The strongest evidence, at moderate certainty, pointed to peppermint oil (combined with caraway oil), turmeric, and licorice root for improving overall symptoms and quality of life versus placebo.

A separate umbrella review of randomized IBS trials found that peppermint oil carried the highest certainty evidence among all plant-based compounds studied ([Zeraattalab-Motlagh 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae107)). That's a meaningful distinction in a field where "promising preliminary data" gets thrown around a lot.

One important caveat worth knowing: the majority of these clinical trials were conducted in people with diagnosed functional digestive conditions--primarily irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or functional dyspepsia ([Moniruzzaman 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010)). That doesn't mean the herbs won't help otherwise healthy people, but it does mean the evidence is strongest in those specific populations.

### What Sets Herbs Apart

A review published in a leading gastroenterology journal found that herbs work through several distinct mechanisms: improving how quickly food moves through the gut, reducing the gut's sensitivity to pain signals, calming low-grade intestinal inflammation, and shifting the gut microbiome ([Moniruzzaman 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010)).

What makes herbs different from single-compound drugs is that most contain multiple active compounds targeting several of these pathways at once. Researchers suggest this multi-target approach may be part of why they show up consistently in the clinical literature.

## Peppermint: The One With the Receipts

If you had to pick a single herb for digestive support, peppermint oil would be the one with the most evidence behind it. Its active compound, menthol, relaxes the smooth muscles lining your digestive tract--which is why it can help with cramping, bloating, and that uncomfortable "tightness" after eating.

Clinical trials have shown that enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules can reduce symptoms in people with IBS, including abdominal pain, bloating, and gas ([Alammar 2019](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12906-019-2440-7)). The enteric coating matters--it helps the oil bypass the stomach and reach the intestines, where it does its work.

One thing to be aware of: peppermint oil can relax the lower esophageal sphincter (the muscle that keeps stomach acid where it belongs), so if you deal with acid reflux, peppermint tea might be a gentler option than concentrated oil capsules.

## Ginger: Your Gut's Motility Ally

Ginger has been a go-to for nausea for centuries, and modern research has caught up with the traditional wisdom. A detailed review mapping ginger's mechanisms from human clinical trials found that it works primarily by blocking the receptors that trigger nausea, reducing gut inflammation, and speeding gastric emptying--the rate at which food moves from your stomach into your small intestine ([Crichton 2023](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac115)).

That last point is particularly relevant if you tend to feel uncomfortably full long after eating. Slow gastric emptying is one of the more common drivers of that post-meal heaviness, and ginger appears to help move things along.

Fresh ginger in cooking, ginger tea, and even powdered ginger have all been used in studies, though doses and preparations vary. The mechanisms have been documented across gastrointestinal and metabolic conditions in adults, giving ginger one of the broader evidence bases among culinary herbs.

## Turmeric: The Microbiome Connection

Turmeric's story is a bit different from the other herbs on this list. Its active compound, curcumin, is famously poorly absorbed into the bloodstream--which sounds like a problem until you realize that it means curcumin accumulates in the gut, where it can interact directly with intestinal cells and gut bacteria ([Servida 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25147710)).

Research shows that curcumin can modulate the composition of gut bacteria, strengthen the gut barrier, and reduce intestinal inflammation ([Cerullo 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17091430)). That gut barrier piece is worth paying attention to--it's the physical lining that keeps what's inside your intestines from leaking into your bloodstream.

The Cochrane review found moderate-certainty evidence for turmeric improving digestive symptoms in functional dyspepsia ([Baez 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013323.pub2)). And the umbrella review of IBS trials noted that curcumin improved symptom severity compared to placebo, though the evidence certainty remains moderate to low ([Zeraattalab-Motlagh 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae107)).

It's worth noting that most clinical studies on curcumin's gut-specific effects have been conducted in adults with metabolic conditions like type 2 diabetes and metabolic syndrome. The research is promising for broader populations, but that's the context.

## Chamomile and Licorice Root: Supporting Players With Real Evidence

Chamomile has a long history as a digestive soother, and while the human clinical data is more limited than peppermint or ginger, it's not without substance. Studies have shown it can help with colic symptoms in infants and mild diarrhea in children, though these trials often combined chamomile with other herbs ([Srivastava 2010](https://doi.org/10.1186/1472-6882-10-56)). Its antioxidant properties may help protect against stomach ulcers, though most of that evidence comes from animal models.

Licorice root--specifically the deglycyrrhizinated (DGL) form--rounds out the Cochrane review's top tier. DGL has the glycyrrhizin removed (the compound that can raise blood pressure), while retaining properties that may support the gut's protective mucus lining. The Cochrane review found moderate-certainty evidence for licorice root improving digestive symptoms ([Baez 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013323.pub2)).

A multi-herb preparation combining peppermint, ginger, and chamomile also showed consistent benefit for adults with functional dyspepsia and IBS, suggesting these herbs may work well together through their overlapping pathways ([Moniruzzaman 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010)).

## Where Herbs End and Probiotics Begin

Here's something that often gets lost in the conversation about herbs for gut health: herbs primarily work by easing symptoms. They relax muscles, reduce inflammation, speed up motility, and calm pain signaling. Those are all genuinely helpful--but they're addressing what you feel, not necessarily what's happening in your gut's microbial community.

Probiotics work through a fundamentally different mechanism. Rather than calming symptoms from the outside in, probiotics are live microorganisms that interact directly with the gut ecosystem--engaging immune cells, producing beneficial metabolites, and supporting the gut barrier from within.

"Herbs can be effective tools for calming digestive symptoms, but they work through different pathways than probiotics," says **Dirk Gevers, Ph.D.** "While peppermint may relax gut muscles or ginger may speed gastric emptying, probiotics interact directly with the microbial ecosystem--and that distinction matters when you're thinking about long-term digestive health."

These are different tools for different jobs, and one doesn't replace the other. You can drink ginger tea for post-meal comfort and take a clinically studied probiotic for microbiome support--they're addressing two separate dimensions of gut health.

## The Key Insight

The best herbs for gut health aren't interchangeable--they each work through specific mechanisms, and the strength of evidence behind them varies more than most lists acknowledge. Peppermint oil leads the pack with the highest-certainty clinical evidence, followed by ginger for motility, turmeric for its unique microbiome and barrier-supporting properties, and licorice root for mucosal protection.

What's most interesting about the research is that multi-herb preparations targeting several pathways at once show some of the most promising results. Your gut is complex, and supporting it well often means addressing more than one mechanism.

And if you're thinking about your gut microbiome specifically--the trillions of microorganisms that influence everything from digestion to immune function--that's where probiotics come in as a complementary approach. Herbs can help you feel better day to day. A clinically studied probiotic can help support the microbial environment that keeps your gut working well over time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best herb for gut health?

Based on the available clinical evidence, peppermint oil has the most consistent and highest-certainty data for relieving digestive symptoms like bloating, cramping, and abdominal pain. A Cochrane review of 41 trials found moderate-certainty evidence for peppermint oil (especially combined with caraway oil) improving symptoms in functional dyspepsia ([Baez 2023](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013323.pub2)), and an umbrella review ranked it highest among all plant-based interventions studied in IBS trials ([Zeraattalab-Motlagh 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae107)). That said, the "best" herb depends on your specific symptoms--ginger may be better for nausea and slow digestion, while turmeric may offer more support for gut inflammation.

### What are the super six foods for your gut?

The "super six" typically refers to a rotating variety of plant-based foods that feed your gut microbiome--things like garlic, onions, leeks, asparagus, bananas, and oats. These foods are rich in prebiotic fiber, which serves as fuel for the beneficial bacteria already living in your gut. While herbs like peppermint and ginger can help ease digestive symptoms, prebiotic-rich foods and probiotics work more directly on the microbial community itself. A well-rounded approach to gut health includes symptom-soothing herbs, prebiotic-rich foods, and--if you're looking for clinically studied microbiome support--a probiotic formulation.

### What is the 7 day gut reset?

A "7 day gut reset" usually involves eliminating processed foods, alcohol, and added sugars while increasing fiber, fermented foods, and hydration for one week. While there's nothing wrong with eating cleaner for a week, it's worth knowing that the gut microbiome doesn't fundamentally transform in seven days. Research shows that meaningful shifts in gut microbial composition take consistent effort over weeks to months. If you're experiencing persistent digestive discomfort, a better long-term strategy includes regular use of evidence-backed herbs (like peppermint or ginger), a diverse diet rich in fiber and polyphenols, and consistent daily use of a clinically studied probiotic.
