10. **When to supplement vs. when diet is sufficient** - Found in 5/5 articles
    - Typical depth/angle: Universal emphasis: supplements only help if you're deficient. Taking more doesn't equal more energy if levels are adequate.

11. **Safety considerations and side effects** - Found in 5/5 articles
    - Typical depth/angle: All articles warn about potential toxicity (iron overload, vitamin D toxicity), drug interactions, and importance of consulting healthcare provider.

12. **Third-party testing and quality** - Found in 2/5 articles
    - Typical depth/angle: Some mention NSF, USP certification for supplement quality.

### Topic Coverage Patterns:
- Start with iron as #1 most important energy nutrient for women (due to menstruation)

## 6. Synthesis for Drafting

### Competitive Baseline Summary:

Competitive articles are comprehensive (1500-3500 words), evidence-based with 10-15+ academic citations, and follow a predictable structure: introduction to fatigue → individual nutrient deep-dives → lifestyle factors → safety considerations. They emphasize 'test before supplement' and 'only helps if deficient' messaging. Most include specific food sources for each nutrient. Tone ranges from highly authoritative/scientific (Harvard, PMC) to practical/clinical (GoodRx, Verywell, Midi). All balance biochemical explanation with practical application.

**Word count range**: 900-3500 words (average ~2200 for consumer articles)

### Gaps & Opportunities for Seed:

- **Gap 1**: Most articles are gender-neutral despite keyword targeting women - opportunity to focus more explicitly on women's unique energy challenges (menstruation, pregnancy, perimenopause, menopause)
- **Gap 2**: Limited coverage of how nutrients work synergistically together - most treat each vitamin/mineral in isolation
- **Gap 3**: Few articles distinguish between 'energy for daily activities' vs 'athletic/exercise performance' - could clarify this distinction
- **Gap 4**: Minimal discussion of bioavailability differences between supplement forms (e.g., methylcobalamin vs cyanocobalamin for B12)
- **Gap 5**: Limited coverage of how gut health/microbiome affects nutrient absorption and energy
- **Gap 6**: Could differentiate more clearly between nutrients that provide immediate energy vs those that support energy production over time
- **Gap 7**: Opportunity to include more women-specific clinical scenarios and case examples

### Recommended Approach:

1. **Meet baseline** by covering: Iron (menstruation connection), B vitamins (B12, B6, B9, riboflavin, niacin, thiamine), Vitamin D, Magnesium, CoQ10, food sources, deficiency symptoms, safety considerations

2. **Layer Seed perspective** via:
   - Bioavailable forms and precision dosing (DM-02 emphasis on methylated B vitamins, vitamin D3 vs D2)
   - Synergistic formulation approach (how nutrients work together, not in isolation)
   - Microbiome connection to nutrient absorption
   - Women-specific contexts (menstrual cycle, perimenopause)

3. **Target word count**: 1600-1800 words (baseline articles range: 900-3500)

4. **Citation strategy**: Aim for ~12-15 academic sources based on competitive patterns, focusing on deficiency symptoms, mechanisms of action, and women-specific research
