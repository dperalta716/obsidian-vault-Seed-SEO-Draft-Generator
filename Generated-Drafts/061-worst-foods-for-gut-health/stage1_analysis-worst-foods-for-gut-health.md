## 4. User Intent Analysis

**Primary intent**: Informational — users want a definitive list of foods to avoid for better gut health.

**Secondary intents**:
- **Action-oriented**: What specific dietary changes to make NOW
- **Validation-seeking**: Confirmation that foods they suspect are causing issues are actually problematic
- **Solution-seeking**: What to eat INSTEAD (replacement foods)
- **Symptom-relief**: Many searchers are actively experiencing GI discomfort

**Core questions users are trying to answer**:
1. Which specific foods should I stop eating to improve my gut health?
2. WHY do these foods damage my gut? (mechanism)
3. What should I eat instead?
4. Are foods I thought were healthy (kombucha, probiotic foods, added fiber) actually bad?
5. How quickly can my gut recover if I eliminate these foods?

**Audience profile**: Health-conscious adults experiencing digestive issues (bloating, gas, irregular bowel movements), likely already aware of gut health basics but seeking specific, actionable guidance.

## 5. Competitive Baseline & Content Gaps

### Standard advice across all competitors:
- Processed/ultra-processed foods are the #1 enemy
- Refined sugar and high-fructose corn syrup feed harmful bacteria
- Artificial sweeteners disrupt the microbiome
- Fried foods reduce microbial diversity
- Alcohol damages gut barrier and microbiota
- Refined grains lack fiber needed for beneficial bacteria
- Red meat and processed meats are inflammatory

### Content gaps and opportunities for Seed:

1. **Microbiome mechanism depth**: Most articles list foods to avoid but don't explain the specific microbial mechanisms (which bacteria are affected, how SCFA production changes, what happens to the gut barrier at a cellular level). Seed can go deeper on the science while keeping it accessible.

2. **The emulsifier/additive story**: Only Oshi Health touches on emulsifiers and food additives specifically. This is an underexplored, high-impact angle — research on polysorbate 80, carboxymethylcellulose, and carrageenan disrupting the mucus layer is compelling and relatively unknown to general audiences.

3. **Fiber nuance**: EatingWell's contrarian take on added fiber is a strong angle no one else covers. Seed can differentiate by explaining why NOT ALL fiber is created equal — some fermentable fibers can worsen symptoms, while prebiotic fibers from whole foods support beneficial bacteria.

4. **Strain-specificity of probiotics**: EatingWell notes that probiotic-fortified processed foods aren't the same as clinically studied strains. This is a natural lane for Seed to own.

5. **Gut-brain axis connection**: Only Oshi Health and Healthline mention the gut-brain axis. This is an area where Seed can connect dietary choices to mood, cognition, and energy — cross-product relevance (PM-02 sleep, AM-02 focus).

6. **Recovery timeline**: No competitor addresses how long it takes for the microbiome to recover after eliminating harmful foods. This is a high-value question users have.

7. **The "healthy food" trap**: EatingWell's angle on kombucha and added-fiber products being counterproductive is underexplored. Seed can expand on this — many "gut health" marketed products are not evidence-based.

## Synthesis & Recommendations for Draft

### Recommended article angle:
**"The science behind why everyday foods quietly sabotage your gut — and what actually helps"**

Focus on the WHY (mechanisms) rather than just the WHAT (lists). Most competitors provide surface-level lists. Seed's differentiator should be explaining the microbiome science in accessible language — how emulsifiers thin the mucus layer, how artificial sweeteners confuse bacterial signaling, how ultra-processed foods starve beneficial microbes of the diverse substrates they need.

### Key differentiators for Seed's version:
1. Lead with the ultra-processed food story (strongest evidence, most relevant to Seed's positioning)
2. Include the "healthy food trap" angle (kombucha, added fiber, probiotic-fortified snacks)
3. Explain strain-specificity — not all probiotics are equal
4. Connect gut health to broader systemic health (gut-brain axis, immune function)
5. Cite Seed's own clinical research where relevant (DS-01 studies)
6. Emphasize the microbiome diversity angle throughout

### Structural recommendation:
- H2 sections organized by mechanism (inflammation, dysbiosis, barrier damage) rather than simple food lists
- Each section names the specific foods but explains the shared mechanism
- "Key Insight" section contrasts Seed's precision approach with the general "just avoid processed food" advice
- FAQs use the 4 selected PAA questions above
