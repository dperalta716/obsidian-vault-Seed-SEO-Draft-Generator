# Effect of Non-Nutritive Sweeteners on the Gut Microbiota

- **Source**: pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
- **Author**: Andrea Conz, Mario Salmona, Luisa Diomede
- **URL**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10144565/

## Abstract

The human gut microbiota consists of more than 1500 species distributed in more than 50 different phyla, with 99% of bacteria coming from about 30–40 species. The colon alone can harbor up to 100 trillion bacteria. With the widespread use of non-nutritive sweeteners (NNS) in the diet, recent investigations have focused on their effect on the gut microbiota as a mediator of potential impacts generated by gastrointestinal-related disturbances, such as insulin resistance, obesity, and inflammation. This review summarizes results from pre-clinical and clinical studies over the last ten years examining the single effects of the most consumed NNS: aspartame, acesulfame-K, sucralose, and saccharin.

**Keywords:** gut microbiota, non-nutritive sweetener, aspartame, acesulfame, sucralose, saccharin

**DOI**: https://doi.org/10.3390/nu15081869 | Published: Nutrients, 2023 Apr 13;15(8):1869

## Key Findings by Sweetener

### Aspartame
- Structurally a dipeptide methyl ester; almost completely broken down by GI peptidases
- As such, aspartame in its whole form cannot interact directly with colonic microbiota
- Animal studies show altered microbiota diversity (increases Enterobacteriaceae and Clostridium leptum), linked to impaired glucose metabolism
- Human studies show mixed results

### Acesulfame-K
- Absorbed almost entirely in the small intestine as an intact molecule
- More than 99% excreted in urinary tract within 24h; less than 1% eliminated in feces
- Rapid absorption causes negligible Ace-K concentration reaching fecal/colonic bacteria
- Improbable that this NNS could directly affect the colonic microbiota
- Some studies show changes in specific bacteria with prolonged high-dose intake

### Sucralose
- Low absorption level (less than 15%); more than 85% reaches the colon unchanged
- Contacts colonic microbiota extensively
- Animal studies: decrease in beneficial bifidobacteria, lactobacilli, and Clostridium clusters
- Clinical trials: potential to disrupt microbial populations associated with gut health
- "Maltodextrin carrier" nuance: powdered sucralose products often contain maltodextrin as carrier, which itself has prebiotic/microbiome effects

### Saccharin
- 85-95% absorbed as intact molecule; does not undergo gastrointestinal metabolism
- A small percentage of non-absorbed saccharin is excreted in feces—high concentrations could change intestinal microbial population
- Inhibitory effects on beneficial bacteria including Lactobacillus and Akkermansia muciniphila
- Human studies: can cause gut microbial imbalances linked to metabolic dysfunction

## Key Conclusion
Pre-clinical studies have given conflicting results. A dysbiotic effect of NNS was observed in some human trials, but many other RCTs reported a lack of significant impacts on gut microbiota composition. The scientific community has no unanimous consensus on appropriate outcomes and biomarkers.
