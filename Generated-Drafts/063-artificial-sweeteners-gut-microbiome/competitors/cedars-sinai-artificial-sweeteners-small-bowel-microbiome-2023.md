# RESEARCH ALERT: Artificial Sweeteners Significantly Alter the Small Bowel Microbiome

- **Source**: cedars-sinai.org
- **Author**: Ruchi Mathur, MD (lead author); newsroom summary

- **Scraped**: 2026-06-17

---

## Summary of Research

Cedars-Sinai investigators examined the potential effects of consuming non-sugar sweeteners (non-aspartame non-sugar sweeteners AND aspartame alone) on the composition of stool and duodenal microbiomes. Compared to controls, scientists noted significant differences in both stool and duodenal (small intestine) microbial diversity and composition. Circulating inflammatory markers were also altered.

**More than 140 million people in the United States use sugar substitutes.** Sucralose, aspartame, saccharin, and plant-based stevia extract are among the most popular.

## Method
- Small group of adults undergoing standard esophagogastroduodenoscopy
- Non-aspartame non-sugar sweeteners (NANS, N=35)
- Aspartame only (ASP, N=9)
- Controls (CON, N=55)
- Subset also provided stool samples (N=40)

## Key Findings
- Richness of bacteria in the small bowel was LESS in those consuming non-aspartame non-sugar sweeteners vs. controls
- Bacterial richness in aspartame consumers was similar to controls
- Cylindrospermopsin pathway (a toxin) was enriched specifically in small bowel bacteria of aspartame consumers — potentially harmful to liver and nervous system, classed as potential cancer-causing agent
- Effects differ in small bowel vs. large bowel
