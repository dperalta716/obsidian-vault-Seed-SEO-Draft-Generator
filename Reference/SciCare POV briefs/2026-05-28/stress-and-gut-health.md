## SciCare POV | Stress \+ Gut Health

Studies in adults show that [multiple forms of stress](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-77473-9)—perceived, physiological, and life event-based—are each associated with distinct shifts in gut microbiota composition. Higher stress levels are consistently linked to lower microbial diversity, elevated potentially pathogenic bacteria such as *Escherichia/Shigella*, and alterations in bacterial groups involved in immune and neurological signaling. In a cohort of predominantly healthy women experiencing stress, researchers observed [bacterial shifts](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcimb.2024.1324794) alongside alterations in neuroactive gut pathways. 

An RCT in adults found that a [psychobiotic diet](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41380-022-01817-y) high in prebiotic and fermented foods reduced perceived stress and supported microbial stability in a healthy adult population. A separate trial found supplementation [with heat killed *Enterococcus faecalis* EC-12 improved GI symptoms](https://doi.org/10.1163/18762891-bja00046) and altered gut metabolite composition in academically stressed students. These findings point to practical, diet-based strategies for supporting the gut through the demands of modern stress.

**Key Takeaway:** The psychological pressures of modern life can measurably shift gut microbiota composition and alter gut-brain signaling pathways—with dietary and microbiome-supportive interventions showing early promise as practical strategies for supporting gut stability under stress.

## Suggested References

### **1\. Biological, Environmental, and Psychological Stress and the Human Gut Microbiome in Healthy Adults**

Delgadillo DR et al. | Scientific Reports (2025)

* **Article Type:** Observational/comparative study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adults  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-77473-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-77473-9)

**Why it's useful:** This study examined how three different types of stress—perceived stress, major life events, and biological stress markers—each relate to gut microbiome composition in two separate groups of healthy adults. It found that different stress types are linked to distinct microbial shifts, suggesting the gut-stress connection is more nuanced than a single pathway.

### **2\. Investigations of Microbiota Composition and Neuroactive Pathways in Association with Symptoms of Stress and Depression in a Cohort of Healthy Women**

Bashir Z et al. | Frontiers in Cellular and Infection Microbiology (2024)

* **Article Type:** Cross-sectional study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Healthy women, ages 18–40  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fcimb.2024.1324794](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcimb.2024.1324794)

**Why it's useful:** This study looked at gut microbiome composition and functional neurotransmitter pathways in healthy women with varying levels of stress and depressive symptoms. It found that specific bacteria associated with stress also correlate with disruptions in pathways tied to dopamine, GABA, and serotonin production—connecting gut composition to mood-relevant signaling.

### **3\. Feed Your Microbes to Deal with Stress: A Psychobiotic Diet Impacts Microbial Stability and Perceived Stress in a Healthy Adult Population**

Berding K et al. | Molecular Psychiatry (2022)

* **Article Type:** Randomized controlled trial  
* **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adults  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41380-022-01817-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41380-022-01817-y)

**Why it's useful:** This trial tested whether a diet emphasizing prebiotic and fermented foods could shift the gut microbiome and reduce perceived stress in healthy adults over four weeks. Higher adherence to the diet correlated with greater stress reduction and gut microbial stability.

### **4\. Oral Supplementation of Heat-Killed Enterococcus faecalis Strain EC-12 Relieves Gastrointestinal Discomfort and Alters the Gut Microecology in Academically Stressed Students**

Li J et al. | Beneficial Microbes (2024)

* **Article Type:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial  
* **Population/Demographic:** Students under academic stress  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1163/18762891-bja00046](https://doi.org/10.1163/18762891-bja00046)

**Why it's useful:** This trial found that daily supplementation with heat-killed *Enterococcus faecalis* EC-12 reduced abdominal pain and stomach rumbling in students under academic stress compared to placebo. Shifts in gut metabolites—particularly increased tryptamine—were observed, offering a potential mechanistic link between the supplement and digestive relief under stress. Important to note that the strain was not live when administered, hence it doesn’t qualify as a probiotic.
