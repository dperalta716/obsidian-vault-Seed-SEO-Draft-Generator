## SciCare POV | Best Protein Powder for Gut Health

Among protein powder types studied for gut health, plant-based options—particularly pea protein—show the most favorable effects. [Pea protein](https://doi.org/10.20517/mrr.2025.77) selectively promotes beneficial bacteria including *Bifidobacterium* and *Faecalibacterium*, a key [butyrate producer](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41538-026-00785-9) that supports the gut lining. Plant-based proteins broadly are linked to greater [microbial diversity](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300555) and enrichment of bacteria involved in short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, compared to [animal-based sources](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2025.100582). Soy protein has similarly shown favorable effects, increasing [bacterial diversity](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2025.100582) and SCFA-producing activity in fermentation models.

Whey protein, the most widely used animal-based powder, has a mixed gut health profile. Its [bioactive components](https://doi.org/10.1002/fsn3.71487)—including lactoferrin and glycomacropeptide—carry antimicrobial and prebiotic-like properties that may support *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium*. When dietary carbohydrates are low, both animal and plant proteins can shift the gut toward more [proteolytic bacteria](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300555), raising ammonia and branched-chain fatty acids—byproducts linked to less favorable gut conditions. No protein powder type is considered overtly detrimental in typical amounts, but [animal-based sources](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300555) appear more likely to promote these [less favorable byproducts](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2025.100582) at higher intakes. Brown rice protein lacks adequate research to draw firm conclusions. The bulk of this research has been conducted *in vitro* or in animal models, so findings should be interpreted with caution until replicated in humans.

**Key Takeaway:** Pea protein has the strongest evidence for supporting beneficial gut bacteria, while whey protein offers mixed but potentially favorable effects through bioactive compounds—no powder type appears overtly harmful, though animal-based options may be less optimal for gut health when dietary fiber is low.

## Suggested References

### **1\. Disclosing the Effects of Pea-Derived Proteins on the Human Gut Microbiota**

Mancabelli L et al. | Microbiome Research Reports (2025)

* **Article Type:** *In vitro* laboratory study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Controlled lab samples using human fecal microbiota; not a human subject study  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.20517/mrr.2025.77](https://doi.org/10.20517/mrr.2025.77)

**Why it's useful:** This study examines how two commercial pea protein preparations affect gut bacteria using lab-based models seeded with human fecal samples. It identifies selective growth responses in beneficial bacteria like *Bifidobacterium*, supporting pea protein's potential as a gut-friendly option. It's one of the few studies to focus specifically on pea protein isolates and their microbiota-modulating effects.

### **2\. Gut Microbiome Modulation by Cricket, Pea, and Whey Protein Using the SHIME *In Vitro* Simulator**

Franciosa I et al. | npj Science of Food (2026)

* **Article Type:** *In vitro* laboratory study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Controlled lab samples using human fecal microbiota; not a human subject study  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41538-026-00785-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41538-026-00785-9)

**Why it's useful:** This study directly compares pea, whey, and cricket protein side-by-side on gut microbiota using a validated simulator of the human gut. It found that each protein type promoted a distinct bacterial profile, with pea protein favoring butyrate-producing bacteria like *Faecalibacterium*. The direct comparison makes it especially relevant for evaluating which protein powder may be most supportive of gut health.

### **3\. *In Vitro* Fermentation of Animal and Plant Protein Isolates by the Human Gut Microbiota Under High and Low Carbohydrate Conditions**

Behounek M et al. | Molecular Nutrition & Food Research (2024)

* **Article Type:** *In vitro* laboratory study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Controlled lab samples using human fecal microbiota; not a human subject study  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300555](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300555)

**Why it's useful:** This study fermented five protein isolates—beef, egg white, milk, pea, and soy—with human gut bacteria to observe how each affects microbial activity. It highlights how low-carbohydrate conditions shift the gut toward protein-degrading bacteria, producing less favorable byproducts like ammonia. It provides useful context for understanding how overall diet composition—not just protein source—shapes gut outcomes.

### **4\. Dietary Protein Source Shapes Gut Microbial Structure and Predicted Functional Potential: A Systematic Integrative Re-Analysis Using Machine Learning**

Adejumo SA et al. | Advances in Nutrition (2026)

* **Article Type:** Systematic review with machine learning re-analysis  
* **Population/Demographic:** Animal studies (murine)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2025.100582](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.advnut.2025.100582)

**Why it's useful:** This review re-analyzed sequencing data from multiple animal feeding studies to compare how plant- and animal-based protein diets shape gut microbial communities. It found that plant protein diets consistently increased microbial diversity and favored bacteria linked to beneficial fermentation, while animal protein promoted more proteolytic species. Though conducted in mice, the patterns identified provide useful mechanistic context for understanding protein type and gut health.

### **5\. Nexus of Whey Proteins, Gut Dysbiosis, and Colonic Health**

Ashaolu TJ et al. | Food Science & Nutrition (2026)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Mixed (*in vitro*, animal, and human studies)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/fsn3.71487](https://doi.org/10.1002/fsn3.71487)

**Why it's useful:** This review covers how specific bioactive compounds in whey protein—including lactoferrin and glycomacropeptide—interact with gut bacteria and may help restore microbial balance. It synthesizes evidence across lab, animal, and clinical studies, making it a broad overview of whey protein's nuanced gut health effects. It's particularly relevant for understanding why whey isn't straightforwardly beneficial or harmful.
