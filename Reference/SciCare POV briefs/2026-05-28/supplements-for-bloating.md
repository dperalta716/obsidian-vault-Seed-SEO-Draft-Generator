## SciCare POV | Supplements for Bloating

**Note:** Most of the studies behind these findings were conducted in people with IBS or functional dyspepsia, so results may not translate directly to occasional or mild bloating in otherwise healthy individuals.

Of the supplements studied for bloating, enteric-coated peppermint oil, probiotics, and ginger have the strongest overall evidence. [Peppermint oil](https://doi.org/10.1111/apt.17179) outperformed placebo for bloating and gut symptoms—though heartburn was a more common side effect. [Probiotics](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40001-025-02788-w) were associated with reduced bloating across multiple reviews, with *Bifidobacterium* and *Bacillus* strains showing the [strongest results](https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2023.07.018).

Ginger is shown to [speed up digestion](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.46061), easing post-meal pressure and [fullness](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12906-023-04227-x)—and carries fewer side effects than peppermint oil with no strain selection required. Fiber (especially [psyllium](https://doi.org/10.1136/gutjnl-2021-324784)) and [digestive enzymes](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopha.2023.115858) have solid evidence too, but are more narrow in scope—psyllium for fiber-triggered gas, enzymes for post-meal discomfort.

**Key Takeaway:** Enteric-coated peppermint oil, select probiotic strains (particularly *Bifidobacterium*), and ginger have the strongest overall evidence for reducing bloating, each through a different mechanism, although most of these studies were in people with IBS or functional dyspepsia.

## **Suggested References**

### **1\. Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis: Efficacy of Peppermint Oil in Irritable Bowel Syndrome**

Black CJ et al. | Alimentary Pharmacology & Therapeutics (2022)

* **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1111/apt.17179](https://doi.org/10.1111/apt.17179)

**Why it's useful:** This review pools data from multiple randomized controlled trials to evaluate peppermint oil's effect on gut symptoms. It finds that peppermint oil outperformed placebo for bloating and overall gut discomfort, while also noting that heartburn was a more common side effect—relevant context for anyone considering it as a supplement.

### **2\. Probiotics and Gastrointestinal Disorders: An Umbrella Meta-Analysis of Therapeutic Efficacy**

Zeng Q et al. | European Journal of Medical Research (2025)

* **Article Type:** Umbrella meta-analysis  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s40001-025-02788-w](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40001-025-02788-w)

**Why it's useful:** This review synthesizes findings across multiple systematic reviews to assess how well probiotics work for a range of gut symptoms, including bloating. It provides a broad look at the evidence and identifies which probiotic strains tend to perform best.

Goodoory VC et al. | Gastroenterology (2023)

* **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2023.07.018](https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2023.07.018)

**Why it's useful:** This review draws on a large body of randomized trials to evaluate probiotic efficacy for gut symptoms including bloating. It also highlights that strain type matters, with certain strains—particularly *Bifidobacterium*—showing stronger results than others.

### **4\. The Effect of Ginger Supplementation on the Improvement of Dyspeptic Symptoms in Patients With Functional Dyspepsia**

Aregawi LG et al. | Cureus (2023)

* **Article Type:** Open-label before-and-after clinical study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adult humans (functional dyspepsia patients)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.46061](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.46061)

**Why it's useful:** This study examines ginger's effect on post-meal symptoms like fullness, early satiety, and bloating over four weeks. It supports ginger's role in speeding up how quickly food leaves the stomach, which is the key mechanism behind its bloating relief.

Gunn D et al. | Gut (2022)

* **Article Type:** Randomized crossover trial with *in vitro* fermentation component  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adult humans (IBS patients) \+ controlled lab samples  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1136/gutjnl-2021-324784](https://doi.org/10.1136/gutjnl-2021-324784)

**Why it's useful:** This study looks at how psyllium interacts with other fermentable fibers to reduce gas production in the gut. It helps explain why psyllium is particularly useful for fiber-triggered bloating and supports its use as a gentler, gas-reducing fiber option.
