## SciCare POV | Probiotics for Histamine Intolerance

Histamine intolerance (HIT) develops when the body's ability to break down histamine is outpaced by its accumulation, primarily due to reduced activity of the intestinal enzyme [diamine oxidase](https://doi.org/10.3390/biom10081181). [Symptoms](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16081219) range from headaches and skin flushing to GI distress and nasal congestion, and researchers recognize HIT as fundamentally a [gut disorder](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu13041262). Beyond DAO deficiency, gut dysbiosis plays a role—certain bacteria can synthesize [histamine from histidine](https://doi.org/10.3390/metabo12100895), adding to the dietary histamine burden and worsening tolerance thresholds.

Research found that [individuals with HIT](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.1018463) tend to have more histamine-secreting bacteria in the gut, and that dietary strategies reducing histamine load were associated with a decrease in these bacteria alongside an increase in *Bifidobacterium* spp. This has opened interest in [microbiome-targeted approaches](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17152473), including probiotic supplementation, as a complement to low-histamine diets and DAO supplementation. Importantly, probiotic strain selection matters, and dedicated clinical trials on probiotics specifically for HIT remain limited, and current evidence is largely mechanistic and observational.

**Key Takeaway:** Histamine intolerance is linked to impaired histamine degradation and gut dysbiosis, with emerging evidence suggesting microbiome modulation—particularly reducing histamine-producing bacteria and favoring non-producing strains like *Bifidobacterium*—may support management, though dedicated probiotic clinical trials in HIT are still lacking.

## Suggested References

### **1\. Histamine Intolerance: The Current State of the Art**

Comas-Basté O et al. | Biomolecules (2020)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3390/biom10081181](https://doi.org/10.3390/biom10081181)

**Why it's useful:** This review covers the foundational understanding of histamine intolerance, including how DAO enzyme deficiency drives histamine accumulation and what diagnostic and treatment strategies currently exist. It also touches on probiotics as a keyword area, making it a relevant background for the microbiome angle of this topic.

### **2\. Histamine Intolerance: Symptoms, Diagnosis, and Beyond**

Jochum C | Nutrients (2024)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16081219](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16081219)

**Why it's useful:** This review provides an updated look at the causes, symptoms, and management of histamine intolerance, noting that symptoms extend beyond the GI tract. It's a useful reference for contextualizing the wide range of presentations associated with HIT.

### **3\. Histamine Intolerance Originates in the Gut**

Schnedl WJ & Enko D | Nutrients (2021)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3390/nu13041262](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu13041262)

**Why it's useful:** This review makes the case that HIT is fundamentally a gastrointestinal disorder, centered on DAO activity at the intestinal mucosa. It supports the framing that gut-level interventions—including microbiome modulation—are relevant to managing HIT.

### **4\. Histamine: A Mediator of Intestinal Disorders—A Review**

Smolinska S et al. | Metabolites (2022)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies; includes preclinical and animal study data  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3390/metabo12100895](https://doi.org/10.3390/metabo12100895)

**Why it's useful:** This review covers how gut bacteria can produce histamine from histidine, directly linking the gut microbiome to histamine load. It connects the microbial angle of HIT to broader GI disorders and is useful for understanding why strain selection in probiotics matters.

### **5\. The Dietary Treatment of Histamine Intolerance Reduces the Abundance of Some Histamine-Secreting Bacteria of the Gut Microbiota in Histamine Intolerant Women**

Sánchez-Pérez S et al. | Frontiers in Nutrition (2022)

* **Article Type:** Pilot observational study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human subjects (women diagnosed with histamine intolerance)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.1018463](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.1018463)

**Why it's useful:** This is the most directly relevant study to the topic—it observed that a low-histamine diet combined with DAO supplementation reduced histamine-secreting bacteria, including the genera *Proteus* and *Raoultella* in the gut. It provides the closest available clinical evidence linking microbiota changes to symptom improvement in HIT, though the sample size is small.

### **6\. Histamine Metabolism in IBD: Towards Precision Nutrition**

Kanta D et al. | Nutrients (2025)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies (IBD patients with histamine intolerance)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17152473](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17152473)

**Why it's useful:** This review proposes a dietary framework for managing histamine metabolism through microbiome modulation—specifically targeting histamine-producing bacteria. While focused on IBD, it directly addresses the same bacterial and enzymatic mechanisms relevant to HIT and supports the rationale for microbiome-targeted approaches.
