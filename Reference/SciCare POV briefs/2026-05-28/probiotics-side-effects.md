## SciCare POV | Probiotic Side Effects

Probiotics are generally well-tolerated in healthy adults, with most reported side effects being mild and gastrointestinal in nature. The most common complaints include temporary bloating, gas, loose stools, or constipation—particularly during the first few days of use as the gut adjusts. Clinical trials across various strains, including [*Lactiplantibacillus plantarum*](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu18091406), [*Bifidobacterium longum* subsp. *infantis*](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12602-026-11044-4), and [*Bacillus coagulans*](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.94845), consistently found no serious adverse events and no meaningful changes to blood markers, liver, or kidney function in otherwise healthy individuals.  A [multi-strain probiotic study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8352413/) in healthy adults found that while GI symptoms were the most commonly noted complaints, all participants completed the study without issue, and liver function markers shifted modestly—a finding researchers flagged as worth monitoring with further study.

Beyond short-term GI discomfort, two broader safety considerations exist. First, in individuals who are immunocompromised or critically ill, there are documented reports of *Lactobacillus* [bloodstream infections](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0022029925101155), making probiotic use in these populations a matter of clinical caution. Second, a concern at the population level involves antibiotic resistance: probiotic strains can, under certain conditions, acquire and potentially [transfer resistance genes](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2025.2541870) to other gut bacteria—an area researchers highlight as an important safety consideration that warrants ongoing regulatory attention. For healthy adults, these risks are considered low, but the field continues to call for strain-specific safety assessments before new products reach consumers.

**Key Takeaway:** For healthy adults, probiotic side effects are typically mild and short-lived—mainly temporary GI discomfort—while more serious risks like bloodstream infections or antibiotic resistance gene transfer are rare and primarily a concern in vulnerable or immunocompromised populations.
