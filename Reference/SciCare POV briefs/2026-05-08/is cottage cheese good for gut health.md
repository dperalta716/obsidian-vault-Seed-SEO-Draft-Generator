## SciCare POV | Cottage Cheese + Gut Health

Cottage cheese is an acid-curdled dairy product made through direct acidification, rennet addition, or lactic acid bacteria (LAB) starter cultures. While it can technically be a fermented dairy product when LAB cultures are used, cottage cheese is cooked after curd formation—a step that can eliminate the starter culture—and is therefore not typically considered a probiotic-rich food. Unlike more studied fermented dairy products such as yogurt or kefir, cottage cheese has not been extensively examined for its effects on gut health. Instead, the [existing research](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/pdf/10.1080/10408398.2025.2487682) largely focuses on cottage cheese in the context of nutrition, appetite, and metabolic health.

Some [newer research](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nutres.2020.06.017) points to "probiotic-enriched" cottage cheese—formulations where live cultures are added after heat processing—as a potential avenue for gut health benefits. One mouse study observed that such formulations induced changes in gut microbiota composition, and certain cottage cheese brands now use live cultures in the dressing (the creamy liquid component) to create a probiotic-rich product. However, there remains a significant gap in human research on cottage cheese and gut health specifically. Most health claims associated with cottage cheese are derived from its individual components—such as [casein, whey,](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jafc.2c03662) and calcium—rather than from studies on cottage cheese as a whole food.

**Key Takeaway:** Overall, cottage cheese is best understood as a nutritionally valuable dairy food with a well-established role in metabolic health and satiety. Its potential gut health benefits remain largely theoretical or indirect, extrapolated from its components or from analogy with more extensively studied fermented dairy products.

## Suggested References

### 1. Probiotic-Enriched Milk and Dairy Products Increase Gut Microbiota Diversity: A Comparative Study

Aljutaily T et al. | Nutrition Research (2020)

- **Article Type:** Preclinical animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse model
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nutres.2020.06.017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nutres.2020.06.017)

**Why it's useful:**
Examines the effect of adding probiotic cultures to dairy products—including cottage cheese made from different animal milks (e.g., cow, camel)—on microbial community composition in a mouse model. The study focuses purely on microbiota abundance and diversity rather than health outcomes, making it a useful early-stage reference for the hypothesis that probiotic-enriched cottage cheese may influence the gut microbiome, while underscoring the need for human research.

### 2. Cottage Cheese, a Relatively Underexplored Cultured Dairy Product with Potential Health Benefits?

Farsi E S.M. et al. | Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition (2025)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Both human + preclinical studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2025.2487682](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2025.2487682)

**Why it's useful:**
A recent review summarizing the landscape of research on cottage cheese across nutrition, metabolic health, and gut-related outcomes. Notably, the review highlights that most health claims associated with cottage cheese are indirect—derived from its individual components such as casein—rather than from studies examining cottage cheese as a complete food. Useful for contextualizing the current state of evidence and identifying gaps in the literature.

### 3. Production and Evaluation of Novel Functional Cream Cottage Cheese Fortified with Bovine Colostrum and Probiotic Bacteria

Abdeen D.N. et al. | Journal of Food Science and Technology (2024)

- **Article Type:** Preclinical *in vitro* study
- **Population/Demographic:** *In vitro* cell model
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1007/s13197-023-05910-0](https://doi.org/10.1007/s13197-023-05910-0)

**Why it's useful:**
Explores the fortification of cottage cheese with bovine colostrum and probiotic strains to assess effects on nutritional value. While results suggest increased enrichment of certain nutrients, the study is conducted entirely in vitro and is more focused on the influence of colostrum and palatability than on probiotic-specific or gut health outcomes. Useful as a proof-of-concept reference for functional cottage cheese formulations, but limited in its direct applicability to human gut health.
