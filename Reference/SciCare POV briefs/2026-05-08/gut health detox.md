## SciCare POV | Gut Health Detox

The body already has a built-in detoxification system (liver, kidneys, etc) that does a robust job on its own. "Detox" or "cleansing" have been co-opted as a wellness term to flush out toxins or reset the body. There is [no scientific evidence](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jhn.12286) that healthy individuals need special cleanses, juices, teas, or supplements to "remove toxins" or that these are more efficient than the body's natural detoxification systems.

[Researchers](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29958034/) have not found evidence that detoxes or colon cleansing improves health, and it may pose significant harm—such as, [electrolyte imbalances](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32672366/), [kidney injury](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29203127/), [liver injury](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36636365/)—and agencies like the FDA and FTC have [taken action](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/detoxes-and-cleanses-what-you-need-to-know) against certain detox products for false claims or unsafe ingredients. Interestingly, [studies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10265323/) show that gut cleanses disrupt the very microbial balance they claim to restore, and a healthy, diverse microbiome is actually what supports the body's natural detoxification processes. Diets rich in fiber, fermented foods, fruits, vegetables, and diverse plant foods are associated with healthier gut microbial diversity and intestinal function. Rather than "detoxing," the gut appears to function best when it is consistently nourished and supported.

**Key Takeaway:** The body continuously detoxes on its own, and evidence consistently suggests that commercial cleansing interventions neither enhance these processes nor remove toxins more effectively—and in some cases, pose serious harm and can actively disrupt the microbial diversity that supports them.

## Suggested References

### 1. Detox Diets for Toxin Elimination and Weight Management: A Critical Review of the Evidence

Klein AV & Kiat H | Journal of Human Nutrition and Dietetics (2015)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** General adult population; review of available human and animal studies
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1111/jhn.12286](https://doi.org/10.1111/jhn.12286)

**Why it's useful:**
A widely cited review examining whether detox diets are necessary, effective, or harmful. The authors found very little clinical evidence supporting the use of detox diets, noting that the few studies claiming benefit were hampered by flawed methodologies and small sample sizes. Importantly, the review distinguishes between whole-food dietary approaches (which have some evidence) and commercial detox products (which do not), making it a useful reference for nuanced discussion rather than blanket dismissal.

### 2. "Detoxes" and "Cleanses": What You Need to Know

National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health (updated 2025)

- **Article Type:** Evidence-based consumer health summary (federal agency)
- **Population/Demographic:** General public; synthesizes evidence across populations
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **URL:** [https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/detoxes-and-cleanses-what-you-need-to-know](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/detoxes-and-cleanses-what-you-need-to-know)

**Why it's useful:**
This is the NIH's official position on detoxes and cleanses. It confirms that existing human studies on detox programs are low quality, with design problems and small sample sizes. It also outlines specific safety risks—including serious adverse effects in vulnerable populations—and flags that the FDA has taken enforcement action against companies making false health claims about these products. This can be useful as an accessible, easily readable resource.

### 3. Clinical Effects of Colonic Cleansing for General Health Promotion: A Systematic Review

Acosta RD & Cash BD | American Family Physician / American Journal of Gastroenterology (2009/2010)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review
- **Population/Demographic:** General adult population; reviewed studies across diverse clinical settings
- **Open Access:** No (abstract publicly available; full text via AAFP subscription)
- **DOI/URL:** [https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2010/0201/p337.html](https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2010/0201/p337.html)

**Why it's useful:**
The authors found no methodologically rigorous controlled trials supporting colon cleansing for general health promotion, while identifying multiple case reports documenting serious adverse effects.

### 4. A Purported Detoxification Supplement Does Not Improve Body Composition, Waist Circumference, Blood Markers, or Gastrointestinal Symptoms in Healthy Adult Females

Tinsley G et al. | Journal of Dietary Supplements (2019)

- **Article Type:** Double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial (RCT)
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy young adult females (university setting, USA)
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1080/19390211.2018.1472713](https://doi.org/10.1080/19390211.2018.1472713)

**Why it's useful:**
One of the few RCTs directly testing a commercially sold detox supplement against placebo. The study found no significant improvements in any measured outcome (body composition, waist circumference, blood biomarkers, or GI symptoms) compared to the placebo group.

### 5. Drug-Induced Liver Injury from Herbal Liver Detoxification Tea

Niazi B et al. | Case Reports in Gastroenterology (2022)

- **Article Type:** Clinical case report
- **Population/Demographic:** 36-year-old healthy female; no prior liver disease or medication use
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1159/000526311](https://doi.org/10.1159/000526311)

**Why it's useful:**
This is a case report on a clinically significant acute liver injury in a previously healthy woman who consumed an over-the-counter herbal "liver detox tea" for one month. The product was marketed as supporting liver health, but ironically caused measurable liver damage. The authors note this is the first reported case of hepatotoxicity from the specific ingredient combination in the tea (burdock root, stinging nettle leaf, and others), highlighting how unregulated herbal products can cause organ injury in the very individuals trying to protect their health.

### 6. Safety and Effectiveness of Diet and Detox Teas for Weight Loss: A Mini-Review

Noor F et al. | Frontiers in Nutrition (2026)

- **Article Type:** Mini-review (peer-reviewed)
- **Population/Demographic:** General adult population; review of human, animal, and product studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2026.1777795](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2026.1777795)

**Why it's useful:**
The review finds limited support for weight loss benefits and raises concerns about safety, noting that many commercial detox teas contain stimulant compounds like caffeine or ephedra-like substances that may produce short-term effects but carry cardiovascular and metabolic risks. The review also notes the lack of human trials and studies—there was one uncontrolled human trial and one rat model study.
