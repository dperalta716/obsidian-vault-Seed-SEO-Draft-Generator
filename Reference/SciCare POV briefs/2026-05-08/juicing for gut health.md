---

Juicing can increase intake of certain vitamins, minerals, and polyphenols from fruits and vegetables, but whether it supports gut health is a bit more nuanced. While juice-based diets have grown in popularity, research on their overall health and microbiome-related effects remains limited. One key consideration is that juicing often removes much of the naturally occurring fiber—particularly insoluble fiber—which plays an important role in digestive health, stool regularity, and feeding beneficial gut microbes. Because of this, whole fruits and vegetables generally provide more comprehensive gut-supportive benefits than juice alone.

Fruit juices can also contain concentrated amounts of naturally occurring sugars without the buffering effect of fiber, which may contribute to more rapid glucose spikes compared to eating whole produce. For some individuals, large amounts of juice may also lead to occasional gastrointestinal discomfort depending on the ingredients used and individual digestive sensitivity.

**Key Takeaway:** Juicing retains vitamins and polyphenols but removes the fiber that feeds beneficial gut bacteria and buffers blood sugar, leaving concentrated natural sugars that can cause more rapid glucose spikes than eating whole fruits and vegetables.

## Suggested References

### 1. Effects of Vegetable and Fruit Juicing on Gut and Oral Microbiome Composition

Sardaro MLS et al. | Nutrients (2025)

- **Article Type:** Clinical study
- **Population/Demographic:** Human trials
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11820471/](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11820471/)

**Why it's useful:**
While juice-based diets can provide vitamins and plant compounds, removing fiber may reduce some gut-supportive benefits and may contribute to quicker blood sugar spikes. The researchers also found no major changes in overall gut microbiome composition after either the elimination diet or juice intervention diets, highlighting how individualized microbiome responses can be.

### 2. Health Benefit of Vegetable/Fruit Juice-Based Diet: Role of Microbiome

Henning SM et al. | Scientific Reports (2017)

- **Article Type:** Clinical study
- **Population/Demographic:** Human trials (n=20 healthy adults)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5438379/](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5438379/)

**Why it's useful:**
In a small study, 20 healthy adults followed a 3-day juice-only diet, which was associated with short-term weight loss, temporary shifts in gut microbiome composition, and changes in markers linked to blood flow and oxidative stress. Some of these effects persisted after participants returned to their usual diet, though additional long-term research is needed.

### 4. An Exploratory Study on the Effect of Daily Fruits and Vegetable Juice on Human Gut Microbiota

Choi YJ et al. | Food Science and Biotechnology (2018)

- **Article Type:** Exploratory study
- **Population/Demographic:** Human trials (small sample)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI:** [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6170287/](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6170287/)

**Why it's useful:**
Small study observed a modest increase in diversity of the host fecal microbiota after 3 days of fruit and vegetable juice intake. Note: study was funded by Hurom, a juicing company, which should be considered when interpreting the findings.
