## SciCare POV | Oregano Oil + Gut Health

**Important:** Oregano oil's reported effects are primarily attributed to two compounds: carvacrol and thymol. However, it's important to note from the outset that the vast majority of research on oregano oil has been conducted in laboratory and animal models, not in humans. While these studies provide valuable insights into potential mechanisms and biological activity, their findings do not necessarily translate directly to human health outcomes. Well-designed human clinical trials are still needed to determine whether the benefits observed in preclinical research are clinically meaningful. This limitation should be clearly acknowledged whenever discussing or citing animal-based findings throughout the presentation.

A [systematic review](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.phymed.2025.156630) of animal studies found that these compounds reduce gut inflammation and oxidative stress and help regulate the gut microbiota. Their best-documented action is antimicrobial: in mice carrying a human gut microbiota, carvacrol [lowered loads](https://doi.org/10.3390/biom13020320) of the foodborne pathogen *Campylobacter* and eased intestinal inflammation and bloody diarrhea. In another animal model, carvacrol reduced [intestinal injury](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-38577-w) and inflammation triggered by a bacterial toxin while shifting gut bacteria toward a more balanced composition.

Oregano compounds may also support the gut lining and beneficial microbes. In mice, carvacrol strengthened the [intestinal barrier](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tranon.2025.102431) by restoring tight-junction proteins and increased beneficial bacteria such as *Lactobacillus*. In poultry, oregano oil [increased the richness](https://doi.org/10.3390/ani13162550) and altered the makeup of gut bacteria and improved the structure of the [gut wall](https://doi.org/10.3390/ani14223235), and blends containing carvacrol and thymol increased bacteria that produce [short-chain fatty acids](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psj.2025.105911)—compounds that nourish the gut lining. Concentrated oregano oil is potent, and because its antimicrobial action is not limited to harmful bacteria, it can also reduce beneficial species. This in mind, oregano oil's broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity is an important consideration. If you are also taking a probiotic, it may be beneficial to separate their intake to help minimize potential interference.

**Key Takeaway:** Oregano oil's active compounds, carvacrol and thymol, show antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and gut-barrier benefits in animal and lab studies, but human evidence is lacking, so its value for everyday gut health in people remains unproven.

## Suggested References

### 1. Dietary Essential Oil Components: A Systematic Review of Preclinical Studies on the Management of Gastrointestinal Diseases

Gopalsamy RG et al. | Phytomedicine (2025)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review (preclinical/animal studies)
- **Population/Demographic:** Animal studies
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.phymed.2025.156630

**Why it's useful:** This review pulls together animal research on plant essential oil compounds, including carvacrol and thymol from oregano, and how they affect the gut. It reports that these compounds tend to lower gut inflammation and oxidative stress and help balance the microbiota, while noting that human studies are still needed.

---

### 2. Therapeutic Oral Application of Carvacrol Alleviates Acute Campylobacteriosis in Mice Harboring a Human Gut Microbiota

Foote MS et al. | Biomolecules (2023)

- **Article Type:** Placebo-controlled preclinical intervention study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse model (mice carrying a human gut microbiota)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/biom13020320

**Why it's useful:** This study tested carvacrol, oregano oil's main active compound, in mice infected with a common foodborne gut pathogen. It found that carvacrol lowered the pathogen load and eased intestinal inflammation and diarrhea, pointing to an antimicrobial benefit in the gut.

---

### 3. Carvacrol Attenuated Lipopolysaccharide-Induced Intestinal Injury by Down-Regulating TLRs Gene Expression and Regulating the Gut Microbiota in Rabbit

Wu D et al. | Scientific Reports (2023)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Rabbit model
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-023-38577-w

**Why it's useful:** This study looked at whether carvacrol could protect the gut after a bacterial toxin challenge in rabbits. It showed reduced intestinal injury and inflammation along with a healthier balance of gut bacteria, supporting carvacrol's anti-inflammatory and microbiota effects.

---

### 4. Carvacrol Attenuates Mucosal Barrier Impairment and Tumorigenesis by Regulating Gut Microbiome

Fan Y et al. | Translational Oncology (2025)

- **Article Type:** Preclinical experimental study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse model
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tranon.2025.102431

**Why it's useful:** This study examined how carvacrol affects the gut lining in mouse models of colitis. It found that carvacrol helped restore the intestinal barrier and tight-junction proteins and increased beneficial bacteria such as *Lactobacillus*, suggesting support for gut barrier health.

### 5. Effect of *Bacillus subtilis* and Oregano Oil on Performance, Gut Microbiome, and Intestinal Morphology in Pullets

Kim HJ et al. | Animals (2023)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal feeding study
- **Population/Demographic:** Poultry (pullets)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/ani13162550

**Why it's useful:** This study fed oregano oil to young hens and tracked changes in their gut. It found greater microbial richness and improved gut wall structure, which is why it's included as evidence that oregano oil can shift the gut microbiota in animals.

Xian L et al. | Animals (2024)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal feeding study
- **Population/Demographic:** Poultry (laying hens)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/ani14223235

**Why it's useful:** This study added oregano oil to laying hens' diets and measured effects on the gut. It showed shifts in gut bacteria and improvements in gut wall structure, reinforcing oregano oil's ability to alter the microbiota in animal models.
