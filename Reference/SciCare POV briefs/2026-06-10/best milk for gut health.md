## SciCare POV | Best Milk for Gut Health

When it comes to the gut microbiome, fermented dairy relatively has greater evidence for support compared to other milk types. Particularly, yogurt and kefir contain fermentation bacteria tied to [helpful microbiota shifts](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00284-025-04061-z), though whether enough stay alive to act as true probiotics is uncertain and [human kefir trials](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac054) remain small. In people who cannot digest lactose, the undigested sugar reaches the colon, where [bacteria ferment it](https://doi.org/10.1136/gutjnl-2019-318404) into gas and bloating, and the lactose dose, lactase expression, and individual microbiome shapes how severe symptoms are. Regular cow's milk also comes in two protein types (A1 and A2), and during digestion, A1 beta-casein releases a fragment ([beta-casomorphin-7](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10068-023-01428-5)) that A2 lacks. Because A1 may lead to increased transit time and inflammation in some individuals, [A2-only milk](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxaa454) can often be a gentler and better tolerated option for some, though it contains the same lactose.

Among non-dairy milks, the beta-glucan that makes whole oats [lower blood sugar](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41430-021-00875-9) and feed [butyrate-producing bacteria](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodres.2024.115153) is largely [stripped out](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40643-026-01007-6) when oats become oat milk, so oat milk offers little of that benefit and can often raise blood sugar. Soy milk's isoflavone, daidzein, is converted by gut bacteria into equol. A study found that [equol producers](https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqac252) tend to have more diverse microbiomes, and findings suggest there to be a beneficial association between equol and obesity, mediated by gut microbiota. [Although human studies are lacking, almond milk](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.13415) was observed to shift gut bacteria differently from cow's milk and soy in an animal study. Overall, plant-based milks/beverages, including those derived from coconut and macadamia nuts, are unstudied. Additives, including emulsifiers, oftentimes included in these beverages, also differ in effect. For example, the emulsifier [carboxymethylcellulose](https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2021.11.006) lowered microbial diversity and short-chain fatty acids in a human trial, and [ultra-processed food reviews](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17223567) tie heavy emulsifier and sweetener intake to microbiota and gut-barrier changes. However, this warrants further research, and consumption in moderation and individual constitution is key.

**Key Takeaway:** For the gut microbiome, fermented dairy has the strongest support though lactose can trigger digestive symptoms in people who are intolerant, while plant-based and alternative milks show varying effects that warrant further human study—so the best choice ultimately depends on individual tolerance and preference.

## Suggested References

### 1. Impact of Fermented Milk on Gut Microbiota and Human Health: A Comprehensive Review

Abd El-Salam MH et al. | Current Microbiology (2025)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s00284-025-04061-z

**Why it's useful:** This review summarizes how fermented milks like yogurt and kefir affect the gut microbiota and broader health. It anchors the point that fermented dairy is associated with helpful shifts in gut bacteria.

---

### 2. The Effects of Kefir Consumption on Human Health: A Systematic Review of Randomized Controlled Trials

Kairey L et al. | Nutrition Reviews (2023)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review of randomized controlled trials
- **Population/Demographic:** Adult humans
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac054

**Why it's useful:** This systematic review pools human trials of kefir. It supports that kefir is generally safe for healthy adults and shows early signals for gut and oral bacteria, while noting the trials are small and limited.

---

### 3. Update on Lactose Malabsorption and Intolerance: Pathogenesis, Diagnosis and Clinical Management

Misselwitz B et al. | Gut (2019)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Humans
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1136/gutjnl-2019-318404

**Why it's useful:** This review explains how undigested lactose travels to the colon and is fermented by gut bacteria, producing gas and bloating. It also notes that lactose dose, lactase expression, and the individual microbiome shape how strong symptoms are.

---

### 4. A2 Milk Consumption and Its Health Benefits: An Update

Jeong H et al. | Food Science and Biotechnology (2023)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10068-023-01428-5

**Why it's useful:** This update reviews A2 versus A1 milk and digestion. It supports that A1's beta-casomorphin-7 fragment is tied to discomfort and that A2 milk is often easier to digest for sensitive people.

---

### 5. Beneficial Effects of Milk Having A2 β-Casein Protein: Myth or Reality?

Kay SS et al. | The Journal of Nutrition (2021)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxaa454

**Why it's useful:** This review weighs the evidence on A2 beta-casein. It connects A1 beta-casein to GI discomfort and inflammation, supporting why A2-only milk can be gentler for certain individuals.

---

### 6. The Effect of Oat β-Glucan on Postprandial Blood Glucose and Insulin Responses: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Zurbau A et al. | European Journal of Clinical Nutrition (2021)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Population/Demographic:** Humans (with and without diabetes)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41430-021-00875-9

**Why it's useful:** This meta-analysis of human trials shows oat beta-glucan blunts the rise in blood sugar and insulin after meals. It establishes the blood-sugar benefit of the fiber that is largely lost in oat milk.

---

### 7. Comparative Study on Static and Dynamic Digest Characteristics of Oat β-Glucan and β-Gluco-Oligosaccharides

Ni J et al. | Food Research International (2024)

- **Article Type:** In vitro fermentation comparative study
- **Population/Demographic:** Controlled lab samples (human fecal microbiota); not a human-subject trial
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodres.2024.115153

**Why it's useful:** This lab study shows oat beta-glucan is fermented by gut bacteria into short-chain fatty acids like butyrate. It supports the fiber's gut benefit in whole oats.

---

### 8. Exploring the Utilization of Oat By-Products in the Circular Bioeconomy: The Potential for the Development of Extruded Snacks Enriched with β-Glucan

Lin CR et al. | Bioresources and Bioprocessing (2026)

- **Article Type:** Food-processing experimental study
- **Population/Demographic:** Controlled lab/food samples; not a human-subject study
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s40643-026-01007-6

**Why it's useful:** This study notes that beta-glucan-rich material is separated off as a by-product during oat-milk production, and that adding it back lowers a food's glycemic index. It supports the point that oat milk loses much of the fiber and can raise blood sugar.

---

### 9. Gut Microbiota and Acylcarnitine Metabolites Connect the Beneficial Association Between Equol and Adiposity in Adults: A Prospective Cohort Study

Wu YY et al. | The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition (2022)

- **Article Type:** Prospective cohort study
- **Population/Demographic:** Adults
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/nqac252

**Why it's useful:** This adult study links the gut-derived soy metabolite equol to a richer, more diverse microbiome and to a beneficial association with body fat. It supports that gut bacteria convert soy's daidzein into equol and that equol status tracks with microbiome diversity.

---

### 10. The Impacts of Bovine Milk, Soy Beverage, or Almond Beverage on the Growing Rat Microbiome

Cakebread J et al. | PeerJ (2022)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Rat model
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.13415

**Why it's useful:** This rat study compared cow's milk, soy, and almond beverages and found each shifted gut bacteria differently. It's the basis for noting almond milk's distinct microbiome effect and the lack of direct human comparisons.

---

### 11. Randomized Controlled-Feeding Study of Dietary Emulsifier Carboxymethylcellulose Reveals Detrimental Impacts on the Gut Microbiota and Metabolome

Chassaing B et al. | Gastroenterology (2022)

- **Article Type:** Double-blind randomized controlled-feeding trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adults
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2021.11.006

**Why it's useful:** This double-blind human trial tested the emulsifier carboxymethylcellulose. It found reduced gut bacterial diversity and short-chain fatty acids plus some abdominal discomfort, anchoring the caution about heavy emulsifier intake.

---

### 12. Ultra-Processed Food Consumption and Irritable Bowel Syndrome: Current Evidence and Clinical Implications

Dale HF et al. | Nutrients (2025)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Humans
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17223567

**Why it's useful:** This review examines ultra-processed foods and gut symptoms. It connects emulsifiers and non-nutritive sweeteners to changes in the microbiota and gut barrier, supporting the additives discussion.
