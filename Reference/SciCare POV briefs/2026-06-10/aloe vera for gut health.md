## SciCare POV | Aloe + Gut Health

Aloe vera gel's [polysaccharides](https://doi.org/10.3390/polysaccharides6020036), primarily acemannan and glucomannan, support gut health through several pathways. These compounds function as prebiotics, promoting [beneficial gut bacteria](https://doi.org/10.3390/polysaccharides6020036) and supporting [production of short-chain](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodres.2025.116675) fatty acids—compounds that fuel cells lining the gut and help regulate inflammation. They also reinforce the intestinal barrier by upregulating [tight junction proteins](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms22126515) and supporting [intestinal stem cell](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodres.2025.116675) renewal and repair. In people with IBS, aloe vera [improved symptom scores](https://doi.org/10.5056/jnm18077) compared to placebo in a meta-analysis of clinical trials, with benefits most apparent in short-term use.

These effects are specific to the inner gel; the outer leaf is a different matter. [Aloin](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2019.00474), found in the outer leaf latex, is metabolized by gut bacteria into compounds that drive its laxative effect—but at higher concentrations, it decreases [butyrate-producing bacteria](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2019.00474) and disrupts gut microbiota balance. Chronic or high-dose consumption of whole-leaf extract has been linked to [intestinal tissue changes](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2019.00474) in animal studies, a possible carcinogenicity classification by the International Agency for Research on Cancer, and [hepatotoxicity in](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11033-022-07176-9) case reports. Decolorized products that remove the latex fraction are considered [lower risk](https://doi.org/10.3390/polysaccharides6020036), but until larger human trials confirm aloe vera's gut-protective effects, it would not typically be considered a [first-line option](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11033-022-07176-9) for gut health support.

**Key Takeaway:** Aloe vera gel polysaccharides support gut health through prebiotic activity and intestinal barrier reinforcement, but aloin from the outer leaf can disrupt gut bacteria and raises safety concerns at higher doses or with whole-leaf products—making product form and dose important considerations.

## Suggested References

### 1. Aloe Vera Polysaccharides as Therapeutic Agents: Benefits Versus Side Effects in Biomedical Applications

Matei CE | Polysaccharides (2025)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human, animal, and *in vitro* studies reviewed (mixed)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/polysaccharides6020036

**Why it's useful:** This review examines the therapeutic potential and safety profile of aloe vera polysaccharides across biomedical applications. It covers the prebiotic and anti-inflammatory properties of acemannan and glucomannan, as well as documented concerns around long-term use and various aloe vera preparation forms.

---

### 2. Polysaccharide from *Aloe vera* Gel Improves Intestinal Stem Cells Dysfunction to Alleviate Intestinal Barrier Damage via 5-HT

Zhang D et al. | Food Research International (2025)

- **Article Type:** *In vivo* and *in vitro* experimental study
- **Population/Demographic:** Animal model + *in vitro* cells
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodres.2025.116675

**Why it's useful:** This study examines how aloe vera gel polysaccharides protect the gut lining by supporting intestinal stem cell function through serotonin (5-HT) signaling. It provides mechanistic insight into how aloe vera may help maintain and repair the intestinal barrier at a cellular level.

---

### 3. The Role of Processed *Aloe vera* Gel in Intestinal Tight Junction: An *In Vivo* and *In Vitro* Study

Phan THL et al. | International Journal of Molecular Sciences (2021)

- **Article Type:** *In vivo* and *in vitro* experimental study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse model + *in vitro* cells
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms22126515

**Why it's useful:** This study investigates how processed aloe vera gel affects the tight junction proteins that control what passes through the intestinal wall. It found that aloe gel improved intestinal permeability in aging mice, directly supporting its role in gut barrier function.

---

### 4. *Aloe vera* Is Effective and Safe in Short-Term Treatment of Irritable Bowel Syndrome: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Hong SW et al. | Journal of Neurogastroenterology and Motility (2018)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Population/Demographic:** Human (IBS patients)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.5056/jnm18077

**Why it's useful:** This is the only published meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials evaluating aloe vera specifically for IBS symptoms, making it the primary source of human clinical evidence on this topic. It found that aloe vera outperformed placebo for symptom improvement in short-term use, with no significant adverse events reported across the included trials. Albeit, these are all in patients with IBS.

### 5. Dose-Dependent Effects of Aloin on the Intestinal Bacterial Community Structure, Short Chain Fatty Acids Metabolism and Intestinal Epithelial Cell Permeability

Gokulan K et al. | Frontiers in Microbiology (2019)

- **Article Type:** *In vitro* experimental study
- **Population/Demographic:** Controlled lab samples; not a human subject study
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2019.00474

**Why it's useful:** This study looks specifically at how aloin—the compound found in the outer leaf latex of aloe vera—affects gut bacteria and intestinal permeability. It found that higher concentrations reduce butyrate-producing bacteria and disrupt microbiota balance, touching on the potential safety concerns around whole-leaf aloe vera products.

### 6. The Dark Side of Miracle Plant — Aloe Vera: A Review

Jangra A et al. | Molecular Biology Reports (2022)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human, animal, and *in vitro* studies reviewed (mixed)
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11033-022-07176-9

**Why it's useful:** This review addresses the adverse effects and safety concerns associated with aloe vera, covering hepatotoxicity, potential carcinogenicity, and other risks documented in the literature. It serves as a direct counterpoint to the commonly cited benefits and is central to the downsides discussed in the summary.
