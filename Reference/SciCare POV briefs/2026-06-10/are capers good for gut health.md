## SciCare POV | Capers + Gut Health

Capers (*Capparis spinosa*) are among the richest [natural dietary sources](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodchem.2022.135258) of rutin, a polyphenol flavonoid, and also contain quercetin, [phenolic acids](https://doi.org/10.3390/molecules30183705), glucosinolates, and polysaccharides. These compounds give capers [antioxidant and anti-inflammatory](https://doi.org/10.1002/cbdv.202200300) properties that may benefit the gut. In animal research, a [caper extract](https://doi.org/10.1155/2021/1227876) reduced colonic inflammation, lowered pro-inflammatory signals, restored gut barrier proteins, and increased the diversity and abundance of beneficial bacteria. Quercetin has also shown in lab research to improve gut barrier function by enhancing [tight junction proteins](https://doi.org/10.3390/ph16111511), and rutin has been proposed as a prebiotic-type polyphenol—though a 12-week human trial on isolated rutin did not confirm significant shifts in [gut bacteria composition](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu15153360).

A notable downside is sodium: capers are commercially brined or salt-cured, and research shows [high-salt diets](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu14061171) deplete beneficial gut bacteria—including *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium*—while enriching potentially harmful strains. Rinsing capers before use can substantially reduce sodium intake. Capers also contain [glucosinolates](https://doi.org/10.3390/molecules30183705), compounds shared with mustard and broccoli, which can occasionally cause [digestive discomfort](https://doi.org/10.1002/cbdv.202200300) or mild allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Overall, capers hold genuine gut health promise through their polyphenol content, but no studies have examined capers as a food in human gut health trials. The bulk of the evidence on capers and gut health has been generated in animal models and cell-based studies, and more controlled research in humans is needed before firm conclusions can be drawn.

**Key Takeaway:** Capers are exceptionally rich in rutin and quercetin, polyphenols with real anti-inflammatory and gut-supportive potential, but their high sodium content from processing may partially offset these benefits, and direct human evidence remains limited.

**NOTE: Quercetin is present in Co-Biotics so we should be careful about making any claims on quercetin.**

## Suggested References

### 1. *Capparis Spinosa* L. as a Potential Source of Nutrition and Its Health Benefits in Foods: A Comprehensive Review of Its Phytochemistry, Bioactivities, Safety, and Application

Sun Y et al. | Food Chemistry (2023)

- **Article Type:** Comprehensive narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Mixed (animal, *in vitro*, and human studies)
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodchem.2022.135258

**Why it's useful:** This review covers the full nutritional and phytochemical profile of *Capparis spinosa* (capers), summarizing the evidence behind its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and other health-promoting properties. It provides an overview of the key bioactive compounds in capers—including flavonoids, phenolic acids, and polysaccharides—and how they may support health. It also addresses safety considerations and food applications, giving useful context for understanding capers beyond just one isolated benefit.

Alsharif B and Boylan F | Molecules (2025)

- **Article Type:** Scoping review
- **Population/Demographic:** Mixed (animal, *in vitro*, and human studies)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/molecules30183705

**Why it's useful:** This scoping review maps available research on the *Capparis* genus, identifying the key compounds found in capers—like rutin, quercetin, and glucosinolates—and summarizing what's known about their anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant effects. It's a useful reference for understanding how capers' phytochemical profile relates to gut and overall health.

### 4. *Capparis Spinosa* Alleviates DSS-Induced Ulcerative Colitis via Regulation of the Gut Microbiota and Oxidative Stress

Zhu X et al. | Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine (2021)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse model (DSS-induced ulcerative colitis)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1155/2021/1227876

**Why it's useful:** This study is the most direct evidence for capers' effects on the gut, testing a caper water extract in mice with chemically induced gut inflammation. It found that the extract reduced inflammatory signals, restored gut barrier proteins, and increased beneficial bacteria diversity—providing a mechanism for how capers may support gut health. However, these results are from an animal model of colitis and may not directly apply to humans.

---

### 5. Effect of a 12-Week Polyphenol Rutin Intervention on Markers of Pancreatic β-Cell Function and Gut Microbiota in Adults with Overweight without Diabetes

Mathrani A et al. | Nutrients (2023)

- **Article Type:** Double-blind randomized controlled trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Adult humans (adults with overweight, ages 18–65)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu15153360

**Why it's useful:** This trial tested rutin—capers' most abundant flavonoid—as a supplement in adults to see whether it shifted gut bacteria composition. While significant gut microbiota changes were not observed, fasting blood sugar negatively correlated with the abundance of a beneficial bacterium, pointing to a more nuanced relationship between polyphenols and the gut microbiome.

---

### 6. High-Salt Diet Induces Depletion of Lactic Acid-Producing Bacteria in Murine Gut

Hamad I et al. | Nutrients (2022)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse model
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu14061171

**Why it's useful:** This study examined how different levels of dietary salt affect gut bacteria in mice, finding that high-salt diets depleted beneficial bacteria—including *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium*—in a dose-dependent way. It's directly relevant to the downside discussion around capers, since commercial capers are heavily salt-cured or brined, and high sodium intake may partially counteract the gut benefits their polyphenols offer.

### 7. Influences of Yogurt with Functional Ingredients from Various Sources That Help Treat Leaky Gut on Intestinal Barrier Dysfunction in Caco-2 Cells

Aleman RS et al. | Pharmaceuticals (2023)

- **Article Type:** *In vitro* experimental study
- **Population/Demographic:** *In vitro* (Caco-2 human intestinal cells)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/ph16111511

**Why it's useful:** This cell-based study tested a range of gut-supportive ingredients—including quercetin—in yogurt formulations applied to human intestinal cells under inflammatory conditions. Quercetin was among the ingredients associated with improved gut barrier integrity and tight junction protein expression, lending support to its relevance as one of capers' key compounds for gut lining health.
