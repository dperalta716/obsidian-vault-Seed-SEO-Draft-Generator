## SciCare POV | Gut Health Testing

Microbiome testing is [not currently recommended](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253(24)00311-X) for routine or self-directed use. An international panel of experts found insufficient evidence to support it for most people and discourages testing without guidance from a licensed healthcare provider. At-home microbiome tests have grown in popularity, but a recent evaluation found [major inconsistencies](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42003-025-09301-3) both between and within providers—with results varying as much as they would between two entirely different people. Many of these tests report metrics that sound meaningful, like bacterial "balance scores," but researchers have flagged these as [not clinically meaningful](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253(24)00311-X).

Both at-home and most clinician-ordered microbiome tests rely on stool samples, which only capture part of the gut's bacterial landscape—a significant portion of bacteria deeper in the colon [do not appear in stool](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.heliyon.2023.e13602) at all, and many compounds they produce are missed entirely. Clinicians can collect samples from inside the colon or from the gut lining for a more complete picture, but research shows [each sample type captures a different picture](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12876-021-01975-3) and none fully reflects the whole gut environment. Microbiome testing is [not a standalone diagnostic](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253(24)00311-X); it is intended to complement standard clinical tests like bloodwork or endoscopy. It may, however, provide useful supporting information for conditions like [inflammatory bowel disease](https://doi.org/10.1002/ueg2.12338) (IBD), recurring gut infections like *Clostridioides difficile*, liver disease, and metabolic conditions.

**Key Takeaway:** Routine microbiome testing is not recommended for self-directed use, and most at-home microbiome tests lack the reliability and clinical context to be meaningful. When ordered by a healthcare provider, microbiome testing *may* be a complement to clinical testing to inform diagnosis + monitoring for certain conditions, but even the best available methods still only capture a snapshot of the gut microbiome.

## Suggested References

### **1. International Consensus Statement on Microbiome Testing in Clinical Practice**

Porcari S et al. | Lancet Gastroenterology & Hepatology (2025)

* **Article Type:** Consensus statement  
* **Population/Demographic:** Clinical practice guidelines; not a single study population  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253(24)00311-X](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-1253(24)00311-X)

**Why it's useful:** This consensus statement covers recommendations from an international expert panel on when microbiome testing is and isn't appropriate in clinical practice. Concludes there isn't enough evidence for routine use and discourages direct-to-consumer testing, while noting specific conditions where provider-ordered testing may be useful.

---

### **2. Evaluating the Analytical Performance of Direct-to-Consumer Gut Microbiome Testing Services**

Jackson SA et al. | Communications Biology (2026)

* **Article Type:** Analytical performance evaluation  
* **Population/Demographic:** Controlled lab samples; not a human subject study  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s42003-025-09301-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42003-025-09301-3)

**Why it's useful:** This study evaluates seven at-home microbiome testing services using standardized fecal samples prepared in a lab setting. Finds major inconsistencies both within and across providers—with results varying as much as they would between two different people—pointing to reliability concerns with commercial DTC tests.

---

### **3. The Gut Microbiome–Does Stool Represent Right?**

Levitan O et al. | Heliyon (2023)

* **Article Type:** Observational/comparative study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Healthy human adults  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.heliyon.2023.e13602](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.heliyon.2023.e13602)

**Why it's useful:** This study compares stool samples to samples collected from different regions inside the colon, finding that a significant portion of bacteria and the compounds they produce simply don't show up in stool. Supports the limitation that stool gives an incomplete picture of what's actually happening in the gut.

---

### **4. Comparison of Sampling Methods in Assessing the Microbiome From Patients With Ulcerative Colitis**

Kim D et al. | BMC Gastroenterology (2021)

* **Article Type:** Observational/comparative study  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human adults with ulcerative colitis and healthy controls  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s12876-021-01975-3](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12876-021-01975-3)

**Why it's useful:** This study looks at three different sample types—stool, fluid from inside the colon, and tissue from the gut lining—collected from the same patients. Finds each captures a different microbial picture, reinforcing that no single sampling method tells the whole story.

---

### **5. The Role of Gut Microbiome in Inflammatory Bowel Disease Diagnosis and Prognosis**

Zheng J et al. | United European Gastroenterology Journal (2022)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies (IBD patients)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1002/ueg2.12338](https://doi.org/10.1002/ueg2.12338)

**Why it's useful:** This review covers how shifts in gut bacteria relate to IBD diagnosis, disease activity, and treatment outcomes. Relevant to the point that microbiome testing, when used alongside standard diagnostics, may offer additional supporting information for conditions like Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.
