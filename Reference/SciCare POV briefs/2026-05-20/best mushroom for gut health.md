## SciCare POV | Best Mushrooms for Gut Health

Mushrooms support gut health through their [polysaccharide content](https://doi.org/10.3390/biology12010122)—particularly β-glucans and chitin—which function as prebiotics that feed beneficial gut bacteria without being absorbed. Turkey tail (*Trametes versicolor*), lion's mane (*Hericium erinaceus*), and reishi (*Ganoderma lucidum*) are the [most studied species](https://doi.org/10.3390/jof10060394) for gut health. Turkey tail has the strongest human clinical evidence: [its compound PSP](https://doi.org/10.4161/gmic.29558) increased *Bifidobacterium* and *Lactobacillus* while reducing *Clostridium* in a randomized trial. Lion's mane specifically supports [gut barrier integrity](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijbiomac.2025.144013) and shows activity against *Helicobacter pylori*, a bacterium linked to stomach ulcers.

No single mushroom is definitively the best—each shares the same core mechanism but targets different aspects of gut function. Most evidence comes from [animal and lab studies](https://doi.org/10.3390/jof10060394); large-scale human trials remain limited. Mushrooms can cause [digestive discomfort](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu10101402) when introduced in larger amounts, particularly in the first few days. Their chitin, while prebiotic, is only [partially broken down](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu10101402) during digestion, meaning some material may pass through undigested. Most research has studied [polysaccharide extracts](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijbiomac.2025.140994) rather than whole mushroom foods, so how dietary mushroom consumption compares to supplementation is not fully established.

**Key Takeaway:** Multiple mushrooms—particularly turkey tail and lion's mane—support gut health by acting as prebiotics that feed beneficial bacteria, but no single species is definitively superior and most evidence comes from animal or lab research rather than large human trials.

* **Open Access:** Yes
