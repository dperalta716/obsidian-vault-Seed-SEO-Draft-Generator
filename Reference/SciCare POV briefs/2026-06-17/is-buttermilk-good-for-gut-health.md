## SciCare POV | Is Buttermilk Good for Gut Health?

Buttermilk is especially rich in the [milk fat globule membrane](https://doi.org/10.17113/ftb.57.04.19.6460) (MFGM), a natural coating of phospholipids, sphingomyelin, and glycoproteins that carries bioactive compounds with potential health benefits. Unlike a probiotic, which delivers live bacteria, MFGM and its sphingomyelin are [non-living milk compounds](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2019.108224) that act through the gut lining and the microbes already present rather than adding new ones; in cell and rodent studies they protect against disrupted lipid metabolism, gut dysbiosis, and inflammation, with some human evidence for reduced cholesterol absorption. This evidence comes from the isolated compounds, not buttermilk itself.

In mice, whey- and buttermilk-based formulas with lactoferrin and MFGM [reversed microbiota damage](https://doi.org/10.1002/mnfr.202300248), restoring beneficial bacterial families and short-chain fatty acid production and [normalizing gut motility](https://doi.org/10.1089/jmf.2024.0155) and immune-receptor balance. Lab (*in vitro*) digestion shows [buttermilk MFGM lipids](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16162798) reach and influence intestinal cells. As a fermented drink, buttermilk also belongs to a category whose [lactic acid bacteria](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17010009) are linked to favorable microbiota shifts and eased lactose intolerance—though this is category-level evidence—and [one human trial](https://doi.org/10.2486/indhealth.2017-0030) measuring gut bacteria after buttermilk intake found no change. Most direct buttermilk evidence comes from animal and lab studies, so human research is needed to confirm any gut effect.

**Key Takeaway:** Direct evidence that buttermilk benefits gut health is limited and comes mostly from animal and lab studies, with the one human trial finding no change in gut bacteria; its MFGM compounds and fermented cultures are biologically plausible aids, but the benefit remains largely unproven in people.

Barukčić I et al. | Food Technology and Biotechnology (2019)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Review of food-technology and laboratory studies; not a human subject study  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.17113/ftb.57.04.19.6460](https://doi.org/10.17113/ftb.57.04.19.6460)

**Why it's useful:** This review surveys how whey and buttermilk—both dairy by-products—can be turned into functional beverages, with attention to buttermilk's milk fat globule membrane and its bioactive compounds. It connects to the topic by explaining why buttermilk is viewed as a source of potentially health-promoting components.
