## SciCare POV | Fecal Transplant

Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) transfers gut bacteria from a healthy, screened donor into a recipient's digestive tract—via colonoscopy, enema, or oral capsule—to restore a disrupted gut microbial community. Its most established use is recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* infection (rCDI), where repeated antibiotic treatment fails to prevent relapse. FMT is now integrated into [clinical practice guidelines](https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22) for rCDI, working by restoring the bacterial diversity that prevents *C. difficile* from re-establishing. Beyond rCDI, FMT is actively studied in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), IBS, metabolic conditions, and cancer immunotherapy, reflecting recognition that the gut microbiome plays a role in [diseases far beyond](https://doi.org/10.1097/MOG.0000000000001027) the digestive tract.

In IBD, evidence is promising but uneven. Meta-analyses of randomized trials in ulcerative colitis found FMT significantly improved [clinical and endoscopic remission](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10151-025-03113-7) versus placebo, with oral delivery and multi-donor protocols associated with better results. An umbrella review of 16 meta-analyses on FMT in IBD found remission benefits across all analyses, but flagged that [methodological quality](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10620-025-08946-8) was critically low in the majority—calling for more rigorous trials before wider clinical adoption. FMT also showed [favorable effects](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16132114) for IBS without serious adverse events. Key limitations include the absence of standardized protocols for donor selection, delivery route, and dosing; high variability in outcomes depending on [donor characteristics](https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22); and limited understanding of which specific bacteria drive success versus failure.

**Key Takeaway:** FMT is a clinically established treatment for recurrent *C. difficile* infection and shows real promise in ulcerative colitis and IBS, but its broader application is constrained by a lack of standardized protocols, high variability in outcomes, and a research base that—outside of *C. difficile*—remains methodologically limited.

### **Suggested References**

#### **1\. Fecal Microbiota Transplantation: Current Challenges and Future Landscapes**

Yadegar A et al. | Clinical Microbiology Reviews (2024)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults; covers human studies across recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* infection, IBD, metabolic conditions, and cancer immunotherapy  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22](https://doi.org/10.1128/cmr.00060-22)

**Why it's useful:** This review is the broadest and most detailed source on FMT available, covering what the procedure is, how it's administered, what conditions it's used for, and what its current safety and efficacy evidence looks like. It also addresses regulatory challenges, donor screening, and the limitations that prevent wider clinical adoption—making it the foundational reference for understanding FMT comprehensively.

#### **2\. Fecal Microbiota Transplantation for Patients with Ulcerative Colitis: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Control Trials**

Gefen R et al. | Techniques in Coloproctology (2025)

* **Article Type:** Systematic review / Meta-analysis  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults with ulcerative colitis (median age 40.7 years)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1007/s10151-025-03113-7](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10151-025-03113-7)

**Why it's useful:** This meta-analysis pooled 14 randomized controlled trials and found FMT significantly improved clinical and endoscopic remission in ulcerative colitis versus placebo. It also identified oral delivery and multi-donor protocols as associated with better outcomes—practical details relevant to understanding how administration method shapes results.

#### **3\. Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT) and Clinical Outcomes Among Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) Patients: An Umbrella Review**

Malik S et al. | Digestive Diseases and Sciences (2025)

* **Article Type:** Umbrella review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults with IBD (ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease)  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1007/s10620-025-08946-8](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10620-025-08946-8)

**Why it's useful:** This umbrella review synthesized 16 meta-analyses on FMT in IBD and found remission benefits consistently across all of them—but also transparently flagged that methodological quality was critically low in the majority of included reviews. It's the most honest and high-level assessment of where the IBD evidence base actually stands, strengths and weaknesses included.

#### **4\. The Efficacy of Probiotics, Prebiotics, Synbiotics, and Fecal Microbiota Transplantation in Irritable Bowel Syndrome: A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis**

Wu Y et al. | Nutrients (2024)

* **Article Type:** Systematic review / Network meta-analysis  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults with IBS  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16132114](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16132114)

**Why it's useful:** This network meta-analysis compared FMT head-to-head against probiotics, prebiotics, and synbiotics in IBS, finding FMT and probiotics had the most favorable effects—with no serious adverse events in either group. It provides useful context for where FMT sits relative to other microbiome-based interventions for IBS specifically.

Montrose JA et al. | Current Opinion in Gastroenterology (2024)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults with IBD (ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease, pouchitis)

It's a practical source for understanding the limitations that currently prevent FMT from moving beyond clinical trials in IBD.
