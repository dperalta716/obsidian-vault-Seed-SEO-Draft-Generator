## SciCare POV | Ginger \+ Gut Health

⚠️ **Note:** Most of the studies behind these findings were conducted in people with functional dyspepsia, IBS, or other functional GI conditions. Results may not apply directly to otherwise healthy individuals.

Ginger (*Zingiber officinale*) contains active compounds—mainly gingerols and shogaols—that act on the gut through several mechanisms at once. A narrative review found ginger [reduces inflammation](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac115) and blocks the receptors behind nausea and vomiting, consistent with human trials. A randomized controlled trial in adults with mild-to-moderate functional dyspepsia found [steamed ginger extract](https://doi.org/10.1039/d5fo01172h) improved gastrointestinal symptom scores versus placebo, with a favorable safety profile. A review of food-based options for functional GI disorders rated ginger among the [more promising](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae047) for IBS and dyspepsia, though short-term use may offer little benefit.

Ginger's gut mechanisms are better characterized than those of most herbs. A review of herbal treatments for gut-brain disorders found ginger [improves motility](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010), dampens visceral pain sensitivity, and suppresses low-grade inflammation, and identified it as one of the few herbs with placebo-controlled evidence across more than one gut mechanism. Its strongest evidence is for nausea: a meta-analysis in adults receiving chemotherapy found [ginger capsules](https://doi.org/10.1097/NCC.0000000000001355), added to standard antiemetics, reduced severe nausea and vomiting. Ginger is generally [well-tolerated](https://doi.org/10.1039/d5fo01172h), with [no clinically meaningful](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010) adverse effects reported across these trials.

**Key Takeaway:** Ginger has well-characterized gut mechanisms—improving motility, lowering inflammation, and blocking nausea receptors—and the most consistent human evidence among herbs for functional GI symptoms and nausea, though nearly all of it comes from clinical populations rather than healthy adults. There is little to show it does much for everyday gut health in people without a specific complaint, so it shouldn't be presented as a general gut-health aid.

### **Suggested References**

#### **1\. Therapeutic Health Effects of Ginger (Zingiber officinale): Updated Narrative Review Exploring the Mechanisms of Action**

Crichton M et al. | Nutrition Reviews (2023)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human studies; mechanistic data drawn from preclinical research to explain human clinical trial findings  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac115](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac115)

**Why it's useful:** This review maps out exactly how ginger works in the body—covering its effects on gut inflammation, gastric emptying, nausea receptors, blood sugar, and more—by connecting preclinical mechanistic data to findings from human trials. It's a strong foundation for understanding why ginger consistently appears across gut health research and what distinguishes it from herbs with less mechanistic clarity.

---

#### **2\. Efficacy and Safety of Steamed Ginger Extract for Gastric Health: A Randomized, Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Multi-Center Clinical Trial**

Baek HI et al. | Food & Function (2025)

* **Article Type:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled multicenter trial  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults with mild-to-moderate functional dyspepsia (ages 19–75)  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1039/d5fo01172h](https://doi.org/10.1039/d5fo01172h)

**Why it's useful:** This trial is one of the most focused human clinical studies on ginger specifically for GI symptom relief, finding significant improvements in abdominal pain, constipation, indigestion, and reflux compared to placebo over 12 weeks. It also assessed safety via blood and urine markers, finding no clinically meaningful changes—making it a useful source for both efficacy and tolerability.

#### **3\. The Effectiveness and Safety of Natural Food and Food-Derived Extract Supplements for Treating Functional Gastrointestinal Disorders—Current Perspectives**

Liu T et al. | Nutrition Reviews (2025)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review of randomized controlled trials  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults with functional dyspepsia and IBS  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae047](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae047)

**Why it's useful:** This review covers food-based and food-derived supplements for functional GI disorders—including ginger—and identifies it as one of the more promising options across both dyspepsia and IBS. It's a useful reference for contextualizing ginger within the broader landscape of food-as-medicine approaches to gut symptoms, and it is transparent about the limitations of short-term use and the need for larger trials.

#### **4\. Mechanisms of Action and Clinical Effectiveness of Herbal Treatments for Disorders of Gut-Brain Interaction**

Moniruzzaman M et al. | Digestive and Liver Disease (2025)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults with functional dyspepsia and IBS  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010)

**Why it's useful:** This review assesses herbal treatments through both a clinical and mechanistic lens, identifying ginger as one of the few herbs with placebo-controlled trial evidence across multiple gut mechanisms—including motility, pain sensitivity, and gut lining inflammation. It's particularly useful for explaining why ginger's multi-compound nature may underlie its effectiveness, and for placing it alongside peppermint oil as one of the better-evidenced herbal options in this space.

#### **5\. Efficacy and Safety of Ginger on Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials**

Lin CY et al. | Cancer Nursing (2024)

* **Article Type:** Systematic review / Meta-analysis  
* **Population/Demographic:** Adults undergoing chemotherapy (cancer patients)  
* **Open Access:** No  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.1097/NCC.0000000000001355](https://doi.org/10.1097/NCC.0000000000001355)

**Why it's useful:** This meta-analysis synthesized 35 randomized controlled trials and found ginger capsules, when combined with standard antiemetics, significantly reduced severe nausea and vomiting in cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy. While the population is specific, it provides the strongest body of human trial evidence for ginger's anti-nausea mechanism—supporting its broader relevance as a gut-affecting compound.
