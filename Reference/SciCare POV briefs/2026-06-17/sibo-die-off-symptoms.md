## SciCare POV | What are Die-Off Symptoms

**Important SciCare note: Non-compliant to use SIBO and die-off together since it extrapolates die-off symptoms (Herxheimer reaction) to another medical condition (SIBO), and there is no scientific evidence to support that link.** We should not use "SIBO" and "die-off symptoms" as keywords since we'd largely be extrapolating “Herxing” to another medical condition (SIBO), and there isn't scientific evidence to support that link.

“Die-off" is a popular term for symptoms such as chills, fever, headache, and fatigue that often get attributed to first starting antibiotics, antifungals, probiotics, or a "detox" or cleanse, despite little scientific evidence supporting most of these links. The one version with solid scientific backing is the [Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction](https://doi.org/10.4269/ajtmh.16-0434), which can occur after antibiotic treatment for spirochetal infections and is thought to be driven by bacterial components and inflammatory signals released as the bacteria die. It is well documented when antibiotics treat infections such as [syphilis](https://doi.org/10.4103/ijstd.ijstd_107_22), [Lyme disease](https://doi.org/10.4269/ajtmh.16-0434), and [leptospirosis](https://doi.org/10.4103/singaporemedj.SMJ-2022-196).

Beyond these specific infections, the evidence for die-off is thin. Standard care for small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) is usually oral antibiotics, and [evidence-based reviews](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.8860) describe their effects and side effects without any recognized die-off reaction. Since broad-spectrum antibiotics carry notable side effects, probiotics are being studied as an alternative and may help [reduce overgrowth](https://doi.org/10.1089/acm.2020.0275) and ease symptoms, though their effects are [strain-specific](https://doi.org/10.1177/17562848251399033) and targeted research for this condition is still needed. Whether a die-off-like process occurs with antimicrobials or probiotics in conditions such as SIBO is not confirmed in controlled studies and remains open for research.

**Key Takeaway:** "Die-off" is widely invoked for antibiotics, antifungals, probiotics, cleanses, and conditions like SIBO, but the only well-documented version is the Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction seen when antibiotics treat spirochetal infections such as syphilis and Lyme disease. There is currently no solid evidence for a die-off reaction in conditions such as SIBO or during probiotic use.

## Suggested References

#### **1\. The Jarisch-Herxheimer Reaction After Antibiotic Treatment of Spirochetal Infections: A Review of Recent Cases and Our Understanding of Pathogenesis**

Butler T | The American Journal of Tropical Medicine and Hygiene (2016)

* **Article Type:** Narrative review  
* **Population/Demographic:** Human (clinical cases across spirochetal infections)  
* **Open Access:** Yes  
* **DOI/URL:** [https://doi.org/10.4269/ajtmh.16-0434](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5239707/)

**Why it's useful:** This review explains what the Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction is and why it happens, summarizing cases across spirochetal infections like syphilis, Lyme disease, and leptospirosis. It describes how the reaction is triggered by bacterial components and inflammatory signals when antibiotics kill the bacteria. It anchors the one well-documented form of "die-off."
