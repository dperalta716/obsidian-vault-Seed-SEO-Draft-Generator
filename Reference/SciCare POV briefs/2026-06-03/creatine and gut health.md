## 📝 Creatine and Gut Health

**Target KW:** creatine and gut health

---

### SciCare POV | Creatine + Gut Health

Creatine is best known for its role in muscle support, but it plays an active role in keeping the gut wall structurally sound. The [cells lining the gut](https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2020.05.033) depend on a specialized transporter to absorb creatine, which they use to fuel the tight junction structures that maintain gut barrier integrity. When creatine availability is disrupted, these cells shift toward a [stressed metabolic state](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25126537), causing the barrier to become leaky and less effective. Higher [dietary creatine](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2025.1586569) intake from food sources has also been associated with a lower risk of chronic constipation, with no significant link to diarrhea. Beyond the gut barrier itself, creatine appears to [protect intestinal cells](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2025.2489072) from a form of stress-related cell death, pointing to a broader protective role in gut tissue.

However, much of what is known about creatine's role in gut function comes from research in people with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and from animal models. Studies in IBD patients show that the [creatine transporter](https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2020.05.033) is significantly reduced in inflamed colon tissue, suggesting that creatine deficiency may contribute to disease progression rather than simply result from it. More recent work confirms that [transporter dysregulation](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25126537) occurs in inflammatory conditions and in response to certain bacterial infections. In animal models, [disrupting the pathway](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mucimm.2023.09.002) made mice markedly more susceptible to intestinal inflammation. Most of these findings reflect what happens when creatine metabolism is disrupted in disease—though one [animal study](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.intimp.2024.113054) suggests creatine supplementation itself may reduce effects from gut inflammation. Whether similar effects would occur in otherwise healthy individuals has not been established. Notably, the population data linking creatine to lower constipation risk was based on [dietary creatine](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2025.1586569) from meat sources, not supplements—and whether supplemental creatine behaves the same way in the gut has not been directly tested in healthy individuals.

**Key Takeaway:** Creatine supports gut barrier integrity and may reduce constipation risk, but most mechanistic evidence comes from IBD populations and animal models—and it is unclear whether the effects of supplemental creatine would similarly affect healthy adults.

### Suggested References

#### 1. Creatine Transporter, Reduced in Colon Tissues From Patients With Inflammatory Bowel Diseases, Regulates Energy Balance in Intestinal Epithelial Cells, Epithelial Integrity, and Barrier Function

Hall CHT et al. | Gastroenterology (2020)

- **Article Type:** Translational research study (human biopsy analysis, cell models, and animal models)
- **Population/Demographic:** IBD patients (Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis) + mouse models + intestinal cell lines
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1053/j.gastro.2020.05.033

**Why it's useful:** This study analyzes biopsy samples from IBD patients and finds that the transporter responsible for getting creatine into gut lining cells is significantly reduced in disease—and that without adequate creatine, the gut barrier breaks down.

---

#### 2. Intestinal Epithelial Creatine Transporter SLC6A8 Dysregulation in Inflammation and in Response to Adherent Invasive *E. coli* Infection

Sawant H et al. | International Journal of Molecular Sciences (2024)

- **Article Type:** Preclinical study (*in vitro* cell models and animal models)
- **Population/Demographic:** Intestinal cell lines (Caco-2, IEC-6) + mouse models; not a human subject study
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25126537

**Why it's useful:** This study examines how inflammation and a specific bacterial infection disrupt the creatine transporter in gut lining cells. It shows that pro-inflammatory signals reduce creatine uptake into cells, and that infection compounds this effect—creating a creatine deficit at precisely the time gut cells need the most energy.

---

#### 3. Creatine-Mediated Ferroptosis Inhibition Is Involved in the Intestinal Radioprotection of Daytime-Restricted Feeding

He Y et al. | Gut Microbes (2025)

- **Article Type:** Animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse models
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2025.2489072

**Why it's useful:** This study reveals a specific mechanism by which creatine protects intestinal cells—it prevents a form of stress-induced cell death triggered by oxidative damage. Daytime time-restricted feeding elevated gut creatine levels through changes in the gut microbiome (particularly *Bifidobacterium pseudolongum*), which in turn protected the intestinal lining. It broadens the understanding of creatine's protective role in the gut beyond barrier function alone.

---

#### 4. Association of Dietary Creatine Intake From Meat Protein Sources With Different Types of Intestinal Problems: Insights From NHANES 2005–2010

Zheng B et al. | Frontiers in Nutrition (2025)

- **Article Type:** Cross-sectional observational study
- **Population/Demographic:** US adults (NHANES national survey data)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2025.1586569

**Why it's useful:** This is the only study in this set that examines creatine and gut health in a real-world dietary context among generally healthy adults. Using national health survey data, it found that higher dietary creatine intake from meat sources was linked to a lower risk of chronic constipation, with no significant association with diarrhea. It's an important reference for distinguishing dietary creatine from supplemental creatine.

---

#### 5. Fundamental Role for the Creatine Kinase Pathway in Protection From Murine Colitis

Hall CH et al. | Mucosal Immunology (2023)

- **Article Type:** Animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse models (DSS-induced colitis)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mucimm.2023.09.002

**Why it's useful:** This study examines what happens when the creatine kinase energy pathway is genetically disrupted in mice, finding that those animals become significantly more vulnerable to intestinal inflammation. It demonstrates that creatine metabolism plays a role in the gut immune response—not just structural integrity.

---

#### 6. Integrative Analysis Revealed Novel Putative Therapeutic Targets of Ulcerative Colitis: Role of Creatine

Zhu J et al. | International Immunopharmacology (2024)

- **Article Type:** Translational research study (metabolomics, Mendelian randomization, and animal models)
- **Population/Demographic:** UC patients (serum metabolomics) + mouse colitis models
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.intimp.2024.113054

**Why it's useful:** This study combines metabolomic data from UC patients with Mendelian randomization—a statistical method used to test for causal relationships—and a mouse supplementation experiment to examine creatine's role in gut inflammation. It found that lower creatine levels are causally linked to higher UC risk, and that supplementing creatine in mice with colitis actively reduced inflammation by shifting immune cells toward a less inflammatory state. It's the most direct evidence in this set that creatine supplementation itself—not just creatine deficiency—may play a role in gut protection.
