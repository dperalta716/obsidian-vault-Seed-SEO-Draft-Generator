## 📝 Humic Extract for Gut Health

**Target KW:** humic extract for gut health

---

### SciCare POV | Humic Extract + Gut Health

Humic extracts are naturally occurring organic compounds formed from the decomposition of organic matter in soil, peat, and coal over long periods of time. They consist of several fractions—primarily humic acid, fulvic acid, and humin. Fulvic acid, the smaller and more bioavailable fraction, is soluble under all pH conditions and crosses cell membranes more readily than the other fractions, making it the most studied for health applications and the focus of gut-relevant research. A broad review of humic substances found that fulvic acid demonstrates [antioxidant, anti-inflammatory](https://doi.org/10.3390/antiox14091139) antimicrobial, and immunomodulatory properties, including the ability to neutralize inflammatory signals and [support gut bacteria](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10661-026-15146-y) balance.

The most direct gut-focused evidence comes from a combination of human and animal research. In the only human study identified, oral humic acid supplementation significantly increased overall gut bacteria concentration in [healthy volunteers](https://doi.org/10.3748/wjg.v23.i5.885) across a broad range of bacterial groups without disrupting existing microbial balance. In mice, humic acid reduced gut inflammation, increased Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium populations, lowered inflammatory markers in blood, and supported [tight junction proteins](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2023.1147110) that maintain the gut barrier. Fulvic acid formulations in lab and animal models stimulated beneficial bacteria, suppressed pathogenic strains, enhanced [intestinal cell growth](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-026-37331-2), and increased microbial diversity—with no meaningful toxicity detected. Much of this evidence comes from animal models and laboratory research, and human clinical trials on humic and fulvic acid extracts for gut health remain very limited.

**Key Takeaway:** Fulvic acid—the bioavailable fraction of humic extracts—shows early promise for gut health through bacteria balance, gut barrier support, and reduced inflammation, but the field is largely preclinical and human evidence is sparse, with only one small human study directly addressing gut microbiome effects.

### Suggested References

#### 1. Biomedical Applications of Humic Substances: From Natural Biopolymers to Therapeutic Agents

Gvozdeva Y et al. | Antioxidants (2025)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies; includes in vitro data
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/antiox14091139

**Why it's useful:** This review covers the broad biological properties of humic substances—including both humic acid and fulvic acid—with sections on antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and immunomodulatory effects, as well as their influence on gut microbiota balance. It provides a useful foundation for understanding why fulvic acid, as part of the humic extract family, is gaining interest for health applications.

---

#### 2. Advances in Fulvic Acid Extraction from Lignite: Techniques, Challenges, and Applications

Yousaf I et al. | Environmental Monitoring and Assessment (2026)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Not a human subject study; covers chemistry, extraction methods, and health application overview
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10661-026-15146-y

**Why it's useful:** This review explains what fulvic acid is, where it comes from, and why its molecular properties—including its smaller size and full solubility—make it the most bioavailable fraction of humic extracts. It also covers its immunomodulatory effects and potential as a nutraceutical, providing the scientific basis for why fulvic acid is the most relevant fraction to focus on for gut health research.

---

#### 3. Integrated Safety and Microbiota Profiling of Fulvic Acid Formulations Across In Vitro and In Vivo Models

Szwed-Georgiou A et al. | Scientific Reports (2026)

- **Article Type:** Controlled laboratory and animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** In vitro cell models (intestinal epithelial, liver, fibroblast) + mouse model
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-026-37331-2

**Why it's useful:** This study directly tested fulvic acid formulations for safety and gut microbiome effects, finding stimulation of beneficial bacteria including Lactobacillus, suppression of pathogens, enhanced intestinal cell growth, and increased microbial diversity—all with a favorable safety profile. It is the most focused and recent study on fulvic acid's gut-specific biological activity.

---

#### 4. Humic Acids Alleviate Dextran Sulfate Sodium-Induced Colitis by Positively Modulating Gut Microbiota

Huang J et al. | Frontiers in Microbiology (2023)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Mouse model of colitis (DSS-induced); includes in vitro cell experiments
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2023.1147110

**Why it's useful:** This study examined humic acid's effect on gut inflammation in mice, finding that it increased Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium, reduced inflammatory markers, and supported tight junction proteins that maintain the gut barrier. It provides the most direct mechanistic evidence for how humic substances interact with the gut microbiota and gut lining.

---

#### 5. Impact of Humic Acids on the Colonic Microbiome in Healthy Volunteers

Swidsinski A et al. | World Journal of Gastroenterology (2017)

- **Article Type:** Observational study
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adults (ages 18–55)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3748/wjg.v23.i5.885

**Why it's useful:** This is the only published human study on humic acid supplementation and gut microbiome composition. It found that 45 days of oral supplementation significantly increased overall gut bacteria concentration across a broad range of bacterial groups without disrupting microbial balance or diversity—a meaningful finding given how sparse the human evidence base is for this topic.
