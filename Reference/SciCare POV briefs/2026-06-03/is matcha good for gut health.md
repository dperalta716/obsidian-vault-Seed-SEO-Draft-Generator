## 📝 Is Matcha Good for Gut Health

**Target KW:** is matcha good for gut health

---

### SciCare POV | Matcha + Gut Health

Matcha delivers catechins (especially EGCG), [fiber-bound polyphenols](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2023.2194419), and [insoluble dietary fiber](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu18101613) directly to the gut because you consume the whole powdered leaf rather than a steeped infusion. These fiber-bound compounds resist digestion until they reach the colon, where gut bacteria break them down and use them as fuel—[boosting the bioavailability](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2023.2194419) of matcha's polyphenols and their impact on the microbiome. A [double-blind trial](https://doi.org/10.3164/jcbn.22-81) in healthy adults found that two weeks of matcha consumption meaningfully shifted gut bacteria diversity, increasing *Coprococcus* (linked to butyrate production and gut immune health) while reducing *Fusobacterium*, a genus associated with gut inflammation. A [separate trial](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12937-023-00859-4) in healthy men also observed microbiota shifts following regular matcha intake.

EGCG and other catechins selectively support [beneficial bacteria](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu18101613) while suppressing potentially harmful ones. In animal studies, matcha also strengthened [gut lining integrity](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jafc.6c00065) by supporting tight junction proteins and shifted [bile acid metabolism](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.931060) in ways that may benefit broader digestive function. On the downside, matcha's [caffeine and tannin](https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2023.2194419) content can cause nausea, digestive discomfort, or [loose stools](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu18101613)—particularly in high amounts or when consumed on an empty stomach. [Tea polyphenols](https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxab159) have also been shown to meaningfully [reduce iron absorption](https://doi.org/10.1111/nbu.12643), which is worth considering for those at risk of deficiency. Much of the mechanistic evidence for matcha's gut benefits comes from animal research, and more human trials are needed to confirm these specific effects.

**Key Takeaway:** Matcha supports gut health primarily through its catechins and fiber-bound polyphenols, which feed beneficial bacteria and improve microbiome diversity—but high intake may cause digestive discomfort, and its polyphenols can reduce iron absorption in those at risk of deficiency.

### Suggested References

#### 1. A Comprehensive Review of Matcha: Production, Food Application, Potential Health Benefits, and Gastrointestinal Fate of Main Phenolics

Ye JH et al. | Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition (2023)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Mixed; covers human, animal, and lab-based studies
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2023.2194419

**Why it's useful:** This review covers matcha's chemical composition, how it's processed, and what happens to its key compounds as they move through the digestive system. It explains why consuming the whole leaf—rather than a steeped tea—increases the amount of fiber-bound polyphenols that reach the gut, where they can influence the microbiome. It's a strong foundational source for understanding how matcha's unique form drives its gut health potential.

#### 2. Matcha as a Source of Bioactive Compounds: A Review of Health-Promoting Properties and Potential Applications

Slawińska P & Raczkowska E | Nutrients (2026)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Mixed; covers human, animal, and *in vitro* studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu18101613

**Why it's useful:** This review summarizes the current science on matcha's major bioactive compounds—including EGCG, catechins, and L-theanine—and how they relate to metabolic health and gut microbiota composition. It covers both the benefits and the limitations of the existing evidence, making it useful for contextualizing what is and isn't yet confirmed in humans.

#### 3. A Randomized, Double-Blinded Study Evaluating Effect of Matcha Green Tea on Human Fecal Microbiota

Morishima S et al. | Journal of Clinical Biochemistry and Nutrition (2022)

- **Article Type:** Randomized controlled trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adult humans
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3164/jcbn.22-81

**Why it's useful:** Participants who drank matcha for two weeks showed meaningful changes in microbiota diversity, including increases in a bacteria type linked to gut immune health and decreases in one associated with inflammation. It provides some of the strongest human-level evidence available on matcha and the gut microbiome.

#### 4. Matcha Green Tea Beverage Moderates Fatigue and Supports Resistance Training-Induced Adaptation

Shigeta M et al. | Nutrition Journal (2023)

- **Article Type:** Randomized controlled trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adult men
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s12937-023-00859-4

**Why it's useful:** This trial was primarily designed to study matcha's effect on muscle recovery and fatigue, but gut microbiota was also measured. Participants who consumed matcha showed shifts in several bacterial groups compared to placebo, supporting the idea that regular matcha intake changes microbiome composition in healthy people. While gut health is a secondary finding, it adds useful human data to the picture.

#### 5. Synergistic Effects of Nanoselenium and Environmental Factors on the Quality, Antioxidant Activity, and Gut Health Benefits of Matcha

Fang Q et al. | Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry (2026)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal and lab study
- **Population/Demographic:** Animal (mice); not a human subject study
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jafc.6c00065

**Why it's useful:** This study looked at how different cultivation conditions affect matcha's functional properties, including its effects on the gut. It found that matcha supported gut lining integrity by increasing the expression of proteins that keep the intestinal barrier intact—a mechanism relevant to overall digestive health. While it's an animal study, it provides useful mechanistic detail on how matcha may protect the gut wall.

#### 6. Matcha Green Tea Targets the Gut-Liver Axis to Alleviate Obesity and Metabolic Disorders Induced by a High-Fat Diet

Wang Y et al. | Frontiers in Nutrition (2022)

- **Article Type:** Controlled animal study
- **Population/Demographic:** Animal (mice); not a human subject study
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2022.931060

**Why it's useful:** This animal study explored how matcha affects the relationship between the gut and the liver, finding that it helped restore healthy gut bacteria and shifted bile acid metabolism in ways that supported metabolic function. While results are from a mouse model, it provides mechanistic context for how matcha's gut effects may extend to broader digestive and metabolic health.

#### 7. Tea Consumption Reduces Iron Bioavailability from NaFeEDTA in Nonanemic Women and Women with Iron Deficiency Anemia: Stable Iron Isotope Studies in Morocco

Lazrak M et al. | The Journal of Nutrition (2021)

- **Article Type:** Randomized controlled trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Adult women (nonanemic and iron-deficient)
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/nxab159

**Why it's useful:** This trial directly measured how tea polyphenols affect iron absorption in women, finding a substantial reduction in both healthy and iron-deficient participants when tea was consumed alongside a meal. Though the study used Moroccan green tea rather than matcha specifically, the polyphenol content is comparable—making it a strong source for the claim that regular tea consumption can interfere with iron uptake.

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Mixed; synthesizes evidence across populations
- **Open Access:** No

**Why it's useful:** This review covers the full range of dietary factors that either enhance or inhibit iron absorption, including polyphenols from foods like tea.
