## 📝 Best Herbs for Gut Health

**Target KW:** best herbs for gut health

---

### SciCare POV | Best Herbs for Gut Health

*⚠️ **Note:** The majority of studies behind these findings were conducted in people with functional dyspepsia (indigestion) or irritable bowel syndrome (IBS). Results may not apply directly to otherwise healthy individuals without these conditions.*

Herbs have been used for gut complaints across traditional medicine systems for centuries, and a growing body of clinical research now offers a clearer picture of how they work and which ones have the most evidence. A review published in a leading gastroenterology journal assessed placebo-controlled trials in adults with functional dyspepsia and IBS across multiple herbal preparations and found they work through [several distinct mechanisms](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010)—improving how quickly food moves through the gut, reducing the gut's sensitivity to pain signals, suppressing low-grade gut lining inflammation, and shifting the gut microbiome. What sets herbs apart from single-compound drugs is that most contain multiple active compounds targeting several of these mechanisms simultaneously, which researchers suggest may underlie their effectiveness. A Cochrane review of 41 trials in adults with functional dyspepsia, assessing 27 different herbal medicines, found the strongest evidence—at moderate certainty—for [peppermint oil combined](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013323.pub2) with caraway oil, turmeric (Curcuma longa), and licorice root (*Glycyrrhiza glabra*) for improving overall digestive symptoms and quality of life versus placebo. Peppermint oil carried the [highest certainty evidence](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae107) among plant-based compounds across an umbrella review of randomized IBS trials in adults as well.

Of the individual herbs, ginger and turmeric have the most mechanistic detail in the literature. Ginger works in the gut primarily by blocking the receptors that trigger nausea and vomiting, reducing gut inflammation, and speeding gastric emptying—making it particularly relevant for motility-related complaints, based on mechanisms drawn from [human clinical trials](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac115) in adults across gastrointestinal and metabolic conditions. A multi-herb preparation combining peppermint, ginger, and chamomile also showed consistent benefit in adults with functional dyspepsia and IBS through these overlapping pathways. Turmeric's active compound, curcumin, is notable because—despite poor absorption into the bloodstream—it reaches meaningful concentrations directly in the gut, where it [modulates bacteria composition](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25147710), strengthens the gut barrier, and reduces intestinal inflammation; clinical studies supporting these effects have largely been conducted in adults with metabolic conditions such as type 2 diabetes and metabolic syndrome. Reviews also show curcumin [enhances intestinal barrier](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17091430) function and reduces systemic inflammation through its interactions with the gut microbiome. An umbrella review of IBS trials in adults noted that both peppermint oil and curcumin improved symptom severity compared to placebo, though the evidence certainty across most herbal interventions remains low to moderate and most studies are short-term.

**Key Takeaway:** The herbal medicine research landscape for gut health is broad but variable in quality; peppermint oil has the most consistent and highest-certainty human evidence for GI symptom relief, followed by ginger (for motility and nausea), turmeric/curcumin (for gut inflammation and microbiome support), and licorice root—each working through distinct mechanisms, which is why multi-herb preparations targeting several pathways simultaneously show some of the most promising clinical results.

### Suggested References

#### 1. Mechanisms of Action and Clinical Effectiveness of Herbal Treatments for Disorders of Gut-Brain Interaction

Moniruzzaman M et al. | Digestive and Liver Disease (2025)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Adults with functional dyspepsia and IBS
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dld.2025.07.010

**Why it's useful:** This review covers both the clinical evidence and the underlying mechanisms for herbal treatments across functional gut disorders, examining how herbs like peppermint oil, ginger, and multi-herb formulas work through gut motility, sensory function, inflammation, and the microbiome. It's a strong foundation for understanding why herbs show up consistently in the gut health literature and what distinguishes the ones with the most evidence.

---

#### 2. Non-Chinese Herbal Medicines for Functional Dyspepsia

Báez G et al. | Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews (2023)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review / Meta-analysis (Cochrane)
- **Population/Demographic:** Adults with functional dyspepsia
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013323.pub2

**Why it's useful:** This Cochrane review assessed 41 trials across 27 herbal medicines and is the most comprehensive head-to-head comparison of herbs for digestive symptoms available. It found moderate-certainty evidence for peppermint-caraway oil combinations, turmeric, and licorice root—making it the key reference for understanding which herbs have the strongest clinical backing.

---

#### 3. Nutritional Interventions in Adult Patients With Irritable Bowel Syndrome: An Umbrella Review of Systematic Reviews and Meta-analyses of Randomized Clinical Trials

Zeraattalab-Motlagh S et al. | Nutrition Reviews (2025)

- **Article Type:** Umbrella review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- **Population/Demographic:** Adults with IBS
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuae107

**Why it's useful:** This umbrella review synthesized evidence from 175 trials across 58 meta-analyses covering nutritional and herbal interventions in IBS, placing peppermint oil and curcumin among those with measurable benefit for symptom severity. It provides the broadest available overview of how herbal interventions compare to other nutritional strategies in an IBS population.

#### 4. Therapeutic Health Effects of Ginger (Zingiber officinale): Updated Narrative Review Exploring the Mechanisms of Action

Crichton M et al. | Nutrition Reviews (2023)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Adults; synthesizes mechanisms from human clinical trials across gastrointestinal and metabolic conditions
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuac115

**Why it's useful:** This review maps out exactly how ginger works in the body at a mechanistic level—covering its effects on nausea receptor pathways, gut motility, inflammation, and blood sugar—connecting these mechanisms to findings from human clinical trials. It's the most detailed source for understanding ginger's specific relevance to gut function.

---

#### 5. Curcumin and Gut Microbiota: A Narrative Overview with Focus on Glycemic Control

Servida S et al. | International Journal of Molecular Sciences (2024)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies; clinical data primarily from adults with type 2 diabetes and metabolic syndrome
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25147710

**Why it's useful:** This review explains why curcumin is uniquely relevant to gut health—despite being poorly absorbed systemically, it concentrates in the gut where it directly interacts with gut bacteria and the intestinal lining. It provides the mechanistic rationale for turmeric's gut benefits and contextualizes the populations in which this has been studied.

#### 6. Curcumin Modulation of the Gut-Brain Axis for Neuroinflammation and Metabolic Disorders Prevention and Treatment

Cerullo M et al. | Nutrients (2025)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies; clinical data primarily from adults with obesity and metabolic disorders
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17091430

**Why it's useful:** This review expands on curcumin's gut-specific effects—covering how it improves gut barrier function, shifts microbiome composition, and reduces systemic inflammation—and connects these effects to broader outcomes via the gut-brain axis. It complements reference [5] by covering the barrier and inflammation side of curcumin's gut mechanism.
