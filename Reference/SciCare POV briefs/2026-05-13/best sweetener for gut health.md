## SciCare POV | Best Sweetener for Gut Health

[Evidence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2161831322001983?via%3Dihub) suggests that the relationship between sweeteners—including non-nutritive sweeteners (NNSs)—and gut health is complex and influenced by factors such as dose, frequency of intake, and overall dietary pattern. Unlike naturally occurring sugars found in whole foods, added sugars are typically consumed in refined forms without fiber or other protective nutrients, leading to faster absorption, larger blood sugar spikes, and greater disruption to the gut environment.

Excessive intake of added sugars has been [linked](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2161831322001983?via%3Dihub) to reduced microbial diversity, increased inflammation, impaired gut barrier function, and a higher risk of chronic diseases like obesity, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease. Some non-nutritive sweeteners may also alter the gut microbiome, though [findings](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(22)00919-9?_returnURL=https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0092867422009199?showall%3Dtrue) remain mixed and research is still emerging. Overall, the quality of the diet appears to matter far more for gut health than any single sweetener alone, with fiber-rich, minimally processed diets showing the most consistent benefits.

**Key Takeaway:** Different sweeteners may affect the gut microbiome in different ways, though their impact likely depends on the broader dietary pattern in which they're consumed.

## Suggested References

### 1. Personalized microbiome-driven effects of non-nutritive sweeteners on human glucose tolerance

Suez J et al. | *Cell* (2022)

- **Article Type:** Randomized clinical trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Human adults; included healthy participants exposed to commonly consumed non-nutritive sweeteners, alongside germ-free mouse transplantation experiments
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2022.07.016

**Why it's useful:**
This paper investigates how non-nutritive sweeteners (NNSs) interact with the gut microbiome and metabolic health in humans. The researchers found that different sweeteners produced distinct microbiome and glycemic responses, and that these effects varied substantially between individuals. Importantly, the study suggests that some NNS-induced changes in glucose tolerance may be mediated by alterations in the gut microbiome, reinforcing the idea that sweeteners can have individualized and microbiome-dependent effects rather than universally "good" or "bad" outcomes.

### 2. Effects of Sweeteners on the Gut Microbiota: A Review of Experimental Studies and Clinical Trials

Ruiz-Ojeda FJ et al. | *Advances in Nutrition* (2019)

- **Article Type:** Randomized, double-blinded, parallel-design study
- **Population/Demographic:** Human clinical studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/advances/nmy037

**Why it's useful:**
Review provides a broad overview of the evidence examining how different sweeteners—including artificial sweeteners, natural non-nutritive sweeteners, and sugar alcohols—may influence the gut microbiota. It is particularly useful because it synthesizes findings across both human and preclinical studies, highlighting that sweeteners can have varying and highly context-dependent effects on microbial composition and metabolic health. The review also emphasizes that evidence remains inconsistent and that more long-term human research is needed before firm conclusions can be drawn about the gut health impacts of specific sweeteners.

### 3. Comparison of a Daily Steviol Glycoside Beverage compared with a Sucrose Beverage for Four Weeks on Gut Microbiome in Healthy Adults

Kwok D et al. | *The Journal of Nutrition* (2024)

- **Article Type:** Randomized clinical trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Human adults
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tjnut.2024.01.032

**Why it's useful:**
Adds nuance to the discussion around non-nutritive sweeteners and gut health, showing that not all sweeteners appear to affect the gut microbiome in the same way. In this randomized controlled trial, stevia consumption at a realistic intake level did not significantly alter gut microbial composition, short-chain fatty acid production, or cardiometabolic markers compared with sucrose, highlighting that sweetener effects may be highly compound-, dose-, and context-dependent.
