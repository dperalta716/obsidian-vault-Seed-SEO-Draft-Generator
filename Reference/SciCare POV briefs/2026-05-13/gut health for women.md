## SciCare POV | Gut Health for Women

Women's gut health is [deeply connected](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7971312/) to hormonal, immune, metabolic, and reproductive health. The gut microbiome helps regulate estrogen metabolism, inflammation, nutrient absorption, neurotransmitter production, and immune signaling—[systems](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9687867/) that naturally fluctuate across menstruation, pregnancy, perimenopause, and menopause. One important example is the [estrobolome](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10416750/), a collection of gut microbes involved in [estrogen metabolism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12113314/) and circulation. Research also suggests that the gut microbiome differs between men and women due to hormonal, immune, physiological, and lifestyle-related [factors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10731147/). These differences tend to emerge after puberty, highlighting the role sex hormones may play in shaping the microbiome and its downstream effects on immune and inflammatory responses throughout life.

At the same time, the gut microbiome is highly [dynamic and influenced](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6699480/) by many factors during adulthood, including diet, stress, sleep, medications, environment, genetics, and hormonal transitions. Broadly, lifestyle patterns that support overall health also appear to support gut health: consuming a diverse, fiber-rich diet, incorporating fermented foods, engaging in regular movement, prioritizing sleep, managing stress, and minimizing unnecessary antibiotic use when possible. Some probiotic and prebiotic interventions also show promise for supporting digestive health, immune function, and aspects of mood through the gut-brain axis, though outcomes are often strain-specific and can vary between individuals.

**Key Takeaway:** Women's gut health is closely intertwined with hormones, immunity, metabolism, and mood—making the gut microbiome a central player in whole-body health across every stage of life.

## Suggested References

### 1. Efficacy of Gut Microbiome-Targeted Interventions on Mental Health Symptoms in Women Across Key Hormonal Life Stages: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials

- **Author:** Dubois N et al. | Healthcare (2025)
- **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials
- **Population/Demographic:** Human research; women across reproductive and hormonal life stages (including menstruation, pregnancy/postpartum, and menopause)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/healthcare13222851

**Why it's useful:**
This paper consolidates evidence across multiple clinical trials researching effects of microbiome-targeted interventions (probiotics, prebiotics, synbiotics) on depression and anxiety symptoms in women during hormonally sensitive life stages. It is useful for understanding the growing evidence supporting the gut–brain axis in women's mental health and highlights opportunities for microbiome-based therapeutic approaches, while also identifying current research gaps and the need for more standardized studies.

### 2. Effects of Probiotic Supplementation on Hormonal and Clinical Outcomes in Women with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome: A Randomized Double-Blind Placebo-Controlled Trial

- **Author:** Arab A et al. | *Journal of Functional Foods* (2022)
- **Article Type:** Randomized double-blind placebo-controlled clinical trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Human research; adult women diagnosed with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jff.2022.105203

**Why it's useful:**
This study provides clinical evidence that probiotic supplementation may support hormonal and clinical outcomes in women with PCOS, a condition strongly linked to metabolic, reproductive, and mental health challenges. It is useful because it supports the growing understanding of the gut–hormone axis and suggests that microbiome-targeted interventions could be a complementary strategy for managing PCOS-related symptoms and endocrine dysfunction, given the specific probiotic strains and dosages are identified for the specific endpoints.

### 3. Investigating the Effects of Probiotics During the Menopause Transition: A Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis

- **Author:** Andrews R et al. | *Clinical Nutrition ESPEN* (2025)
- **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Population/Demographic:** Human research; perimenopausal and recently postmenopausal women
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnesp.2025.07.009

**Why it's useful:**
This paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of how probiotic interventions may support women during the menopause transition, with reported benefits across menopausal symptoms, psychological health, vaginal and urogenital health, and bone health. It is particularly useful because it synthesizes evidence from a large number of clinical studies and highlights the potential for probiotics to enhance the efficacy and tolerability of estrogenic therapies. The findings further strengthen the evidence base for the gut–hormone axis in menopause care and support the use of targeted microbiome interventions as complementary strategies for symptom management and healthy aging in women.
