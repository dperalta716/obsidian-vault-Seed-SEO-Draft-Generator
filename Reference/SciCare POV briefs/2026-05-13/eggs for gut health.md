## SciCare POV | Eggs + Gut Health

Current research suggests eggs are nutritionally supportive for overall gut health while remaining relatively neutral in their direct effects on the gut microbiome. While eggs are not a prebiotic and do not directly feed gut bacteria the way dietary fiber does, they contain [several nutrients](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu15122657) and bioactive compounds—including choline, carotenoids, and B vitamins—that may influence gut barrier function and inflammation. [Egg white proteins](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jafc.3c03230) and the peptides released during digestion in particular have shown potential for reinforcing the intestinal barrier and reducing inflammatory signaling, though much of this evidence remains preclinical.

At the microbiome level, direct effects appear [limited](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17132059) for most healthy adults. Some [evidence](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/22/4804) suggest modest shifts in genera like *Bifidobacterium* and *Prevotella* and [improvements](https://doi.org/10.1002/fsn3.2671) in gut microbiota function without raising inflammatory markers, though these findings come from small or specific populations and are not consistently observed. A recurring concern is [TMAO](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu12082220), a compound linked to cardiometabolic risk — certain gut bacteria can convert dietary choline into TMAO, raising questions about whether [egg intake](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2021.108906) could negatively affect cardiovascular health. Recent [meta-analysis](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12937-025-01227-0) suggests, however, that egg consumption does not appear to increase plasma TMAO in healthy adults.

**Key Takeaway:** Current research suggests eggs are not a major "microbiome-enhancing" food, though they may support aspects of host gut health and can fit within a gut-friendly dietary pattern when paired with fiber-rich plant foods.

**NOTE:** These are all assuming chicken/hen eggs—no other birds noted

## Suggested References

### 1. Eggs: Healthy or Risky? A Review of Evidence from High Quality Studies on Hen's Eggs

Myers M & Ruxton CHS | Nutrients (2023)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu15122657

**Why it's useful:** A broad overview of egg consumption and health outcomes. Notably finds that eggs do not raise TMAO levels and that RCT data consistently show neutral or beneficial metabolic effects, even in higher-risk populations.

---

### 2. Influence of Prolonged Whole Egg Supplementation on Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 and Short-Chain Fatty Acids Product: Implications for Human Health and Gut Microbiota

Suta S et al. | Nutrients (2023)

- **Article Type:** Cluster-randomized trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Children
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu15224804

**Why it's useful:** Directly measures egg supplementation's effect on gut microbiota and SCFAs. Whole eggs increased *Bifidobacterium* and *Prevotella* abundance and improved growth factors associated with bone health; however, did not meaningfully affect SCFAs.

---

### 3. Potential Benefits of Egg White Proteins and Their Derived Peptides in the Regulation of the Intestinal Barrier and Gut Microbiota: A Comprehensive Review

Yang Q et al. | Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry (2023)

- **Article Type:** Comprehensive review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies
- **Open Access:** No
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jafc.3c03230

**Why it's useful:** Focuses specifically on egg white protein and its bioactive peptides as modulators of gut health—particularly in restoring intestinal barrier integrity, reducing gut inflammation, and increasing beneficial microbiota populations. However, review notes that these still require in-depth analysis + further research, especially human trials.

---

### 4. Are Eggs Good Again? A Precision Nutrition Perspective on the Effects of Eggs on Cardiovascular Risk, Taking into Account Plasma Lipid Profiles and TMAO

Kang JW & Zivkovic AM | Journal of Nutritional Biochemistry (2022)

- **Article Type:** Mini review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human studies (both general adults and those with metabolic conditions)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnutbio.2021.108906

**Why it's useful:** Examines the choline-TMAO-gut microbiota axis in the context of egg intake. Finds that TMAO production from egg choline is highly individual and gut-microbiome-dependent, and that whole eggs do not consistently raise plasma TMAO unlike isolated choline supplements.

---

### 5. The Impact of Egg Consumption on Gastrointestinal Health: A Systematic Literature Review and Meta-Analysis

Sultan N et al. | Nutrients (2025)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adults
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu17132059

**Why it's useful:** A very comprehensive review on eggs and gut health. Meta-analyses confirmed eggs do not significantly raise TMAO or CRP (inflammatory marker), but reliably increase plasma choline; microbiome findings were mixed and inconclusive across studies.

---

### 6. Egg Consumption Improves Vascular and Gut Microbiota Function Without Increasing Inflammatory, Metabolic, and Oxidative Stress Markers

Liu X et al. | Food Science & Nutrition (2022)

- **Article Type:** Prospective non-randomized trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy young adult males
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1002/fsn3.2671

**Why it's useful:** A pilot study showing 2 eggs/day for 2 weeks altered gut microbiota *functional* pathways—notably reducing tryptophan degradation linked to cardiovascular inflammation—without changing microbial composition, TMAO, or inflammatory markers.

---

### 7. Effect of Egg Consumption on Circulating Choline, Betaine, and Trimethylamine N-Oxide in Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials

Aryaeian N et al. | Nutrition Journal (2025)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Population/Demographic:** Adults varying in health status (healthy, overweight/obese, metabolic syndrome)
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s12937-025-01227-0

**Why it's useful:** A meta-analysis on eggs and choline, betaine, and TMAO—no significant effects were found overall, though older adults and those with metabolic syndrome showed greater choline responses.
