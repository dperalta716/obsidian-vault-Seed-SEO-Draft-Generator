## SciCare POV | Beer + Gut Health

Emerging research suggests beer's relationship with the gut microbiome may depend heavily on both the amount consumed and the specific compounds within beer itself. While [excessive alcohol intake](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2022.916765) is consistently associated with gut dysbiosis, inflammation, and increased intestinal permeability, some [studies](https://doi.org/10.26599/FSHW.2023.9250004) suggest that moderate intake of polyphenol-rich or non-alcoholic beer may modestly support microbial diversity and the growth of bacteria involved in short-chain fatty acid production and immune regulation. [Researchers](https://doi.org/10.3390/antiox11040696) are particularly interested in beer-derived compounds such as polyphenols, soluble fibers, melanoidins, and fermented metabolites, which may interact with the gut microbiome in ways that resemble prebiotic activity.

Importantly, much of the potential microbiome-related benefit appears to be linked to these [non-alcoholic](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.alcohol.2019.05.006) compounds rather than ethanol itself. Several [small human](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.alcohol.2019.05.006) and [preclinical studies](https://doi.org/10.12938/bmfh.2024-045) suggest non-alcoholic beer may support some of the same microbial changes without the well-established drawbacks associated with chronic alcohol exposure. At the same time, the broader body of evidence continues to show that [higher alcohol intake](https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms13092000) may negatively affect microbial balance, gut barrier integrity, and inflammatory signaling. Taken together, current research suggests beer is not a "gut health" beverage, though moderate intake of polyphenol-rich or non-alcoholic beer may have context-dependent effects on microbiome composition in otherwise healthy adults.

**Key Takeaway:** Moderate intake of polyphenol-rich or non-alcoholic beer may modestly influence the gut microbiome, though excessive alcohol intake remains associated with negative effects on gut health.

**NOTE:** If this does proceed forward as a topic, it should be **very** careful to not encourage increased alcohol intake and should also highlight the downsides of alcohol.

## Suggested References

### 1. Association of moderate beer consumption with the gut microbiota

Liu Z et al. | Food Science and Human Wellness (2024)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human-focused review
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.26599/FSHW.2023.9250004

**Why it's useful:**
This review summarizes how moderate beer intake may influence gut microbiota through non-alcoholic beer components such as polyphenols and carbohydrates, while also discussing the potential risks associated with alcohol (ethanol) intake.

---

### 2. Effect of Moderate Consumption of Different Phenolic-Content Beers on the Human Gut Microbiota Composition: A Randomized Crossover Trial

Martínez-Montoro JI et al. | Antioxidants (2022)

- **Article Type:** Randomized crossover clinical trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Adult humans
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/antiox11040696

**Why it's useful:**
This clinical trial directly evaluates beers with different phenolic contents and demonstrates that beer polyphenols may alter gut microbiota composition. Interestingly, they found an increase of *Streptococcus* (higher abundance associated with liver damage) after dark beer, but no difference with lager or non-alcoholic beer.

---

### 3. Association of Moderate Beer Consumption with the Gut Microbiota and SCFA of Healthy Adults

González-Zancada N et al. | Molecules (2020)

- **Article Type:** Human observational study
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy adults
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/molecules25204772

**Why it's useful:**
This study found associations between moderate beer consumption, specific gut microbial taxa, and increased fecal butyrate levels—suggests that moderate beer intake may influence beneficial microbial metabolites.

---

### 4. Beer-gut microbiome alliance: a discussion of beer-mediated immunomodulation via the gut microbiome

Zhang S et al. | Frontiers in Nutrition (2023)

- **Article Type:** Mini review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human trials + *in vivo* animal studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2023.1186927

**Why it's useful:**
This review focuses on mechanisms linking beer, the gut microbiome, and immune function. It discusses how beer compounds such as polyphenols and dietary fibers may influence inflammation, gut barrier integrity, and microbial metabolism.

---

### 5. Impact of Beer and Nonalcoholic Beer Consumption on the Gut Microbiota: A Randomized, Double-Blind, Controlled Trial

Marques C et al. | Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry (2022)

- **Article Type:** Randomized controlled clinical trial
- **Population/Demographic:** Healthy men
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jafc.2c00587

**Why it's useful:**
This controlled trial compared alcoholic and non-alcoholic beer and found that both increased gut microbiota diversity. The findings suggest that beer polyphenols may contribute to microbiome-related benefits, independent of alcohol content.

---

### 6. Beer and Microbiota: Pathways for a Positive and Healthy Interaction

Zugravu CA et al. | Nutrients (2023)

- **Article Type:** Narrative review
- **Population/Demographic:** Human + animal studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu15040844

**Why it's useful:**
A broad overview of how beer components—including polyphenols, fiber, melanoidins, and fermentation-derived compounds—may interact with the gut microbiome. It notes that alcohol presence diminished the benefits from beer compounds (e.g. polyphenols), compared to the non-alcoholic beers.

---

### 7. Ethanol-Induced Dysbiosis and Systemic Impact: A Meta-Analytical Synthesis of Human and Animal Research

Alexandrescu L et al. | Microorganisms (2025)

- **Article Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Population/Demographic:** Human and animal studies
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms13092000

**Why it's useful:**
This summarizes evidence linking ethanol exposure to gut dysbiosis and systemic inflammatory effects. It is valuable because it provides important context on alcohol-related microbiome disruption, helping distinguish potential benefits of beer bioactives from the harmful effects of ethanol.

---

### 8. Effects of moderate beer consumption on immunity and the gut microbiome in immunosuppressed mice

Hu S et al. | Bioscience of Microbiota, Food and Health (2025)

- **Article Type:** Research article
- **Population/Demographic:** Mice study
- **Open Access:** Yes
- **DOI/URL:** https://doi.org/10.12938/bmfh.2024-045

**Why it's useful:**
This study examines both alcoholic and non-alcoholic beer in an immunosuppressed mouse model, linking beer intake to changes in gut microbiota composition, short-chain fatty acid production, intestinal barrier integrity, and immune markers. While it notes certain non-alcoholic components of beer may contribute to microbiome and immune modulation independent of ethanol effects, researchers also note that moderate alcohol intake *may* not be harmful if used in a healthy, controlled manner.
