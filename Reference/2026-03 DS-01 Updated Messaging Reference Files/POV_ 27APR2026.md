**Probiotics (in general info; product-agnostic)**   
*Evidence here is not product evidence and thus should not be used to make claims about our products*

**What are they:** The term *probiotic* has a globally recognized [definition](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrgastro.2014.66): *live microorganisms that, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health benefit on the host.* Viability (‘live’) is important, as is dosage (‘adequate amounts’) and evidence of benefit (‘confer a health benefit’). 

**Transient nature:** ﻿There is a lack of scientific evidence to suggest that most probiotic strains can colonize the gut long-term; instead, current research indicates they are [transient](https://doi.org/10.1002/imt2.253) in nature. This means that probiotics travel through the digestive tract, [interact](https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms12010194) with gut and immune cells, and are then excreted in stool rather than permanently colonizing.

This is largely due to a process called [colonization resistance](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2018.08.041)— the gut is home to a dense, stable community of microbes that resists permanent newcomers from taking up residence.. While some [strains](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2019.05.014) may linger for weeks or even months after intake, lasting colonization appears less common and highly dependent on both the strain and the individual.

Importantly, probiotics don’t need to colonize to be effective; their benefits come from targeted, temporary [interactions](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25116022)—such as [engaging](https://doi.org/10.3390/cells12010184) immune cells, [producing](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcimb.2022.851140) beneficial compounds like short-chain fatty acids ([SCFAs](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu12041107)), [reinforcing](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms22136729) the gut barrier, and [supporting](https://doi.org/10.1007/s13205-019-1841-2) native microbes.

**Strain specificity:** When it comes to probiotics, effects are demonstrated on the [strain level](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fmed.2018.00124/full), and therefore cannot simply be extrapolated across an entire species or genus. Just like a golden retriever and a french bulldog (both categorized as Canis lupus familiaris species) are incredibly different, strains within each species of bacteria can also vary significantly. And strain specification is an important distinction to make, as the utility and function of probiotic organisms is dependent on the strain. 

**How they work**: Probiotics can influence health through several distinct [mechanisms](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25116022) as they move through the gastrointestinal tract, such as:

* **Immune system interactions:** A large portion of the immune system resides in the gut (gut-associated lymphoid tissue, or GALT). Probiotics can [engage](https://doi.org/10.3390/cells12010184) with these immune cells to help modulate immune responses locally.  
* **Targeted metabolite production:** Some strains produce [bacteriocins](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcimb.2022.851140) (which inhibit harmful microbes) or [exopolysaccharides](https://doi.org/10.1007/s13205-019-1841-2) (EPSs), vitamins, and other [compounds](https://doi.org/10.3390/nu12041107) that may help nourish native gut microbes.  
* **Gut motility and signaling:** Probiotics can [influence](https://doi.org/10.3945/an.116.014407) gene expression and neurotransmitter activity in the gut, supporting smooth muscle contractions and potentially promoting more regular bowel movements.  
* **Reinforcing the gut barrier:** probiotics can support the physical barrier between intestinal cells by [supporting](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms22136729) tight junction proteins, reducing the likelihood of microbial translocation (often referred to as “leaky gut”).

**Fermented foods versus *probiotics*:** While fermented foods are certainly nutritious, and may contain live microorganisms, it’s not likely that many of them meet the scientific criteria for a probiotic. Fermented foods, as defined in a recent [consensus statement](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41575-020-00390-5) by scientists at the International Scientific Association for Probiotics and Prebiotics (ISAPP), are “foods made through desired microbial growth and enzymatic conversions of food components.” The term [probiotic](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrgastro.2014.66) is specifically used when there is a demonstrated health benefit conferred by a specific dosage of well-defined and characterized live microorganisms.  

This is not to say that you should avoid fermented options. Many fermented foods and beverages are extremely nutritious (and delicious\!) additions to our daily diets. Instead, it is simply important to note that there is a distinction between fermented foods and probiotics, and the expectations in effect of each. 

**What Is a “Healthy” Gut Microbiome, and Can We Measure It?** 

A common question in microbiome science is [what defines a “healthy” gut microbiome](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2792171/). At present, there is no single answer. [Research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6825832/) has consistently shown that microbiome composition varies widely between individuals, and similar microbial profiles can be associated with different health states. Rather than a fixed definition, the field is moving toward understanding health through the functions the microbiome performs—what microbes are doing, not just which ones are present.

This complexity makes measurement challenging. The gut microbiome is highly dynamic, shifting in response to diet, environment, and other day-to-day factors. As a result, single measurements may reflect only a moment in time, not a stable or complete picture of the ecosystem. [1](https://gut.bmj.com/content/65/3/540), [2](https://gut.bmj.com/content/65/1/57), [3](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/cellular-and-infection-microbiology/articles/10.3389/fcimb.2021.639667/full)

While current stool sample testing can provide insight into microbial composition, it may not fully capture activity throughout the gastrointestinal tract, and interpretation remains an evolving area of research. Clear, causal links between specific microbial patterns and health outcomes are still being established, which makes translating results for a “healthy” microbiome into personalized recommendations difficult.

Microbiome testing is best positioned as a tool for exploration rather than a definitive measure of health based on current capabilities. While it can offer interesting insights, it does not currently provide a complete or fully actionable picture of what a microbiome “should” look like, nor a clear roadmap for individualized intervention, given the sciene isn’t settled.

**The Gut-Brain Axis**

The [gut–brain axis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25830558/) describes the bidirectional communication between the gastrointestinal tract and the central nervous system, mediated through neural, immune, and metabolic pathways. Within this system, the gut microbiome is one of several contributors influencing how signals are produced and transmitted.

A commonly cited example is serotonin. It’s true that a large proportion of the body’s serotonin is produced in the gut. However, this is often misunderstood. [Serotonin](https://doi.org/10.1152/physrev.00018.2018) produced in the gastrointestinal tract primarily acts locally—regulating processes like intestinal motility—and does not directly cross the blood–brain barrier to influence mood.

That said, the story doesn’t end there. The gut and brain remain connected through other pathways, including the vagus nerve and microbial metabolites, which may indirectly influence neurological processes. This has led to growing interest in how the microbiome could play a role in [mood and cognition](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6469458/). Still, these connections are complex, and the extent to which changes in the gut microbiome translate to meaningful effects on mental health is not yet fully understood.

Research on probiotics in this context reflects that complexity. Some studies suggest potential interactions with pathways involved in stress or mood, but findings are mixed, often strain-specific, and not yet generalizable. ([1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31914909/), [2](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10501394/), [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32300799/), [4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0149763421002748)) As such, terms like “[psychobiotics](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31858898/)” are still emerging and not firmly established within clinical practice. 

Overall, while the gut–brain axis—and the microbiome’s role within it—is a compelling area of study, it’s best understood as an evolving field. Simplified narratives (e.g., “most serotonin is made in the gut, therefore gut health determines mood”) do not accurately capture the current state of the science.

**AFU versus CFU**

Seed's DS-01® is specifically formulated, validated, and labeled using AFU (Active Fluorescent Units) methodology, which employs flow cytometry to provide a comprehensive count of all live, viable cells—including those in a viable but non-culturable (VBNC) state, which are live microbes that can still offer functional benefits. When we talk about viable but non-culturable cells (VBNCs), these microbes are metabolically active and retain the potential to exert beneficial effects. Although they may not form colonies in a lab dish, they remain biologically relevant—especially in the context of the gut, where environmental conditions differ dramatically from those in a Petri dish. AFU is a rigorous, precise and widely recognized enumeration method appropriate for multi-strain formulations. 

In contrast, CFU (Colony Forming Units), which is the method traditionally employed, only counts cells that happen to form colonies under specific laboratory plating conditions. 

Higher CFU counts do not [necessarily increase benefits](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28008787/) and the *best* dose is the dose that was most efficacious in clinical human studies.

**Navigating Probiotic and Disease Discussions, and Limited Evidence**

Interest in probiotics for specific conditions continues to grow, but the evidence remains highly variable and context-dependent in almost all cases. Research in this space is often strain- and dose- specific, population-specific, and not consistently reproducible, which makes broad or generalized conclusions difficult.

A key aspect of writing about probiotics in the context of a disease is the potential for implied claims, or (unintentionally) misleading narratives. Even when not stated directly, readers may infer that a probiotic product is intended to treat or support a condition simply because it is discussed in proximity to that condition—which introduces risk especially when the research is inconclusive, has documented effects or recognized caution for said condition. This is especially common in educational content that explores emerging research, as readers can find it difficult to distinguish.

To mitigate this, it’s important to clearly separate general research (what has been studied in the literature), from product context (what a specific formulation has been studied to / is designed to do). 

Not all probiotics are interchangeable, and findings tied to individual strains or study conditions should not be generalized to other products or formulations. Writing should avoid suggesting that research on a given strain, condition, or outcome applies broadly. 

This directly relates to an important theme Seed consistently speaks to—strain specificity. Consistency in messaging is important from a brand POV. 

In addition, even when discussing the research at large (not in the context of a specific product) it’s important to present a well-rounded review of the literature, include caveats or limitations of existing research, and be clear about confirmed (or lack thereof) recommendations for certain populations. 

When evidence is limited or inconsistent, this should be stated plainly. This can look like:

- “*However, this is still an emerging area of research, and more studies are needed to understand the effectiveness and safety in this population.*”   
- “*That said, this is an emerging area of research and findings have been mixed in regards to specific outcomes, with study limitations pointing to the need for additional research. Differences in strains, study designs, and variability in the baseline vaginal microbiome may all play an important role.*”  
- *“Importantly, there are no definitive recommendations for use in this population, given study results can be mixed. More research is needed to understand the specific strain(s) and doses which may be most effective.”*

💡Rather than framing probiotics as solutions for specific diseases, position them within the broader context of ongoing research and mechanistic exploration for the most valuable education. The goal is to inform, without implying clinical application if it has not been established.

**Cobiotics: a New Frontier** \[see more [here](https://seed.com/cultured/co-biotics/), written by Seed’s R\&D team)

Co-biotics \= ingredients that can be used by both human cells and the microbiome for dual impacts

Today, most health products are designed around individual organ systems or physiological functions. Cognition, metabolism, energy, skin, and hormonal support are all treated as separate categories—and “gut health” is often positioned as just one more. But the gut isn’t simply another category. It’s a metabolic command center that helps coordinate how you feel and function on a daily basis.[1](https://doi.org/10.1111/lam.13333) When we support the body without also supporting the microbes that help drive it, we’re only addressing half of our health.[2](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1002533)

Co-biotics are bioactive (affecting your biology) compounds—like vitamins, minerals, or other functional molecules—that can be used by both human cells and the microbes that live within you to support a specific health benefit, like energy, sleep, or nutrition. This dual engagement is what makes co-biotics distinct within the biotics landscape.

Probiotics introduce new live microorganisms to the [gut microbiome](https://seed.com/cultured/microbiome-101/), and prebiotics primarily serve as food for resident gut and probiotic microbes.[3](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrgastro.2014.66),[4](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrgastro.2017.75) Co-biotics, on the other hand, are carefully selected to act on biological processes that are shared—or co-regulated—by you and your microbes.[5](https://doi.org/10.20944/preprints202509.2118.v1) By influencing these shared processes, instead of isolated systems, co-biotics can have amplified effects. (Learn more about the differences between co-biotics and other biotics below.)

Just as a specific strain can be described as probiotic, an individual vitamin, mineral, or bioactive compound can be a co-biotic if it acts on both human and microbial pathways. And just as probiotic strains can be combined into a multi-strain supplement, co-biotic ingredients can be combined into a formulation designed to deliver a targeted health outcome or influence specific metabolic pathways.
