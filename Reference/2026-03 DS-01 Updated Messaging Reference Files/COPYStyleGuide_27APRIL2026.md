## Bacteria + Species

### "Bacteria" (plural) vs. "Bacterium" (singular)

Bacteria are single-cell microorganisms that are found in nearly every environment on Earth. The difference between bacteria and bacterium is that bacteria is the plural form of bacterium. "Bacteria" refers to a group or collection of multiple individual bacterium, while "bacterium" refers to a single, individual microorganism of the bacterial kingdom. For example, if you are talking about a single microorganism, you would say, "This bacterium is responsible for causing the infection." On the other hand, if you are talking about a group of bacteria, you would say "These bacteria are commonly found in soil." So, while the meaning of the terms is essentially the same, bacteria is used to refer to multiple microorganisms, while bacterium is used to refer to a single microorganism. Ensure that any accompanying verbs are conjugated correctly.

### "Species" is both singular and plural.

The main difference between a species and bacteria is that bacteria is a broad classification of single-celled microorganisms that belong to the kingdom Bacteria, while a species is a specific group of closely related, genetically similar organisms that share many genetic and phenotypic factors, and can produce viable offspring. So, while bacteria is a broad classification that refers to a diverse group of microorganisms, species is a more specific category that refers to a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce viable offspring.

Additionally, "species" is typically a plural noun referring to a group of organisms, though it can also be used as a singular (collective) noun, meaning that it refers to a group of things or organisms considered as a single entity.

For example, we can write, "This species of bacteria is commonly found in the oral microbiome," referring to a single type of bacteria. In this context, "species" is being used as a singular noun, even though it is technically a plural form.

When you're writing about species, make sure you understand whether or not you're talking about one or multiple species, and then conjugate the verb(s) accordingly.

### Strain vs. species

Put (very) simply, a bacterial "species" refers to a group of bacteria with shared characteristics that are distinct from other bacterial groups. A "strain" is a subset of a bacterial species that shares certain genetic characteristics not found in other members of the species. Microorganisms (such as viruses, bacteria, and fungi) have many strains within a single species. At Seed, we often emphasize the importance of strain specificity as specific strains within the same species can have diverse outcomes. Whenever possible, try to narrow down the specific strains you're writing about or call out when a study or reference is only referring to a species.

Bacterial strains are often named using a combination of the species name, followed by a specific identifier that distinguishes them from other strains of the same species. For example, E. coli O157:H7 is a strain of Escherichia coli.[^strain]

[^strain]: Riley, M. A., & Lizotte-Waniewski, M. (2009). Population genomics and the bacterial species concept. Methods in molecular biology (Clifton, N.J.), 532, 367–377. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-60327-853-9_21

## Microbiome vs. Microbiota

Microbiota and microbiome are terms that are often used interchangeably, but they actually refer to different things:

Microbiota refers to the collection of microorganisms that live in a particular environment, such as the human body. These microorganisms can include bacteria, fungi, viruses, and other microbes. Microbiome, on the other hand, refers to the entire genetic material of all the microorganisms that live in a particular environment, including their genes, proteins, and other molecules.

So while microbiota refers to the actual microorganisms themselves, microbiome refers to their collective genetic material and the interactions between those genes and the host organism. We use microbiome to refer to the entire ecology, and we use microbiota when we are specifically referencing the microbes within a specific ecology.

Here are a few examples:

- *The microbiota in your gut includes millions of different bacteria and other tiny organisms that help you digest your food and stay healthy.*
- *Your skin is covered in a diverse microbiota of bacteria and fungi that help protect you from harmful pathogens.*
- *The microbiome of a coral reef is incredibly complex, with thousands of different species of microbes working together to maintain the health of the ecosystem.*
- *Scientists are studying the microbiome of the human body to learn more about how the microorganisms that live inside us affect our health and well-being.*
- *Even though the microbiota in different parts of the body may be very different, they all contribute to the overall microbiome of the organism.*

## Quotation Marks

In headers, we use single quotation marks. In body paragraphs, we use double quotation marks.

Here is an example:

*Goop says DS-01® is 'the absolute best'*

*In an interview on Monday, Gwyneth Paltrow's company and platform Goop stated that Seed's DS-01® Daily Synbiotic is "the absolute best probiotic product on the market."*

## Em Dashes

Ah, the em dash. We really do love our em dashes. Because of this, we almost never use punctuation like semicolons (;) to break up longer sentences.

Here is an example:

- You are not separate from Earth—you're in symbiosis with it.

We also do not include spaces on either end of the em dash.

Here is an example:

- Coral reefs are important ecosystems—they contain 25% of marine life. NOT Coral reefs are important ecosystems — they contain 25% of marine life.

> Please also be mindful in checking that em dashes are being used for this purpose, rather than en dashes (used for displaying a range of numbers, like "ages 3–17") or hyphens (used for hyphenated words).

## Exclamation Points

Should be used rarely and with intent. They can quickly undermine Seed's warm authority by feeling overly casual, sensational, or cheap. Default to periods for confidence and clarity. If an exclamation is considered, it should serve a precise purpose, for example, signaling celebration in community-driven or light-touch contexts (social engagement, limited internal comms). In most cases, a sharper rewrite will convey more impact than punctuation.

**Do this:** DS-01® Daily Synbiotic is scientifically validated to deliver live and active bacterial strains to the gut microbiome, where they can interact with your resident microbes to confer health benefits to their host (that's you!).

**Not this:** 30% off is ending soon! Act fast!

## Citation Style

We use the APA citation style for all of our sources, except in the very *limited* case of our Strains Mapped to Studies chart on our /reference pages (which uses the NLM citation style due to compliance reasons).

A standard APA citation looks like this:

Denhart, H. (2008). Deconstructing barriers: Perceptions of students labeled with learning disabilities in higher education. *Journal of Learning Disabilities*, *41*(6), 483–497. doi: 10.1177/0022219408321151

You can easily make free APA citations on bibme.org, but to note, it is a little slow and bogged down by ads.

## Citation Location

### In Text

Citations should come AFTER punctuation (periods, commas, etc), *except in the case of em dashes*.

Here is an example:
- Ex. *Science is cool.*¹ NOT *Science is cool*¹.
- Ex. *Science is cool*¹—*especially biology.* NOT *Science is cool*—¹ *especially biology.*

### In Citation List (at the end of the page)

In the citation list, there should be a space between the superscript and the copy.

Here is an example:
- ¹ Indoor Air Quality. (2021, September 7). US EPA. https://www.epa.gov/report-environment/indoor-air-quality
- NOT: ¹Indoor Air Quality. (2021, September 7). US EPA. https://www.epa.gov/report-environment/indoor-air-quality

## Title and Header Capitalization

Please use AP Style for title and header capitalization. In titles or headers that are not complete sentences, each main word should be capitalized (including both words in a hyphenated word), and the "little" words (three letters and shorter) should be lowercase. Note: To-be verbs are the one exception to this rule. 'Is' and 'Are' should always be capitalized in a headline.

Examples:
- AP Style: Trust Your Gut-Brain Axis (*all words are capitalized in this header, including the hyphenated word*)
- Not AP: News From the Seed-verse (*all words are capitalized in this header*)
- Not AP: Can probiotics boost vaginal health? (*only the first word is capitalized since this is a stand-alone sentence*)

## Times and Time Zones

### Time Zones

When writing times, please use a.m. or p.m. to denote time (note the periods). Please also include the time zone in the format of the following:

- PT (Pacific Time)
- MT (Mountain Time)
- CT (Central Time)
- ET (Eastern Time)

Defer to AP Style Guide on other rules:
- Use a single space between the numeral and abbreviation: 8 a.m.
- Drop :00 for whole hours
- Use the word "to" instead of an en dash for ranges. (8 a.m. to 2 p.m.)
- Avoid using "12 a.m." and "12 p.m." when possible; instead use "midnight" or "noon" for clarity

### Per Year / Month / Day

When writing out per year, per month, per day (etc), please use the following format (note the spaces on either side of the slash, full spelling, and lowercase):

- / year
- / month
- / day

For example:
- $49.99 / month

## Emojis 🌱

Emojis can add clarity, context, or relevance — but only when they truly earn their place. Use them sparingly, and only where they make sense: social, internal comms, and the occasional subject line. Before you drop one in, ask: Does this emoji add meaning that isn't already clear? Or could a sharper rewrite do the job better?

### Punctuation First, Emoji Second

When using an emoji, the punctuation (period, comma, exclamation mark) comes before the emoji. There is a space between the punctuation and the emoji, like in the following:

So unlike the other Vegas, what happens in this Vagus, doesn't stay there. 🫠

… Except when it comes to lists.

However, if you are using emojis in a list, the emoji would come *before* the comma, like in the following:

To support your microbiome, prioritize movement 🏃, a diet rich in fiber 🥦, and a consistent sleep schedule. 💤

## Bacteria Strain Rules

Whenever we refer to specific bacteria (whether that's in reference to the probiotics in our products or other bacteria that we are highlighting), they need proper italicization and capitalization. The *genus* and *species* should be italicized, while the strain is not italicized. Additionally, the first letter of the genus is capitalized while the species is never capitalized. The strain is typically all caps, but some strains have exceptions.

Here are some examples:
- *Bifidobacterium breve* SD-BR3-IT (a strain from the digestive blend in DS-01®)
- *Bifidobacterium longum* HRVD90b-US (note here that one letter in the strain is not capitalized—this is fine, it just depends on the strain)
- *Ophiocordyceps unilateralis* (not all bacteria have strains; that's typically reserved for probiotics)

## Numbers

- Spell out zero to nine, use numerals for 10+, but never start a sentence with a number and always use numerals in headlines / titles
- Use numerals for:
  - Time (e.g., 3 days, 6 months)
  - Dosage (e.g., 2 capsules)
  - Supply duration (e.g., 3-month supply)

## CTAs

Title case, always. Aim for brevity and specificity. What is the action you want the person reading to take? In cases where there are multiple CTAs in one piece of content, (like an email), aim to differentiate each CTA to focus on where the customer will be directed to or to optimize click through. For example, if you said 'Start DS-01®' in the hero module, use the second module CTA to mention that it's also on sale: 'Save 30% on DS-01®'.

- Like this: Start DS-01®, Learn More, Read Cultured, Shop Now, Enroll Now
- Not this: Explore more probiotics for you and your microbiome

## Plus Signs and Ampersands

Plus signs should always be used when referencing our product names in full: AM-02™ Focus + Energy, PM-02™ Sleep + Restore, or the phrases 'For You + Your Microbiome' and 'Probiotic + Prebiotic' when appearing in a headline. Outside of those use cases, aim to use 'and' when space allows. In cases where character counts are both fixed and limited, use a plus sign, never an ampersand.\*

For example:
- SMS (160 characters / 70 max when using special characters)
- Mobile PDP descriptions
- PLP product descriptions
- Amazon FAQs or live text descriptions (200 characters total)
- Banners (40 characters)
- Video supers

\*Note: always use 'and' in a CTA over a plus.

## Lists, Bullets, Colons

**Lists:**
Use Title Case for short phrases.
Use Sentence case for full-sentence items.

**Bullets:**
Use colons to introduce lists if the intro is a full sentence.
Avoid using both colons and "•" before lists—pick one. Reserve "•" for visual emphasis in packaging.

**Punctuation:**
End bullet items with periods only if full sentences.
No punctuation for fragments.

## "We" or "Seed"

Our default voice is collective. When writing as the brand, defer to the first-person plural "We" to convey humanity, partnership, and shared pursuit. "We" makes the brand feel less like a faceless company and more like a team of people dedicated to the work.

Use "We" when speaking about what we do, believe, or build:
- *We exist to bridge the gap between science and everyday life.*
- *We design for sustainability from the start.*

Use "Seed" when clarity, attribution, or formality is required:
- *In scientific or press contexts: Published by Seed Health.*
- *In legal or compliance copy: Seed is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent disease.*
- *Seed products are held to a rigorous testing standard.*

When differentiating the brand from the audience: Seed members receive early access.

Think of it this way:
- "We" = our voice, our ethos, our relationship with the reader.
- "Seed" = the entity, the company, the formal name.
