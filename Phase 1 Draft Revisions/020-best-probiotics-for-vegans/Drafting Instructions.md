# Strategic Analysis Context

## Seed SciCare POV Summary

### Key Seed Perspectives Found

- **Fermented foods vs. probiotics**: Seed strictly distinguishes "Live Dietary Microbes (LDM)" from probiotics. Fermented foods (kimchi, sauerkraut, miso, etc.) introduce bacterial diversity but do NOT meet the scientific definition of probiotics. They lack strain specificity, measured dosages, and demonstrated benefits for specific health outcomes. The current article handles this well but could be strengthened with even clearer LDM framing.

- **Probiotics are transient, not colonizing**: Seed emphasizes that probiotics do not permanently settle in the gut. Benefits come from their functional support while present, not permanent colonization. The article already includes this messaging, which is a strength.

- **AFU vs. CFU**: Seed's position is that AFU (Active Fluorescent Units) is a "more precise" measurement than CFU. The article already covers this, but competitors completely ignore this distinction, making it a differentiator worth strengthening.

- **Micronutrient-microbiota bidirectional axis**: Seed has strong scientific messaging on how gut bacteria enable nutrient absorption AND how nutrients shape microbiota composition. This is highly relevant for vegans who may have B12, iron, and zinc absorption concerns. The current article barely touches this angle.

- **Never call probiotics "supplements"**: Seed compliance requires using "probiotics" or "probiotic products" instead of "supplements." The current article mostly avoids this, but the drafter should remain vigilant.

### Conflicts Identified

- **Competitors casually recommend fermented foods as "probiotic" sources**: All four competitors (Medical News Today, VegNews, Olives for Dinner, Optibac) frame fermented foods as probiotic sources without adequately distinguishing them from clinically validated probiotics. The Seed article already differentiates well, but this framing could be sharpened further.

- **"Boost immunity" language**: Competitors use language like "enhanced immune system" (VegNews). Seed must NEVER use "boost immunity" or variations. Approved language: "supports healthy immune function," "helps maintain/support the immune system."

- **Die-off reactions**: Some general probiotic content references "die-off reactions." Seed's stance is this is scientifically unsubstantiated for probiotics. The current article correctly avoids this.

- **Sourdough bread as a probiotic**: Olives for Dinner lists sourdough bread as a probiotic food, which is scientifically inaccurate (live cultures don't survive baking). If this topic ever arises, Seed should not endorse it.

### Topics to Handle Carefully

- **B12 and vegan nutrient concerns**: Seed has extensive data on gut bacteria producing B12 and the micronutrient-microbiota axis. This is a powerful angle for vegan audiences, but claims must be framed carefully within compliance guidelines (support, not treat or cure).

- **"Probiotic supplements" terminology**: Must use "probiotics" or "probiotic products" per Seed compliance. Never "supplements."

- **No-no words to avoid**: "boost immunity," "microflora" (use microbiota), "maximum strength," "guaranteed live delivery" (use "engineered to safeguard bacteria through digestion"), "colonization" (use "presence/action of beneficial microorganisms"), and AI-detected cliches like "let's dive," "game-changer," "groundbreaking."
