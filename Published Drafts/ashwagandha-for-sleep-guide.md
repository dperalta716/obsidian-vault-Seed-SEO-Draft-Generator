# Ashwagandha for Sleep: How This Adaptogen Supports Restful Nights

### **Overview**

- Ashwagandha ( _Withania somnifera_) is an adaptogenic herb used for centuries in Ayurvedic traditions to help the body manage stress.
- Modern science reveals its sleep benefits are closely linked to its ability to help balance cortisol, your body’s primary stress hormone, which should naturally be low at night.
- Clinical research on specific, standardized ashwagandha extracts shows it can improve sleep quality, help you fall asleep faster, and reduce nighttime awakenings.
- Unlike many supplements, the effectiveness of ashwagandha for sleep is tied to a precise, clinically studied dose—not just taking more. It works with your body’s natural rhythms rather than overriding them.
- For a well-rounded approach, ashwagandha works best as part of a system that also supports other aspects of your sleep-wake cycle, like melatonin production and the gut-brain axis.

Tossing and turning, staring at the ceiling, mentally replaying your day—sound familiar? When a good night’s rest feels out of reach, it’s easy to get caught up in the search for a quick fix. We’ve all been there, scrolling through an endless list of solutions, many of which promise to knock you out, but leave you feeling groggy and “off” the next day.

The truth is, quality sleep isn’t just about being unconscious for eight hours. It’s an active, restorative process that your mind and body depend on. And while many turn to high-dose sleep aids, the science is pointing toward a more thoughtful strategy: working _with_ your body’s natural rhythms, not against them.

This is where ashwagandha enters the conversation. Yes, this ancient herb can be a powerful ally for sleep, but its real strength lies in _how_ it works. Rather than forcing sedation, it helps your body recalibrate. 😴

Let’s explore the science behind how ashwagandha can support more restorative sleep.

## **What Exactly Is Ashwagandha?**

Ashwagandha ( _Withania somnifera_) is a small, evergreen shrub native to India and Southeast Asia. You might also hear it called “Indian ginseng” or “winter cherry.”

For over 3,000 years, its roots and berries have been a staple of Ayurveda, the traditional system of medicine in India, where it’s revered for its restorative properties.

### **An Adaptogen with Ancient Roots**

At its core, ashwagandha is classified as an adaptogen. Think of adaptogens as your body’s personal stress-management coaches. They’re a unique class of herbs and mushrooms that can help your body adapt to physical, chemical, and biological stressors. [1](https://doi.org/10.1111/nyas.13399)

Instead of targeting a single pathway, research suggests they work systemically to help bring your body back into balance, or what scientists call “homeostasis.” Research shows that ashwagandha’s sleep-promoting effects involve multiple pathways, including regulation of the **hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA)** axis, **GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)** receptors, and other hormones involved in the stress response, such as cortisol. [2](https://nutritionandmetabolism.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12986-025-00902-7), [3](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.6466)

**👉 TL;DR:** Ashwagandha doesn’t just force you to feel sleepy. It helps address one of the underlying reasons you might be struggling to wind down in the first place: stress.

## **The Science-Backed Link Between Ashwagandha, Stress, and Sleep**

You’ve likely experienced it: after a stressful day, your mind refuses to switch off, replaying conversations and to-do lists.

This isn’t just in your head—your body’s biological response is driven by stress hormones, particularly cortisol.

### **Understanding the Cortisol Connection**

Cortisol is your body’s main stress hormone. It’s necessary for survival, triggering your “fight or flight” response and keeping you focused. Your body produces cortisol in a natural daily cycle, or diurnal rhythm (i.e., it follows a day-night pattern), to keep you focused when you need to be and more relaxed when you don’t. Think of it like your body’s built-in ‘go’ signal.

Ideally, cortisol levels are highest in the morning to help you wake up and feel energized, and they gradually decline throughout the day, reaching their lowest point at night to allow your body to relax and sleep. [4](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.psyneuen.2017.05.018)

But modern life—with its constant notification pings, late-night screen time, and daily pressures—can disrupt this natural rhythm. When you’re chronically stressed, your cortisol levels can remain elevated in the evening, essentially telling your body to “stay ready!” when it should be winding down.

This hormonal imbalance is a common culprit behind difficulty falling asleep and staying asleep. (Ever notice how Sunday nights feel especially restless before a busy week? That’s cortisol working overtime. 😅)

### **How Ashwagandha Helps Regulate Cortisol for Better Sleep**

Here’s where ashwagandha’s adaptogenic properties come in handy. Instead of simply masking the problem, it helps your body recalibrate its stress response.

One study examined the effects of a specific, purified extract of ashwagandha (Shoden®) in healthy adults experiencing high levels of stress. Participants who took 120 mg of the ashwagandha extract daily showed a 66-67% reduction in morning cortisol levels compared to placebo. [5](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405844024129167)

By helping to balance cortisol, ashwagandha can support your body’s natural transition into a restful state, making it easier to unwind and prepare for sleep.

## **More Than Just Stress: How Ashwagandha Directly Improves Sleep Quality**

While its cortisol-balancing act is a plus, research shows that ashwagandha does more than just help you relax before bed. Clinical studies reveal it directly improves several key markers of sleep quality.

### **Falling Asleep Faster and Staying Asleep Longer**

In another clinical trial, researchers investigated the effects of the same 120 mg dose of Shoden® on healthy adults who reported non-restorative sleep.

The study found improvements across the board for the ashwagandha group:

- **Improved Sleep Quality:** Participants reported a 72% average increase in sleep quality scores, compared to just 29% in the placebo group.
- **Faster Sleep Onset:** They fell asleep faster, with a decrease in what’s known as sleep onset latency.
- **Fewer Awakenings:** They experienced fewer and shorter awakenings during the night (a lower wake after sleep onset, or WASO).
- **Increased Total Sleep Time:** They slept longer overall. [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.sleep.2020.03.012)

These findings show that ashwagandha helps you fall asleep _and_ supports deeper, more consolidated, more restorative sleep.

### **Why the Form and Dose Matter**

So, does this mean any ashwagandha supplement will work the same way? Not necessarily. The results seen in these studies are linked to a specific, standardized extract at a precise dose.

“People often think more is better,” says [Dirk Gevers, Ph.D](https://seed.com/cultured/expert-reviewer/dirk-gevers/?srsltid=AfmBOorc3xaYYpAbiRVEyQ8mWc0Lz5wCJXRRetlNFExZoXffrrKXDjjw), Seed’s Chief Scientific Officer. “With ashwagandha, it’s about precision: the right extract at the right dose to support your body’s natural stress and sleep rhythms.”

Standardization ensures that each dose contains a consistent amount of the active compounds, called **withanolides**, which are responsible for the herb’s benefits. The clinical research on Shoden® used a 120 mg dose, showing that this specific amount is effective for supporting both stress and sleep.

Older research however has shown a more clear dose-response relationship for the other potential advantages of ashwagandha such as improved blood glucose (i.e., blood sugar) and lipid profiles (such as [cholesterol levels](https://seed.com/cultured/probiotics-cholesterol-heart-health-support-guide/)).

For example, one study tested 125 mg, 250 mg, and 500 mg daily doses, where all groups saw improvements across stress and sleep quality measures. However, the 250mg and 500mg doses showed additional positive changes to blood glucose and lipid levels not seen at the lowest dose. [7](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/242151370)

The 250 mg and 500 mg groups also saw benefits to blood sugar and lipid levels in this study. This suggests there may be more benefits of ashwagandha beyond sleep in higher doses.

🔬 **Science Translation:** Think of ashwagandha dosing like a key to a lock. You need the right one to unlock the door—not just any key or the biggest key you can find.

## **A Multi-System Approach: Why Ashwagandha Is Part of a Bigger Picture**

Truly restorative sleep is an art that years of human evolution has worked to master. It’s a carefully orchestrated biological symphony conducted by hormones, neurotransmitters, and your internal body clock.

Ashwagandha is a valuable instrument in this orchestra, but it performs best as part of an ensemble. 🎼

### **Calibrating Your Circadian Rhythm**

Your [sleep-wake cycle, or circadian rhythm](https://seed.com/cultured/your-microbiome-has-its-own-circadian-clock/), is governed by the balance of light and darkness and regulated by key hormones like cortisol and melatonin. While ashwagandha can help lower the “daytime” or “stress” hormone (cortisol) at night, melatonin acts as the “nighttime” hormone, signaling to your brain that it’s time for sleep. [8](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405844024002974)

A systematic review found that ashwagandha extract had a small but meaningful effect on overall sleep quality compared to placebo. [9](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0257843) Think of it as gently recalibrating your system, not flipping a switch.

A formula that combines ashwagandha with a physiological dose of melatonin can provide comprehensive support for your circadian rhythm. This combination helps to quiet the “stay awake” signals _and_ amplify “go to sleep” signals.

### **The Role of the Gut-Brain Axis in Sleep**

Growing science also points to a meaningful connection between your gut microbiome and your sleep quality, known as the gut-brain axis. [10](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyt.2018.00669) Your gut microbes can produce neurotransmitters and other compounds that influence sleep, including GABA, which has a calming effect on the nervous system.

This connection is particularly relevant for ashwagandha, since research shows that its sleep-inducing effects are partially mediated through GABAergic pathways. [11](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21369449/)

It combines 120 mg of clinically studied Shoden® ashwagandha with bioidentical melatonin (meaning it’s chemically identical to the melatonin your body naturally produces) in a dual-phase release system, alongside microbiome-supporting ingredients like [prebiotics](https://seed.com/cultured/what-do-prebiotics-do/) and GABA.

## **The Key Insight**

The most effective way to use ashwagandha for sleep isn’t about taking the highest possible dose to force sedation—it’s about precision. The science supports using a specific, clinically studied extract at a validated dose to help your body recalibrate its own natural stress and sleep cycles.

By gently balancing cortisol and working in sync with your circadian rhythm, ashwagandha can help support a deeper, more restorative sleep, so you can wake up feeling refreshed, recharged, and ready for the day. 🌱

## **Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)**

### **How Long Does It Take for Ashwagandha to Work for Sleep?**

**The primary sleep benefits of ashwagandha build over time, with improvements appearing after several weeks of consistent, daily use.** While some effects, like a sense of calm, may be felt sooner, clinical studies show meaningful improvements in sleep quality and cortisol balance after several weeks of consistent, daily use. [6](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.sleep.2020.03.012)

Think of it as gently recalibrating your system, not flipping a switch.

### **What Is the Best Form of Ashwagandha for Sleep?**

**Look for a standardized extract.** Standardization ensures you’re getting a consistent and potent dose of the active compounds ( **withanolides**). Clinically studied extracts like Shoden® have been specifically researched for their effects on sleep and stress at a particular dose (120 mg), providing a higher level of scientific confidence.

### **Can You Take Ashwagandha and Melatonin Together?**

**Yes, ashwagandha and melatonin work on complementary pathways to support your sleep-wake cycle.** Ashwagandha helps balance cortisol to reduce “awake” signals, while melatonin helps increase “sleep” signals.

### **What Are the Side Effects of Taking Ashwagandha at Night?**

**Ashwagandha is generally well-tolerated when taken at recommended doses.** In clinical trials, adverse effects in the ashwagandha groups were comparable to those in the placebo groups and included mild symptoms such as nasal congestion, [constipation](https://seed.com/cultured/probiotics-constipation-relief-guide/), and drowsiness. [12](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/ashwagandha#:~:text=In%20some%20individuals%2C%20ashwagandha%20preparations,not%20be%20used%20while%20breastfeeding.) It’s always best to consult with your healthcare provider before starting any new supplement regimen, especially if you’re pregnant, breastfeeding, or have a pre-existing medical condition. [12](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/ashwagandha#:~:text=In%20some%20individuals%2C%20ashwagandha%20preparations,not%20be%20used%20while%20breastfeeding.)
