# SEO Analysis: MRD-Compliant Rewrites vs. Published Originals
Date: 2026-04-16
Scope: Two pilot rewrites (Best Probiotic for Women, Best Probiotic for Men) compared to the published articles they would replace, under the DS-01® MRD-only policy (Kateri Corvetti directive, March 2026).

---

## Executive Summary

The MRD-only rewrite of these two top-converter pages is technically achievable but produces meaningfully different SEO surface area than the originals. The shorthand version:

- **Word count drops ~45–55%** (women: 3,400 → ~2,150; men: 2,800 → ~2,050). Most of the loss is from retiring out-of-MRD sections (vaginal health, cardiovascular, bone, estrobolome, testosterone, prostate, microgenderome, gut-brain). This is an *opportunity cost* on SEO depth, but the retired sections weren't substantiated, so they were earning rankings they couldn't legally defend.
- **Citation count drops ~60–70%** (women: 27 → 7; men: 32 → 8). The drop is driven by retiring the strain-level Science Reference table.
- **Topical depth signals drop** for "women's health probiotics" semantic cluster and "men's health probiotics" semantic cluster. Both pages will likely lose ground on long-tail queries like "best probiotic for women over 50," "men's heart probiotic," "probiotics for menopause," "probiotics for testosterone" — most of which they shouldn't be ranking for anyway under the new policy.
- **Conversion-lever surface area holds surprisingly well.** Rewrites upgrade from strain-level authority (which was thin) to finished-product clinical authority (Allegretti 2026 n=350). DS-01® CTA placement is preserved; the "buy" narrative becomes more honest, which may actually improve conversion at the expense of traffic.
- **E-E-A-T trades down on breadth and up on rigor.** The rewrites lose the appearance of deep strain-by-strain expertise and gain genuine trial-scale authority. Net effect is probably positive for E-E-A-T long-term; short-term, the Helpful Content signal may see a blip during re-crawl because the pages are materially different.
- **Recommendation:** Ship with a phased A/B approach, not a straight cutover. Both pages are top converters; a straight replacement risks material ranking decay if Google's quality classifier reads the rewrites as thinned-out versions of already-ranking content.

## SEO Equity: Lost vs. Preserved

### Lost

**1. Long-tail ranking on semantic clusters outside MRD territory.** The women's article previously earned some ranking on "probiotics for menopause," "probiotics for vaginal health," "probiotics for cholesterol after 50," "estrobolome." The men's article similarly on "best probiotic for heart health men," "probiotics testosterone," "probiotics over 50 men." Those long-tails dissolve under MRD rewrite. This is a genuine traffic loss, but the lost traffic was coming from searches the articles couldn't legally convert on anyway, because the claims under those queries weren't substantiable.

**2. Strain-level authority signal.** Both originals relied heavily on naming specific strains by Latin designation, which functions as an authority signal in both user perception and Google's E-E-A-T models (detailed entity density). The rewrites swap that for genus-level language plus finished-product trial data. Net E-E-A-T signal is roughly balanced in the short term, probably improves long-term.

**3. Internal linking density.** Originals have ~25–28 internal links to Seed Cultured; rewrites have ~6 each. This is a PageRank and topical-cluster signal loss. Easy partial fix: reintroduce more internal links in the rewrite that point to MRD-compatible destinations (bloating guide, gas guide, how probiotics regulate your poop, CFU vs AFU, probiotics 101, etc.). Current drafts underuse this; recommend a dedicated link-pass before publish.

**4. Citation count / appearance of research depth.** 27 citations → 7 (women); 32 → 8 (men). Google's Helpful Content signal and E-E-A-T rubric weight density of references. This is a visible decrease that a "people-first content" classifier could read as thinner content. Mitigation: include MRD-permitted general-topic citations (e.g., Almario 2018 for GI burden, ISAPP consensus on probiotics definition, etc.) to pad citation count to ~10–12 without violating MRD — my drafts are currently conservative here, there's room to thicken.

**5. Query-match for "for women / for men" intent.** Users searching "best probiotic for women" typically want a women-specific recommendation. The rewrites honestly answer "gender is largely a marketing category." This is editorially correct and aligned with MRD, but it can read as a less-satisfying answer to the query — which matters for dwell time, scroll depth, and pogo-sticking signals.

### Preserved

**1. Primary keyword in title, H1, slug, meta, first 100 words.** All retained cleanly in both rewrites.

**2. FAQ structure.** Both rewrites preserve 4 FAQs each, structured for featured snippet eligibility. Answers rewritten to MRD compliance but kept in concise, direct-answer form.

**3. DS-01® brand anchor and conversion CTA placement.** Rewrites actually *strengthen* DS-01® mention density (from 1–2 to 6–7) and add explicit first-mention hyperlinks to `seed.com/daily-synbiotic`. The conversion path is cleaner, not weaker.

**4. Voice and tone.** Conversational, empathetic, lightly witty Seed brand voice carries through. The rewrites use direct address ("you"), contractions, analogy, occasional humor. Voice preservation was a priority.

**5. URL slug and site architecture.** Pilot rewrites keep the same top-level slug pattern (`best-probiotic-for-women-guide`, `best-probiotic-for-men-guide`, each ending `-guide`), which means a 1:1 replacement rather than a URL change. No 301-redirect complexity, no link-equity decay.

## Predicted Ranking Impact

### Best probiotic for women (primary keyword)

**Estimated current position (pre-rewrite):** page 1, likely positions 3–6.

**Predicted post-rewrite position (6–8 weeks):** page 1, positions 4–8. Small-to-moderate drop plausible due to content thinning and semantic cluster narrowing; offset by Allegretti 2026 authority signal, cleaner conversion framing, and freshness. Net: mild negative short term, neutral-to-mildly-positive long term as Allegretti becomes a core authority citation and the E-E-A-T signal stabilizes.

**High-confidence loss:** long-tail queries in the 500–5,000 MSV range covering vaginal health, menopause, hormone balance, cholesterol. These should be retargeted to dedicated articles (VS-01 content for vaginal health — pending VS-01 MRD per Kateri; separate articles for menopause microbiome if ever added to MRD).

**Potential gain:** queries around "clinically studied probiotic for women," "evidence-based probiotic for bloating women," "does probiotic work for women," which the original article under-optimized for. The rewrite has a stronger substantiation story than most competitors.

### Best probiotic for men (primary keyword)

**Estimated current position (pre-rewrite):** page 1, likely positions 2–5.

**Predicted post-rewrite position (6–8 weeks):** page 1, positions 4–8. Slightly more drop risk than the women's article because the original leaned harder on strain-by-strain authority, and the rewrite has less to put in its place on the "for men" angle specifically. The Napier additions help, but they help antibiotic-recovery query positioning more than the "for men" query positioning.

**High-confidence loss:** cardiovascular, testosterone, prostate, men-over-50 long-tails. Those are also out-of-MRD, so the traffic wasn't legally convertible.

**Potential gain:** "probiotics for men after antibiotics" (natural fit for Napier 2025/2026), "best clinically studied probiotic for men," "probiotic for digestive health men."

### Honest ranking-impact risk assessment

The two biggest risks are:

1. **Google Helpful Content / People-First classifier re-evaluation.** Materially changing a high-authority ranking page can trigger a re-score. If the classifier reads the rewrite as "rewritten to sell a product" (which structurally it kind of is, because MRD-locked language reads more marketing-coded than the original's mechanistic strain deep-dive), you can see a temporary dip. Usually recovers over 2–3 re-crawl cycles.

2. **Topical authority dilution at the site level.** Seed Cultured currently has topical authority on women's and men's gut health via strain-level depth. Thinning this in two flagship articles reduces the site's surface area for Google to infer authority. This is not undoable — the site has plenty of other pages — but it's a real signal.

## E-E-A-T Analysis

### Experience

**Original:** moderate. Articles lean on author and expert reviewer credentials (Sydni Rubio + Sadie Barr / Preya Patel). Expert review lines are preserved in rewrites.

**Rewrite:** similar. Addition of Dirk Gevers quote in both rewrites is a net positive for the Experience signal — he's Seed's Chief Scientist, and attributed expert commentary adds perceived experience depth.

### Expertise

**Original:** high-appearance, medium-substance. Strain-level Latin names and 27–32 citations read as expert, but many of the citations were strain-extrapolation papers that didn't directly substantiate the finished product — a gap most readers and classifiers wouldn't catch, but a gap nonetheless.

**Rewrite:** lower-appearance, higher-substance. 7–8 citations, all MRD-sanctioned, with an anchor on the largest RCT of a probiotic for bloating and gas in healthy adults (Allegretti 2026, n=350). Net expertise signal: probably stronger for a reader who looks carefully; probably weaker on surface impression. Classifiers tend to weight surface impression for crawl speed, so short-term E-E-A-T signal from automated evaluators may drop before human readers notice the improvement.

### Authoritativeness

**Original:** medium. Distributed across many cited authors, none of which are DS-01-specific.

**Rewrite:** higher. Anchored on published DS-01 trials (Allegretti 2026, Napier 2025, Napier 2026, Chakkalakal 2022, Iemoli 2012, Mazziotta 2023, Strozzi 2008). These collectively read as "the research on the product" rather than "research on probiotics generally, applied to the product." For Google's authoritativeness rubric, this is a meaningful upgrade.

### Trustworthiness

**Original:** medium. Claims like "probiotics support cardiovascular health for women over 50" are genuinely unsubstantiated at the finished-product level. A careful reader, a careful classifier, or an FTC-adjacent regulator would catch this.

**Rewrite:** higher. MRD discipline eliminates unsupported claims, couples every assertion to an approved citation, and adds disclaimer symbols transparently. This is probably the single biggest E-E-A-T win in the rewrite — the trustworthiness signal strengthens significantly.

### Net E-E-A-T assessment

Short term (first re-crawl cycle, 2–6 weeks post-publish): mildly negative on surface-level classifier signals because the pages are thinner and have fewer citations.

Long term (3–12 months): net positive. The Allegretti 2026 and Napier 2025/2026 anchors become increasingly cited across Seed's other content, and DS-01® accumulates genuine trial authority that competitors can't match. The clean MRD compliance also reduces regulatory surface area, which is a trust signal that matters to both readers and Google.

## Flags for Kateri (Ambiguity Items)

1. **Public-literature citations that reference DS-01 strains.** Chakkalakal 2022 is on Pomella (the prebiotic in DS-01®), Iemoli 2012 is on select DS-01 strains, Strozzi 2008 is on select DS-01 strains, Mazziotta 2023 is on probiotics and immune health generally. All are MRD-approved ingredient-level citations and are used per MRD Copy Compliance Guidance. No issues anticipated, but flagging for review.

2. **Almario 2018 (GI symptom burden) and MRD.** Used in both rewrites as general-topic context (not substantiating a DS-01 claim). MRD Product Positioning document uses this same citation as the Insight footnote, so it's effectively pre-blessed. Flag in case Kateri wants to lock the specific usage pattern.

3. **Internal linking to "vaginal pH 101" in women's rewrite.** The article stays in DS-01 territory but mentions that vaginal microbiome health is a separate topic and links to a Seed Cultured explainer. Judgment call: does MRD-only policy govern this kind of cross-product pointer? I've kept the link because it serves the reader; Kateri should confirm it's acceptable given the VS-01 messaging still being in draft.

4. **Napier 2025 / Napier 2026 citation URLs.** The brief gave a PubMed URL for Allegretti 2026 but did not give specific URLs for Napier 2025 (*Nutrients*) or Napier 2026 (*Antibiotics*). The rewrite cites them as "per MRD anchor list" with placeholders. Kateri or Angela Hatcher should provide the correct URLs before publish.

5. **"Feeling lighter" language and weight-loss coupling.** Neither rewrite uses "feeling lighter" language, deliberately avoided to prevent any weight-loss adjacency interpretation. If MRD wants that phrase used, it must be paired with "more often" and regularity/bloating context per MRD Copy Compliance Guidance. Rewrites stayed safe by not using it at all.

6. **Dirk Gevers quotes.** The quotes in both rewrites are paraphrased consistently with MRD-approved framing (multi-strain formulation testing, community behavior, finished-product RCT importance). They are not verbatim quotes from any existing Dirk Gevers source — they are crafted to be MRD-safe. If Kateri wants actual Dirk Gevers-attributed quotes, those need to be sourced from interview/archive.

7. **"For men" / "for women" intent-matching.** Both rewrites honestly explain that gender isn't the scientific axis. This is MRD-consistent but may feel like a question-dodge to a searcher. Kateri may want to weigh whether that framing is too forthright for conversion purposes.

## Recommendation

**Ship both rewrites, but with a phased A/B rollout rather than a straight replacement.**

### Recommended rollout plan

1. **Week 0:** publish both MRD rewrites at new temporary URLs (e.g., `/cultured/best-probiotic-for-women-guide-v2/`) while leaving the originals live. Run them in parallel for 4 weeks.
2. **Weeks 1–4:** monitor organic traffic, average position, bounce rate, scroll depth, time-on-page, and conversion rate on both URL sets. The conversion-rate comparison is the most important signal — if the rewrite converts as well or better than the original despite less traffic, the MRD policy is economically net-neutral and compliance wins outright.
3. **Week 4 decision gate:**
   - If rewrites hold ≥85% of original organic traffic AND convert ≥ original: 301 the originals to the rewrites.
   - If rewrites hold <85% of original organic traffic but convert ≥ original: keep both live, consider merging best elements of rewrite into original for a "hybrid" that preserves more word count / citation count while keeping MRD compliance where it matters most. The rewrite's Allegretti framing, DS-01 mention density, and MRD disclaimer system can be grafted into the original's structure.
   - If rewrites underperform on both traffic and conversion: review whether the MRD rewrite is losing something load-bearing that can be added back without violating MRD.
4. **Week 8:** final decision on canonical URL and 301 strategy.

### Structural concerns Kate/Kateri should weigh before shipping

1. **"Best probiotic for men/women" is partially an un-MRD-supported query shape.** The phrase itself implies gender-specific product differentiation. MRD is gender-neutral on DS-01 efficacy. Neither rewrite fully escapes this tension — it's baked into the primary keyword. Option: retire the SEO asset entirely and redirect to `/cultured/what-probiotics-should-you-take-guide/`. Cost: lose the organic traffic. Benefit: cleaner brand message, no gender-framing compromise.

2. **Citation density is conservative in current drafts.** Both rewrites sit at 7–8 citations; MRD permits adding general-topic public literature (e.g., ISAPP consensus definitions, topic-context papers) to thicken this without violating MRD. Recommend a dedicated citation-pass to bring counts to ~10–12 per article before publish. This is an easy SEO lift.

3. **Internal linking density is conservative in current drafts.** 6 internal links per article is light vs. originals' 25+. MRD-compatible internal link targets exist (bloating guide, probiotics 101, CFU vs AFU, how probiotics regulate your poop, antibiotic recovery, etc.). Recommend a link-pass to bring internal linking to 15–20 per article before publish.

4. **Vaginal microbiome content for women.** The rewrite excludes this entirely and points readers to a separate dedicated article. If VS-01 MRD is finalized, a companion piece should launch at the same time — otherwise readers searching "probiotic for women vaginal health" land on a page that redirects them elsewhere without a satisfying endpoint.

5. **Dirk Gevers quote sourcing.** Quotes in rewrites are MRD-safe paraphrases; real attributed quotes would strengthen the E-E-A-T signal further. Kateri or Angela should decide whether to source actual quotes from interviews or use rewrite-safe paraphrases with Dirk Gevers's blessing.

6. **Comparison-to-competitor surface area.** Neither rewrite explicitly compares DS-01® to unnamed competitors. MRD permits the "DS-01® contains 24x probiotic strains than a leading probiotic" claim. Adding this to both rewrites would strengthen conversion surface area. Currently omitted; recommend Kateri weigh adding.

### Bottom line

These rewrites are genuinely shippable under MRD-only policy, and the conversion-funnel analysis suggests they may outperform the originals on that axis even if they lose ground on organic traffic. The main SEO risk is a 10–25% traffic decay during re-crawl and long-tail cluster reshaping. That's meaningful but manageable if the A/B framework above is followed.

The bigger business question isn't "can these rewrites ship" — they can — it's "does Seed want to own 'best probiotic for women/men' SEO under a policy that explicitly makes those queries harder to answer satisfyingly?" That's a Kate + Kateri + Sydney call, not a drafting call.
